# Chapter 58

There was no verdict that day; the decision would be made after considering all opinions.

But the outcome seemed obvious: Kimberly was taken back first. When she stood, her gaze was as cold and sharp as a blade.

People gradually dispersed. Wade remained seated, motionless. CoCo wisely kept silent, puffing her cheeks in boredom as she looked around the room.

Finally, Wade said, “I’ll go see her.”

The second visit was noticeably tense. The guards at the door had increased. They didn’t follow closely but forbade closing the door. Every move was under their watchful eyes.

Kimberly’s mood was clearly low. Seeing the surviving witnesses from the reserve hit her hard. She spoke of a woman: “Her name is Asina. She was the first to enter the reserve, only 16 then, and she cried nonstop. I comforted her for a long time, then taught her how to bandage wounds. She helped me — did you hear her admit her identity? She’s a nurse now.”

She still cared about that.

Wade interrupted, “You never mentioned Jeremy and Serge coming to see you later.”

Kimberly looked at him for a moment, then suddenly smiled. “Wade, before I met you, I lived 27 years. Now, after less than a month with you... I only told you about my past for one night. There are so many things I haven’t said — even a year wouldn’t be enough.”

Wade gave a wry smile and nodded, “Fair enough.”

Kimberly said, “The trial’s outcome was predictable. Jeremy’s smart. Someone with secrets always worries about being exposed and tries to cover up with stories. He knows the truth. He must have dissected the whole process, analyzing every detail, so if anything goes wrong, he has a perfect excuse ready.”

“He’s right. Unless I keep hiding forever, no matter where I sue — Caron or the UN tribunal — I won’t win. No one will believe me.”

Wade said, “I believe you.”

Kimberly reached out, lightly brushing his half-curled hand. “You believe me because you like me. Sometimes, you’re not defending me but desperately defending that feeling. If it were someone else, you’d say: ‘Who can’t make up stories? We need evidence.’” She retracted her hand.

“At the time, Jeremy arranged everything flawlessly. Only three people in this world know the truth. Two are dead. I don’t care what the court thinks, or the world. Even if they sentence me to death, I don’t want you to be disappointed. Everything I said about the reserve is true.”

Wade tried to grasp every possibility. “There must be evidence. Jeremy contacted the Hukas; maybe the others...”

No, that only proves Jeremy was an accomplice. Others can claim he was just following orders, and Kimberly was the mastermind.

His mind raced. “That night at the forest edge, didn’t Jeremy threaten you? The Huka soldiers present can testify. If I find one of them...”

Kimberly whispered, “When the Kwatis Liberation Front retook the city, the remaining Huka soldiers either fled or fought to the death. The few stationed by the river were wiped out. Do you think I haven’t carefully analyzed every possible evidence source over all these years?”

Wade asked bluntly, “Did you kill Jeremy?”

Kimberly answered, “Who would take such a risk if not pushed to the edge? So yes, I have blood on my hands. If I’m to pay with my life, it wouldn’t be unjust.”

---

Back in the room, CoCo was on the phone with Reindeer. Seeing Wade enter, she handed over the satellite phone. “Want to say something?”

Wade felt drained. “Put it on speaker. I’ll listen.”

Lying on the bed, which was hard, he suddenly wished for a soft mattress—soft enough to cocoon his whole body.

CoCo pressed the speaker button.

Reindeer’s voice came through: “I checked. Remember when I told you the safe was wide open when Jeremy died? No one knows what was lost, but police looked into his account records. He withdrew $500,000 before — probably that money went missing.”

“Also, Ms. Lee’s style suddenly changed three years ago.”

Three years ago—so many events happened then: her return to Caron, Jeremy’s murder, the rise of the Hand of God...

Wade vaguely sensed an unseen, crucial thread connecting many things, all in that year.

“Check the exact dates. I want the sequence — who came first, who followed.”

CoCo said, “Does it matter?”

Wade said, “Imagine I threw your shark’s mouth toy out the door, then you ran out to get it — what do you think would happen?”

CoCo tensed, instinctively blocking the shark toy hanging by the bed. “You’d be unreasonable, and I’d be mad!”

Wade: “What if you ran out first, and then I threw it out — what happened then?”

CoCo’s eyes spun. “Maybe I hit you first, then ran out, and you angrily threw the shark toy at me. Or maybe I asked you to throw it out. Depends on the situation.”

Wade: “Exactly. The order matters.”

CoCo realized and fell silent.

Reindeer sighed, “Wade, CoCo told me everything about the trial. It’s hopeless, and you’re still not giving up?”

Wade smiled. “Still learning idioms?”

“Yes.” Reindeer perked up at the mention of idioms. “I like ones like ‘three or two,’ ‘up and down,’ ‘seven or eight.’ The rest are hard.”

Wade: “Look further, there’s one called ‘a ray of hope in desperation.’”

He was at the edge of despair, missing only those two words.

Suddenly, Wade sat up.

Ennu!

Kimberly said Jeremy arranged everything perfectly. Only three people knew the truth. How could Ennu send that anonymous letter exposing the reserve’s secrets and even provide the full list?

---

Scar disagreed with Wade meeting Ennu.

He sneered, “Wade, you can kill me, but if anything happens to Ennu, I can’t take the responsibility. Not just the Hand of God—Ennu is called ‘Caron’s Rising Star’ by many media. So many important matters rely on him. I won’t let him risk a thing. Understand? Absolutely not.”

Wade kept calm. “I just want to talk, not cause trouble.”

Scar shrugged. “I don’t believe you.”

Wade was exasperated. “He has so many bodyguards!”

“No number of bodyguards guarantees safety. What if you ‘talk’ and suddenly attack? What if the guards can’t react in time?”

Wade held out his hands. “Fine. Handcuff or tie me up, have someone hold a gun on me, and escort me in. I’ll talk to him across the table. Is that okay?”

Scar was silent, then said, “I’ll ask Ennu.”

Wade said, “You better. A so-called ‘Rising Star’ can’t even meet a tied-up man with a gun to him—I doubt you’re trustworthy.”

The ‘Rising Star’ did have some guts.

Half an hour later, Wade was taken to Ennu’s room, handcuffed but without a gun pointed.

Ennu lived in a quiet part of the sanatorium, probably the only suite with an outer and inner room. Outside, three bodyguards stood watch—a small number for someone called ‘so many’.

Ennu sat behind his desk, frowning over documents: a tape recorder, letters, photos, diaries, and other confidential files.

Why would a high-ranking official, unrelated to Kimberly, be so interested in this case?

Ennu looked up. “Mr. Wade?”

“Yes.”

“Heard you’re Ms. Lee’s bodyguard, close with her?”

“Yes.”

Ennu smiled. “Young man, you should have some sense of right and wrong. Don’t let emotions cloud your judgment.”

Though middle-aged, he habitually lectured others.

Wade cut to the chase. “What’s your connection to the reserve? Any close relatives inside?”

Ennu shook his head. “None.”

“Then why did you provide the anonymous letter and list?”

Ennu realized Wade thought he was the ‘important person’. “I received it. That’s when I realized how deep the reserve’s problems were. Jeremy was a government favorite.”

“Who gave it to you? Why suspect Jeremy immediately? You admitted he was well-connected. Shouldn’t the first step be to verify the whistleblower?”

Ennu smiled. “Sorry, can’t disclose that. Just know the letter came from someone I deeply respect and trust. So I didn’t need to verify. No matter how powerful Jeremy was, I dared doubt him. The investigation results shocked us all.”

Wade persisted, “Can I meet that person? Only three know the reserve’s truth. If there’s a fourth, maybe meeting them will change things.”

Ennu smiled dismissively, scanning the evidence. “Change?”

He lost interest and signaled the guards to escort Wade out.

At the door, Scar looked at Ennu, who shook his head.

Scar quietly accompanied Wade back to his room. At the door, he said, “The verdict will be announced at 10 a.m. tomorrow.”

---

Though knowing the verdict wouldn’t be favorable or as he hoped, Wade waited anxiously.

Later, Reindeer called with a rough timeline.

In summary: The third anniversary of the April Tragedy, both Jeremy and Kimberly returned to Caron.

Then Jeremy was murdered in France. The Hand of God appeared shortly after, though the order was unclear. The rise of the group took time.

Then Kimberly’s editorial style changed drastically. As Reindeer put it, she went from “bread and milk” to “gunfire” — firing off articles recklessly, unafraid to offend.

What did this sequence mean? Was she just a drowning person struggling, clutching at floating foam?

Wade grew restless, tossing in bed, unable to sleep until the rain’s steady patter lulled him.

His dream was turbulent: a smuggler’s boat tossed in whitecaps. He struggled onto the deck, watching Kimberly’s easel and papers scatter in the storm. Each sheet was numbered, each bearing a Kwatis face, sorrowful.

He shouted, “The waves are too strong! Come to me!”

Kimberly stood still. The boat tilted; she fell and rolled off the side.

Wade lunged and caught her hand just as she fell.

Then he realized it was his left arm.

A current of electricity ran from wrist to elbow. His arm trembled uncontrollably, strength fading, and Kimberly’s hand slipped away...

Wade awoke abruptly.

Outside, rain poured with thunder and lightning, yet he clearly heard an engine over the downpour.

Without hesitation, he jumped out of bed, racing out: faint light flashed at a bend in the mountain road.

His mind exploded. He reached Kimberly’s door. Two guards blocked him. He grabbed one by the neck, headbutted the other, knocking them down, then kicked open the door and turned on the light.

The bed was messy but empty.

On the desk, a gold chain hung half off. A cheap lipstick shell was open, the wax clearly used.

Blood rushed to Wade’s head. Footsteps approached. He turned.

It was Scar, soaked from the rain. “Mr. Wade...”

Before he could finish, Wade, furious like a lion, slammed him to the ground, one hand gripping his throat.

“Where is she?”

Scar gasped, “Moved... relocated.”

“Moved or executed?”

Scar didn’t answer, just laughed. Wade clenched his teeth and punched his face.

Scar spat blood, chuckling, “That’s... why we moved her early. Seems we were right.”

Wade grabbed his collar, pulling him up. “You said the verdict is at 10 a.m. tomorrow!”

Scar stammered, “Yes... but the verdict was decided in court...”

“Call the car back! Is there a car phone? Call it back!”

Scar turned his head, spitting blood. “I don’t have that authority.”

Wade said coldly, “Fine. Your fault. Remember that.”

He left Scar behind.

Scar clutched his throat, struggling to sit up.

CoCo appeared, pulling on clothes. “Where’s Wade? Heard him get up but now he’s gone?”

Scar looked at CoCo, face pale, and hoarsely shouted, “Ennu! Quick! Ennu!”

---

Wade’s eyes were bloodshot but his mind clear.

At the back, he tiptoed to the door, listening.

Three bodyguards were inside—likely one on duty, two resting. He’d observed the room’s layout and their positions.

One against three was tough. He had to act fast. If he didn’t gain the upper hand within five seconds, it would be disastrous.

He gritted his teeth, kicked the door open, fell backward, sliding inside.

Gunfire erupted, bullets flashing from barrels.

Wade seized two guards’ ankles, dragging them down, shouting, “CoCo, shoot!”

The last guard hesitated. Wade charged, kicking open the inner door and rolling inside.

The gunfire stopped. About half a minute later, the lights went out.

The inner door was ajar; a guard cautiously approached.

Wade’s voice came, “Going further? Want him dead?”

---

Reindeer, half-asleep, heard the phone ring again. Eve turned over, grumbling. Reindeer buried his face in the pillow and answered.

After listening, he suddenly jolted upright.

“When now?”

CoCo answered, “He wants the car back. Caron won’t refuse. Should be fine. Ennu’s on the phone, but... next steps are tricky. He’s a high official. We can’t offend him.”

Reindeer said, “He took down three guards?”

CoCo proudly said, “Yeah. Wade was fast—probably under ten seconds. Those three were useless.”

Reindeer shouted into the phone, “Watch those three!”

CoCo snapped to attention.

The local African bodyguard market was chaotic, especially post-war. Political instability caused infighting. Authorities preferred mercenary-backed guard groups, monopolizing territories. A single failure meant losing the turf.

So there was an unspoken rule: if a client is harmed, kill the attackers to offset the loss. If the client was disturbed but safe, killing attackers meant no fault and bonus rewards.

CoCo’s ears buzzed with tension. He suddenly looked up, seeing a montage of scenes:

- Scar, face pale and swollen, sweating nervously.

- Ennu dialing a number.

- Wade standing by the desk, holding his breath.

- One of the three guards suddenly raising a gun.

CoCo shouted, “Wade! Get down!”

He rushed over. Bullets flew, tearing the desk and walls. Papers scattered.

CoCo furiously slammed the attacker’s head to the floor, then glared at the other two.

They froze. CoCo dared not move.

He looked at the desk, voice trembling, “Wade?”

No response or movement.

A trickle of blood seeped from the desk’s edge.

CoCo’s vision blurred. He scrambled forward.

He found Wade lying on the floor, his shoulder wound bleeding heavily. His eyes fixed intently on a document.

It was an anonymous letter exposing the reserve incident, listing those responsible for investigation:

Jeremy, Serge, Kimberly.

Kimberly’s English name was Silvia.

After the English name was her Chinese name, the character “今” written with a deliberate pause, resembling “令.”

# Chapter 59

The car had been parked in the wild for some time.

Rain continuously drummed on the roof, ticking like the watch Kimberly wore in the reserve — its hands moving endlessly.

A car’s headlights appeared in the distance, growing brighter. Kimberly shielded her eyes.

After a while, the door opened with a loud creak.

She looked up to see Ennu, holding an umbrella, standing in knee-high grass. Rainwater streamed off the canopy, glowing eerily in the dark and headlights.

Ennu looked older. The confident, indignant man on TV three years ago had become calm, measured, and composed.

Kimberly waited for him to speak.

He studied her for a long moment, then said, “Ms. Lee?”

“Three years ago, I wasn’t prominent in Caron’s political scene. I was dissatisfied with the government’s handling of war criminals and organized supporters for protests. On the third anniversary of the April Tragedy, I planned a large event but failed. Once, during a speech, police used tear gas, scattering everyone.”

Kimberly listened quietly.

“That night, I was very discouraged watching the news. At midnight, I got a call. The voice was disguised, unrecognizable. Do you know what they said?”

Kimberly smiled. “I guess they asked if I knew about the Jewish Avengers.”

Ennu’s face twitched slightly, then nodded.

“I said I had studied some materials. If the government stayed inactive, I’d want to start such a group in Caron. As long as it’s just and clear conscience. But I was just a broke activist, clueless where to start. They said it was okay.”

“About a month later, they contacted me again and transferred funds through untraceable accounts — the Hand of God’s seed money. Guess how much?”

Kimberly said, “More than money. Besides $500,000 startup funds, they probably gave conditions: be as fair and just as possible, avoid rage and blind retaliation, don’t kill wrongly or let culprits go. Also, don’t trace their origin; just cooperate.”

Ennu was silent for a long time. Nearby, slender grass bent under raindrops, then sprang back.

Finally, he said, “Ms. Lee, you are the founder of the Hand of God.”

Kimberly laughed lightly. “Not really. Your group’s scale isn’t because of me. That $500,000 probably wouldn’t even cover the sanatorium.”

“At the beginning of this month, after three years, you transferred more funds.”

Kimberly nodded. “I heard you shifted focus. Just a token. Anyway... I had no use for the money.”

At last, her eyes lifted slightly. “But how did you find out?”

Ennu said, “Not us — Wade.”

---

Wade pieced together the timeline from Kimberly’s signature. He had no time to deal with CoCo’s threats to dismantle the three bodyguards. Sitting at the bullet-riddled desk, he took paper and pen to explain to Ennu.

“Here: the third anniversary of the April Tragedy. Jeremy, an investor and government guest, returned to Caron. Kimberly also came back, burdened by guilt and troubles. She met Jeremy and revisited old issues.”

“Shortly after, Jeremy died in France. The safe was wide open. Kimberly was a suspect; she was there that night but was cleared for lack of evidence. Now we know she admitted it — she killed Jeremy and took $500,000.”

“Then the Hand of God was founded. Ennu, I heard it started small, so the startup funds shouldn’t be large. As a founder, you know best — was the initial amount $500,000?”

“Then Kimberly’s editorial style changed. Your people say she ‘smelled danger and tried to cover herself step by step.’ But usually, it takes time from founding to fame to awareness. She changed style the day after your group formed. Because it was all planned — she knew her fate and acted without restraint.”

“In the exposé letter, she listed those responsible for the reserve, placing herself last. She was waiting for the others to be dealt with before ending it all.”

“Also, Kimberly was the only one who registered refugees. If the original list is in the national archives, and someone else recites 292 names, it must be her...”

---

Kimberly listened silently, then asked, “Got any cigarettes?”

Ennu didn’t smoke but gestured for an assistant to bring some. Kimberly held the pack in the car light: premium Zimbabwean golden tobacco.

She lit one. The air filled with a faint sweet aroma.

She inhaled and exhaled, the smoke blurring her vision, blurring the past.

She said, “I’m a coward. Threatened by Jeremy, I stayed silent for three years. What made me decide was meeting Jeremy three years ago in Caron.”

That time, threats were inevitable. Jeremy whispered a secret in her ear.

He said, “Remember your colleague who went to find people? He told us the reserve’s location. He said, besides him, only a young, inexperienced girl remained. We thought it’d be easier with just her.”

He laughed loudly — the sound still ringing.

...

Kimberly looked at Ennu. “It’s raining hard. Want to come inside?”

Ennu shook his head, standing firm.

“On the way back, I suddenly understood.”

“This isn’t just my fate or my fight. Jeremy arranged everything perfectly. If I don’t stand up, no one will know the truth. What about those lives? My colleagues? Their bones mixed among 200,000 Kwatis, impossible to separate. Yet the people who hurt them are hailed as heroes.”

Ennu was silent. Rain soaked his shoes.

Kimberly watched the endless rain dripping off the umbrella.

She always dreamed of a hero in armor to rescue her in danger.

But then she realized there was no such person.

The armor was always there — she had to wear it herself.

What she had to give up was just a life and a miserable existence.

“Once I accepted that, it didn’t matter. I had to fight Jeremy and the others. But I didn’t want him to die silently and be remembered as a hero. I want everything exposed, Caron involved, black and white clear!”

“That night, Caron’s channels replayed news. I watched your face, heard your speech, saw you driven away by police. I realized maybe we could cooperate without meeting.”

---

She called Jeremy. He asked, “How much do you want?”

She said, “$500,000.”

He agreed but with conditions. A man like him wouldn’t let money slip away.

“Kim, have you thought about joining forces? You have medals, I have medals. Together, we’d be a strong brand — enough to make money in Caron for ten more years.”

Kimberly said, “Okay.”

At that moment, she was flipping through a page about tetrodotoxin (TTX) from pufferfish.

She liked that poison.

Though victims couldn’t speak or move, their minds stayed clear until death.

Things went as she wished: she stood over the paralyzed but conscious Jeremy, condemning him, telling him the poison was fatal—a rare chance to feel death.

Then she played music and quietly unlocked the safe.

The second was Serge, hiding well. She couldn’t find him but knew he’d come for her and how to argue.

Half a year later, Serge caught her in a dark alley. Kimberly nearly strangled, but smiled and said, “Not me. I know who did it. We can’t escape. If you kill me, you lose your scapegoat.”

Serge doubted but let go.

Kimberly coughed, “Check for a Caron avenger group. That’s why I met Jeremy that night—didn’t expect he’d act first. Check, and you’ll see I’m telling the truth...”

Serge fled, wishing to hide underground, but someone would find him.

She patiently waited for the Hand of God’s rise, then sent the exposé letter. When writing the names, she felt the most important part should be handwritten.

Pursuit isn’t a legend—it takes longer than imagined. When Serge’s hand appeared, Helsinki was still cold with winter winds and snow.

A part-time worker screamed to call police. Kimberly smiled faintly, looking at her blurry reflection in the window.

---

Ennu whispered, “Ms. Lee, you could have erased your name from the letter.”

Kimberly smiled. “No use. Even if erased, Serge would bite me to clear himself. Besides, I don’t hide what role I played in the reserve. I don’t even know myself after six years.”

She wanted a trial and many eyes. The result didn’t matter. She wanted to expose the past—to be seen, scolded, or blamed. Secrets grow like bones inside, tearing the liver and intestines.

“But if I die, I’m a little unwilling. That’s why I personally ended Jeremy’s life — to resolve myself. Even if I lose my life, I’m not truly innocent. You might not know, though evidence was lacking, French police never fully cleared me. If I don’t fall into your hands, I’ll fall into theirs.”

Ennu smiled bitterly. “I truly can’t imagine... Ms. Lee, did you miss anything?”

Kimberly’s smile faded.

Softly, “Yes.”

She hadn’t foreseen the last leg, the final accident.

Wade would probably be very disappointed.

---

Back at the sanatorium, Wade was gone. Only CoCo sat on the bed, face dark. Shark’s mouth toy lay nearby, teeth bared, waiting to stir trouble.

Her first words on seeing Kimberly: “Wade left. He dumped you. Understand?”

Kimberly said, “Oh.”

She sat on Wade’s bed.

He must have left in a hurry; the blanket was tossed aside, pillow slightly dented. The scent lingered. Without CoCo, she might have crawled under the covers and slept soundly, ignoring the mess.

CoCo was unhappy with her reaction. “I’m telling the truth. Can you not react like that?”

Kimberly asked, “What reaction do you want?”

CoCo was speechless, then asked, “Is your business finished?”

Kimberly shook her head. “I’m going back to Caron with Ennu. Some details need confirmation. The final result depends on higher-ups.”

CoCo said, “At least you won’t die.”

Kimberly changed topic, “Is he angry?”

CoCo hesitated, unsure how to answer.

Wade was relieved after confirming Kimberly’s safety. “He almost died. He was desperate, trying everything. Now that he knows the truth, he’s fighting to save what you planned to abandon. How would you feel?”

Kimberly was silent.

“Ms. Lee, have you ever thought about living with Wade?”

Kimberly smiled. “Yes.”

“If there was evidence, who wouldn’t want that? But back then, I worked so hard and saw no way out. I decided to gamble.”

Founding the Hand of God and writing the exposé were background steps, not the focus. She never thought these moves would become new evidence after her fate was sealed.

Ennu was moved. “Lucky. Because you wrote the letter, I never publicized it. I only compared it with evidence at night. Without that accident...”

If not for the accident, the files scattered, Wade seeing the handwriting...

Ennu said, “Once the jury learns this inside story, things will change, especially since it wasn’t you who spoke but others who discovered it.”

“Maybe before, only Wade believed you, but now more will. As the Hand of God’s leader, I want to help. I owe my position and the group’s scale to your call three years ago.”

Kimberly looked at CoCo. “I know you’re mad I didn’t tell him the truth. But if you were me, silent for six years, planning for three, all orderly, then suddenly losing control and falling in love — how do you explain that? How do you close that chapter?”

“Wade calls me ‘little girl.’ I’m not a little girl. Just because you gave me a good man doesn’t solve everything.”

“Before Wade, I had a fiancé, Ming Jiang. After killing Jeremy, I ended it. I knew my path was set and didn’t want to drag anyone down.”

“Gave up life, gave up fiancé, I thought nothing could shake me. When I met Wade, he was a Saudi-hired bodyguard with no feelings for me, just here for a short trip. I never imagined I’d fall for him...”

They’d known each other less than a month.

A knock came.

They looked up to see Scar, half his face swollen, sunglasses crooked. “Ms. Lee, the car’s ready. Ennu’s waiting.”

Kimberly stood. Before leaving, she told CoCo, “You never liked me much. Wade left, but you stayed because he told you to.”

“Tell him this: I respect all his decisions. I don’t regret my past plans. Whether he loves me or not, whoever he loves next, I still love him. My love might not be perfect or pure, but...”

She smiled softly, “Never mind.”

She slipped out the door.

Scar looked at CoCo.

CoCo suddenly angry, “Is she even human? Every time I try to dump my wife, she cries and clings to me...”

He chased after her, shouting, “Hey! Hey! Ms. Lee!”

Kimberly stopped and turned.

Rain fell thickly. CoCo wiped rain from his forehead.

“You know where Wade went. After your business is done, go find him.”

Kimberly said, “Aren’t we already broken up?”

CoCo sulked but couldn’t admit he made it up. “Then you have to chase him.”

“I know Wade. He’s done so much for you, risking his life. When he learned the truth, his first question to Ennu was if you were safe. Do you get it? After all this, if you don’t chase or save him, how hard would that be for him?”

Kimberly smiled. Rain cooled her face, but her eyes burned with tears.

“I’m not for you. I still don’t like you. I’m for Wade. You know he was smuggled to Europe as a child, sold off. He’s not passionate or stable, calls himself a broken ship drifting ashore. I’m surprised he cares so much — you’re not good, but it might be years before he finds someone else. So... make do.”

She choked on a laugh.

“If you feel guilty, that’s good. You’ll treat him better. So chase him. No matter how he annoys, pushes, or scolds you, don’t leave. He won’t mind. Wade’s a good man; just be honest and stop making new groups...”

CoCo suddenly warned, “Wait, you only made the Hand of God, right? No others?”

Kimberly got in the car.

The door closed. CoCo anxiously paced the car, “You haven’t answered yet — will you chase him? And how many did you make...?”

The car started. He moved aside. Suddenly, the window rolled down and a paper airplane flew out.

It drifted, wings damp from rain, slowly falling to the ground.

CoCo stared at it.

So childish—grown up and still playing with paper planes. How will you take care of Wade?

Besides, it didn’t fly as far as his folding.

# Chapter 60 Epilogue

The plane landed in Helsinki at night.

The last leg was turbulent, shaking the plane. Passengers gasped and prayed. When wheels touched down, everyone sighed with relief.

Wade slept heavily, no dreams, feeling like on a boat tossed endlessly by waves, pushed nowhere.

Flight attendants woke him. Time to disembark.

The terminal bustled. A colorful billboard showed smiling Finnish students with the words:

“Put a hat on spring! Welcome to Helsinki Hat Festival!”

A large screen displayed the calendar.

April 30th, the Hat Festival, a Finnish spring celebration.

April was nearly over.

Wade, dressed for summer, stepped outside and shivered. He hurried back to buy a jacket, wrapped up, then went out again.

He chuckled at himself — twice returning to Helsinki in April, dressed oddly: once in dirty animal skins, once too lightly.

At his apartment building, he went as usual to Erinn’s bar. Before entering, he looked up at the sign: “We care about the world.”

He stared a long time. When he said that, he wasn’t very concerned about current events, just annoyed Erinn didn’t know China. Erinn chose it as a catchy name.

— “Wade! I can put the news on in the bar! No bar in Helsinki has done that! How fresh!”

She talked about it often, but rarely acted.

Guests politely asked him to move.

Inside, the bar was lively — smoke, drinks, everything. The first thing he saw was Cleopatra, heavily made up, clutching a Russian old-timer’s neck, laughing wildly.

The bar counter was empty. The jellyfish tank bubbled, glowing dark green. Two old jellyfish were cared for but never lively.

Beside the tank was a peace lily, growing well, new leaves pale green, two white spathes slightly curled, edges close but not touching, as if destined to lean on each other.

Wade smiled, about to approach—

“David’s coming!”

Wade laughed, glancing sideways at Erinn, who came bounding over with an empty tray. He shifted his injured shoulder, making room for her.

She dropped the tray and grabbed his shoulder. “Wade! I think about you every day.”

Just polite words, he thought, but this time she clung longer.

He scanned the bar. “Don’t tell me it’s for show?”

Erinn blushed.

She pulled him. “Look...”

A small brown-haired girl entered the bar, chin pointed, lively big eyes.

Erinn whispered, “That’s Asha.”

Wade commented, “Like the Bulgarian girl last time. You always like petite girls. Why not tall, curvy, long-legged?”

Erinn scoffed, “Pfft, you’re the one who likes them.”

Wade kindly pulled her close. “Just started dating? Trying to make her jealous? I’ll help, but why involve me? You a bisexual?”

Erinn twisted him, but unlike Kimberly, he couldn’t find any soft spots.

Wade patted her head. “No more teasing. I’ll take back my flower. Same as always, then bed.”

He strode to the bar. Erinn hesitated, then followed, “Hey...”

At that moment, Asha snatched the flower pot from his hand.

What’s that about? Didn’t even leave him a bit of soil? How could he trust her with his wife?

Erinn pulled him aside, stammering, “Um... Wade, can the flower be mine?”

Wade realized her meaning: she wanted to keep the flower, leaving none for him. How would she take care of Wade in the future?

Erinn said, “I meant to tell you on the phone, but your signal was bad. This flower really brings luck... but I can’t keep it alive. Almost killed it in less than a week.”

“I thought that’s no good. You said if the flower’s gone, you’re gone. I can’t let you die. So I took the flower and looked for someone who knows...”

On the street, people passed by. Asha didn’t see Erinn but saw the flower, shouting, “You just carry it out like that? It can’t freeze!”

She took off her jacket and carefully wrapped the flower’s windward side.

Wade glanced at her sideways. “So it’s serious? How far along?”

Erinn stammered, “Had coffee a few times. Now she helps at the bar after work, held hands... We’ve known each other less than a month. I don’t want to go too fast. What do you think?”

Wade stayed silent. On ‘fast,’ he had no say.

He paused. “So you just stole my flower?”

Erinn argued, “How is it yours? You just passed it on. Did you ever water it, loosen soil, remove bugs? You did nothing. If the flower protects anyone, it’s not you.”

Wade realized Erinn was a born negotiator. She finished then smiled pleadingly, “Wade, give it to me. Asha and I both like it. For loving you so long...”

Using love as leverage, having loved him so long but never made the bed, and now taking his flower.

Wade gritted his teeth but had to admit she was right.

He said, “...Fine.”

---

He slept deeply, restless, dreaming vividly.

He chased someone holding a magical watch that could turn back time. After a long run, he caught him, forcing him to rewind six years.

The man was slow, so Wade grabbed the watch and spun it hard.

Too hard. Time spun wildly. When he recovered, he stood on a country road in autumn.

Grass was tall and yellowing. Leaves drifted down.

Kimberly walked slowly ahead.

She was only four or five, wearing a floral dress, with two pigtails sticking up stubbornly.

She carried a small backpack, walking leisurely, examining grass, picking stones, comparing heights with trees — the kind of child who’d get scolded by a mother for dawdling.

Wade followed, amused.

She sensed him, turned sharply, and said, “Who are you?”

Wade crouched, unsure what to say. After a long pause, he said, “You’ll know me later. You’ll get on my ship...”

Kimberly shouted, “Go away! Don’t get on bad people’s cars or boats!”

She turned and ran, short legs kicking fast, backpack bouncing. She looked back nervously, stumbled, then quickly got up and ran further.

Wade realized for the first time how fast she could run...

He awoke with a smile lingering, faint city sounds drifting through the window.

He lay a moment, then remembered it was Hat Festival day.

Thousands gathered at the southern harbor square by the Havis Amanda statue.

In the early 1900s, students partying near the statue saw the lonely goddess and, fearing she was cold, draped a tablecloth and put a white hat on her.

She was no longer aloof; the hat and cloth made her playful.

Since then, every year on that day, people put hats on Amanda, making it a tradition.

Wade had experienced it once. The party started in the afternoon, half the city gathered, all wearing white hats, playing music, popping champagne, hugging, celebrating spring’s arrival till dawn.

The celebration had begun.

He got up and checked his phone: a message from Reindeer.

— Tomorrow at 9 p.m., the bar.

He thought for a while, realizing “tomorrow” meant tonight.

---

Due to the festival, the bar was less crowded. Even Cleopatra hadn’t come to work. Erinn and Asha whispered nonstop.

Reindeer arrived on time, heading straight to Wade’s table—the first meeting since the sauna.

Likely bursting with words like a Nubian sandstorm, Wade braced himself against exaggeration and mockery.

“Don’t call me Fir. Don’t hug me right away. Sit down. If you laugh at me for loving a client, get lost.”

His words hit hard. Reindeer froze, displeased, then sat.

He set his bag on the table. “The Saudis wired your pay. They know you like cash but not big bills — exchanged.”

Wade opened the bag, glanced, then remembered. “Did you donate for the circumcision?”

Reindeer said, “Really?”

Wade gave him a sideways look. “A bit painful, but I can’t take back my word.”

Reindeer was thrilled. “Wade! You actually care about money? This month wasn’t wasted! Donated half, kept half. You’re not booking a Lapland charter again, right?”

Wade was silent, then asked, “Is the rest enough to buy my apartment?”

Reindeer couldn’t believe it. “You want to buy a house?”

Wade shrugged, “Need a place to settle.”

He waved for Erinn to bring two dark beers.

Reindeer thought for a moment, then, seeing Wade calm, assumed he wouldn’t avoid the topic.

“There’s something you might want to know. Remember you asked me to check Jeremy’s case?”

Wade: “What about it?”

“I spent some money greasing palms in the police. They said the case isn’t closed but stalled. So they reopened and re-examined suspects, including Ms. Lee.”

“And?”

“On the way here, they updated me. Yesterday, French police received a letter from Caron’s Hand of God, claiming responsibility for Jeremy’s murder three years ago.”

Wade was stunned.

Reindeer clicked his tongue. “Surprised? They closed the case the same day they got the letter. Heard they celebrated with supper.”

Wade murmured, “Surprised...”

He laughed lightly.

Was this a ray of hope in despair? All along, the Hand of God wanted Kimberly dead. At the end, they cleared her final obstacle.

He said, “Kimberly chose well. Ennu is someone who gets things done.”

Reindeer sneered. “Of course she chose right. You’re the right choice too — just blind in the reserve...”

Wade’s face darkened. “She had no choice there.”

Reindeer couldn’t hold back. “Still defending her? You almost died. If the sniper was better, if I hadn’t told CoCo to watch those guards, where’d you be? Could you still be Fir? Burned to ash?”

Wade smiled, then said, “After leaving Tiger Shark’s ship, I planned the route. I asked her, ‘If I bring you into danger, would you blame me?’”

“She said, ‘Following you isn’t a joke. It’s my choice. If really dangerous, I accept the risk. Half is my responsibility. Blaming only you is boring.’”

Reindeer was confused. “What do you mean?”

Wade asked, “Do you know why I’m so desperate to help her?”

“Because you’re lovestruck.”

Wade laughed, clinking his beer with Reindeer, then took a big sip and set it down.

“I like her, of course. Another reason is, even after all this time, though close, she never said ‘Please stay,’ ‘Protect me,’ or ‘Don’t leave me.’”

“She’s so dangerous and already mine. Why no demands? You know, I bought her... two veils. No, the veil was free. Just a cheap local lipstick, probably less than half a euro. Buying a drink for a pretty girl at the bar costs more.”

“When you like a girl, you either spend money or love her fiercely. She wants none of it. What do you do?”

“I was hired by Saudis for the first half. She said she didn’t want me for the second half. I forced her to write an IOU. It was my decision.”

“Before I met her, I knew she got a severed hand; when I signed the contract, I knew someone broke into the white coat’s room; Tiger Shark’s ship was never boarded; the speedboat blew up in international waters — I knew what I faced. Plainly, I accepted the risk.”

“I fought to shield her from all dangers — whether Hand of God was hers, Jeremy’s, or CoCo’s. Even if the knife was in her hand, I wouldn’t watch her commit suicide. I’d step in.”

Reindeer was lost. “So you still left her?”

Wade sneered, “Why not? Can’t a man have a temper? She suffered for six years. I have no right to blame her, even feel sorry for her. But feelings aside, I’m uncomfortable. I don’t want to just smile it off. I want attitude, or I won’t be respected.”

Reindeer was speechless, finally said, “Wade, my wife and I fought once. Eve left home, I chased her... I never heard of a man leaving and expecting the woman to chase.”

“What if she doesn’t come? That Ms. Lee seems proud.”

“She’s been gone days. Wade, maybe you’ll have to turn back and chase her. Where’s your face? But no worries, you have thick skin. You said you’d never get involved beyond money with clients...”

Wade clenched his teeth, ready to throw beer at the wall when the clock struck ten.

News time.

---

Regulars knew the rule: at Erinn’s bar, news time was a truce. No matter what, everyone stopped and focused.

Tonight’s breaking news arrived suddenly.

The announcer’s voice trembled with excitement:

“Today, the month-long standoff over the Saudi oil tanker Sirius reached a breakthrough. At 3 p.m., as demanded by pirates, Saudi forces dropped a $3 million ransom package by seaplane over the designated waters...”

Reindeer’s eyes lit up. “Wade! It’s the Sirius!”

He wanted to shout so the whole bar knew he was involved and had met the white coat.

Wade was watching intently.

The screen showed the seaplane dropping the package, orange parachute opening. Below, pirate speedboats circled swiftly, cutting white waves.

Each pirate wore masks or wrapped shirts over their heads. The image was shaky; no way to tell Tiger Shark from Shadi, who liked chewing Arabic tea.

The bar was focused. Wade quietly stood and slipped out.

---

Outside the apartment, the street was quiet. The festival’s noise was elsewhere.

Leaning against the wall, Wade lit a cigarette, flicked ash onto the shiny wet pavement.

Ten days ago, he was on the ship, with the Red Sea sandstorm raging. The boat was lively: Tiger Shark was irritable but cautious, Shadi unhurried, little pirates bullying whenever possible.

Now they were separated by a TV screen, thousands of miles away.

The pirates were splitting the money — surely noisy and chaotic, shouting “Money! Money! Money!”

From the southern harbor came distant cheers, muffled by distance and uneven buildings.

So lively.

In life, many crowds accompany you in noise, but few share your loneliness.

A tingling sensation crept from Wade’s left wrist toward his elbow.

Footsteps echoed on the quiet street.

Wade didn’t move, smoke swirling before his eyes.

He didn’t look up, but saw a long shadow stretch toward him, merging.

Then, a woman in brown leather ankle boots stood before him.

Wade smiled, flicked the cigarette, and wrapped one arm around her waist, pulling her close.

He heard her say, “Wade...”

He said, “Shh... let me finish this smoke.”

---

The street was silent.

The cigarette was half gone.

The cold wall chilled his back, but his embrace was warm, warming his heart.

He liked sitting high, listening to city sounds, watching people like ants below.

Reindeer and CoCo had joined him on the rooftop, asking what he saw.

He said, “People, of course. People give off scents and breath, talk, fight, share emotions, scream, live and die. All that needs energy.”

CoCo said he was talking nonsense.

Pressed, he added, “You see stories — happening, brewing, fading.”

Actually, he was just talking nonsense.

He just liked watching people, especially those not rushing.

There were couples, sweet or bickering.

A family: father soothing the daughter, son throwing tantrums, mother exasperated.

Wade always watched and smiled for a long time.

He thought none of that would happen to him.

He thought he was just a boat brushing past crowds, never staying, never pausing, just drifting.

Until the deck rotted and he rusted away on an unknown shore.

He looked down and asked her, “Have you decided? Once you board my ship, you can't get off.”
