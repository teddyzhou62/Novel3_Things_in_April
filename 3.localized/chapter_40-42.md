# Chapter 40

The third day of negotiations.

Kimberly felt it was time to change clothes. She rummaged through her luggage bag first thing in the morning, picking up one piece with her left hand only to put it down with her right. No matter how many times she searched, it was always the same few items.

Even the most skillful cook can’t make a meal without ingredients.

She picked up the white T-shirt and jeans that had been soaked in seawater, washed, dried, and had accompanied her through the first two rounds of negotiations.

They were already wrinkled and worn.

She said, “If the Tiger Shark ever writes a memoir about the Sirius negotiations, do you think he’ll write: ‘That female negotiator didn’t change clothes for days and even wore slippers...’?”

Wade replied, “Negotiating the ransom down from 20 million to 3 million—that’s a greater achievement than if you’d worn five evening gowns in a row to negotiate with Tiger Shark.”

Kimberly smiled, probably realizing there was no better solution. She grabbed her clothes and headed to the shower curtain to change.

“Wait,” Wade said.

He pulled his denim shirt from the bag. “Wear this.”

Kimberly glanced at him. “It’s obviously a man’s shirt. I can wear it as pajamas, but going to negotiate in this? Won’t people laugh?”

Wade took the clothes from her hand and stuffed the shirt into her arms. “Trust me. Wear this. I’ve got a plan.”

Kimberly stared at him for a moment, half believing him, and accepted.

When she came out, she wore the denim shirt buttoned up all the way to the collar, looking as if she’d stuffed her head inside a sack.

Wade sat on the bed, staring at her for a long time. “Don’t you have any sense of style? Come here.”

“If you add black-rimmed glasses, you’ll look like the most annoying female headmistress in a Hong Kong film.”

Kimberly walked over with a scowl. “You have one!”

Wade smiled. “I have the simplest taste a man can have—I only know how you should dress to suit me.”

He pulled her close and started unbuttoning the shirt.

After undoing two buttons, he frowned, seemingly dissatisfied, and unbuttoned a third, pulling the collar askew to the side. His eyes reflected a scene that tightened one’s throat: a messy shirt revealing curves half-hidden, half-exposed.

Kimberly looked down at herself. “You want me to wear this in front of Tiger Shark?”

Wade’s expression changed. “What are you thinking? Only one button undone in front of Tiger Shark, got it?”

“Then why did you undo so many?”

Kimberly angrily reached to twist his mouth, but Wade grinned mischievously, tilting his head away, pulling her body toward him with his arm, and gently kissed the slight rise that peeked from her shirt. The wet warmth and heat made Kimberly gasp sharply. Struggling, she scolded, “Don’t mess around... I still have to... negotiate...”

Her words broke into hoarse syllables, and her body softened, unable to resist.

After a while, Wade let go, sliding his hand inside her shirt to adjust the shoulder strap that had slipped due to her struggling, slowly lifting it back onto her shoulder. “See? In front of people with ulterior motives, don’t undo three buttons, or the consequences are unpredictable.”

Kimberly gritted her teeth. “Get lost! I don’t need your help with my clothes.”

Wade laughed heartily, coaxing her, “No, I swear I’ll behave from now on.”

He leaned over, took a knife from the luggage, bit open the sheath, and cut a horizontal slit along the edge of her shirt’s hem. Then he grabbed the corner and tore around the side in one go.

The tearing left many white threads and fabric scraps at the shirt’s hem. Kimberly guessed, “You’re going to tie a belt?”

Though it would accentuate the waist, wrapping a strip of fabric around her waist looked a bit silly.

Wade said nothing, cut the strip in two, and began cinching the excess fabric at her waist, piercing a hole with the knife.

Kimberly understood and pulled a strip from him, threading it through the hole, pinching folds to tie it, then tucked the knot inside the shirt.

She tied one side, Wade had done the other.

A very masculine method: knife, thread, and tie. Rough, straightforward, seemingly functional but not perfect upon close inspection—yet somehow, it radiated an indescribable sexiness.

Kimberly smiled. She liked it.

More than all the carefully sewn evening gowns adorned with dazzling rhinestones and elaborate lace she’d ever worn.

Wade pinched her chin. “Don’t provoke Tiger Shark anymore. He has a terrible temper.”

Kimberly was unfazed. “You have to be careful, but if he needs something from me, he’ll become more cautious in front of me. Yesterday I gave him a choice. If it were you, which would you choose?”

“Does that even need asking? Anyone would want to live safely to old age. But...”

Kimberly raised an eyebrow. “But what?”

“But giving you ransom money, letting you quit piracy, offering government amnesty, diplomatic status, and a peaceful future...”

“That’s not just an opportunity, nor something any one person can simply make happen. It’s tempting, but almost intangible.”

Tiger Shark wasn’t a fool. Who would believe you, little girl?

That day’s negotiations began at breakfast.

They ate canned food—tuna and baked beans—with rare coffee, instant sachets loaded with sugar. The first sip was full of half-melted sugar granules on the tongue.

Kimberly’s prediction was right. Tiger Shark was troubled but even more restrained and cautious than the day before.

He had little appetite, hesitated several times before finally finding an opening, and said in a casual tone, “Kimberly, what you said yesterday—was it a joke?”

Kimberly kept her head down, scooping the last beans from the can with her spoon. “I crossed continents and almost got blown up—just to joke with you? Do I like telling jokes that much?”

Tiger Shark cleared his throat, trying not to sound too concerned. “Kimberly, I’ve hijacked many ships and killed... quite a few people. The government wants to catch me but can’t. How could they just let me go?”

He gave a nervous chuckle and licked his lips, but a flicker of hope shone in his eyes.

Wade saw it clearly, wanting to laugh but feeling a bit sad.

Pirates are human too. Forced to the edge at gunpoint and on the sea, often without choices. When you suddenly show them a path, even if they pretend not to care, they’ll watch it closely, sniff it out, and test the ground to see if it’s solid.

After finishing her meal, Kimberly grabbed a tissue to wipe her mouth and pushed the empty can aside. “How many people have you killed? Two hundred?”

Tiger Shark was startled. “No, definitely not that many.”

He hated how reckless he was when killing—thinking that since he’d probably die at sea anyway, killing one more person meant one more companion in death.

Kimberly said, “Let me tell you a story. Do you know about World War II and the Nazis?”

Tiger Shark nodded.

“Good. In the late war, the German army was retreating and pinned its hopes on new weapons. The lead scientist was Wernher Braun, a Waffen-SS major. Due to severe labor shortages, he used slave workers from concentration camps. About 20,000 laborers died in weapons development.”

“Once the weapons were ready, they were mainly used against the British, killing several thousand.”

“Then, the Allies invaded Germany. Braun secretly contacted the Americans and made a deal to exchange his technology for safe passage from war crime trials.”

“He succeeded, was taken safely to the U.S., hid his dark past, and worked for the Americans. Years later, he helped with the Apollo moon landing.”

“He won many honors, received the U.S. National Science Medal, was called the father of modern spaceflight, and died peacefully in a hospital.”

Tiger Shark listened impatiently and was even a bit angry when Kimberly finished.

“How is that the same? He was a scientist, sending people to the moon! A man of knowledge! What am I? I can’t even build a car!”

Kimberly smiled and leaned close, speaking clearly: “Understand this: Braun escaped trial not because he was a scientist, but because in this world governed by ‘deals,’ he had value the Americans needed.”

“The Somali government doesn’t need you to build cars... Think about what value you have for them.”

Does he have any value? Tiger Shark opened his mouth but couldn’t think of a single one.

After a pause, he said, “Kimberly, tell me—we’re friends, right?”

“Your greatest value is that at your peak, you voluntarily bowed to the government. When you surrender, you should have firepower, followers, deterrence, and momentum.”

“If you’re desperate or already beaten like a dead dog, surrendering brings you no value.”

Tiger Shark swallowed. “You want me to surrender? That’s like throwing myself into the wolf’s mouth. They’ll throw me in prison.”

Kimberly smiled. “Will they? I don’t think so.”

“This Somali interim government is a facade. The civil war rages, warlords control regions, no one respects it, diplomacy is a mess, internal affairs are a mess, pirates run rampant, and it’s a disgrace.”

“At such a time, if a pirate who’s turned the Red Sea upside down suddenly humbly surrenders, do you think they’ll imprison him or celebrate it like a political achievement?”

“Such a prestigious event. They could even follow up, grant amnesty, diplomatic status, and fame, making other pirates jealous: See? Working with the government has benefits.”

Tiger Shark swallowed, poured himself a glass of water, gulped it down, wiped his lips with his sleeve, his face flushed and visibly excited. “Kimberly, keep talking.”

“What’s the point of throwing you in jail? That would block other pirates from surrendering. If you’re locked up and forgotten, a second and third Tiger Shark will immediately rise in the Red Sea.”

She lowered her voice. “Do you feel now that making peace with the government isn’t impossible?”

Tiger Shark chuckled.

“If there’s a chance, of course I want to try. But Kimberly, do you know anyone in the government? I remember you worked for an international organization. Have you... been promoted?”

Kimberly laughed heartily. “You overestimate me. I left the international organization long ago. Now I just write articles occasionally. I don’t know anyone in the government, and they don’t even glance at me.”

Tiger Shark’s smile froze.

Wade sighed and quietly moved closer to Kimberly.

Tiger Shark’s change in expression was a bad sign. Who knew? He might shout, jump at her, or pull a gun like yesterday.

Sure enough, his tone was angry.

“Kimberly, you said so much, sounded so good, but you don’t know anyone in the government. What good is that?”

Kimberly glanced at him coldly. “You can have your men make contact with the government.”

Tiger Shark’s face twisted as if hearing the world’s biggest joke. “A pirate meeting government officials? Who would believe him? He’d be arrested, beaten, or jailed the moment he appeared! Only someone with real influence can make connections!”

“You talk so much. It sounds good but it’s all crap! Crap!”

He stood up, clenched fists pounding the table hard, rattling cups and plates.

Wade comforted himself: At least Tiger Shark was restrained today, didn’t threaten Kimberly. Progress.

At that moment, Kimberly spoke.

“There are people who can make connections and have influence. You know them too.”

Tiger Shark calmed.

He began to grasp Kimberly’s tactic: women can be cunning, deliberately making him anxious and angry, then offering solutions.

“Who?”

Suspicious eyes shifted from her to Wade. “Him?”

Wade felt the pressure: Please don’t guess wildly. The only African he knew was CoCo, and though mysterious, he definitely wasn’t a Somali street prince.

Kimberly said, “The Saudi ship owner.”

Wade smiled.

It was like a light switched on, revealing everything.

All the buildup, digressions, traps, provocations, guidance, and counsel—seemingly chaotic and random—cleared away, revealing the trump card.

He sighed with relief.

Tiger Shark was puzzled. “I hijacked their ship. They hate me. Why would they help me?”

Kimberly interrupted.

“You hijacked their ship, but the ship is intact, right? The 25 hostages are alive, right? The ship is in your hands. How you use it—to get money or money plus a future—depends on you.”

# Chapter 41

Wade felt that the negotiations were practically over.

That night before sleep, he unusually refrained from fooling around with Kimberly. After washing up, he calmly lay on the floor, using his luggage bag as a pillow, reviewing everything about the negotiations.

She must have long planned how to handle Tiger Shark, which was why she acted so indifferent toward the Sirius.

If he ever lost the bodyguard job, he could sell cosmetics, work in environmental protection, or occasionally negotiate.

I also had to change his impression of the Saudis—they were not just payers, but benefactors of his new life.”

In other words, you have to completely overturn everything he believes to have a chance to lead him.

“By now, I’ve successfully shifted the topic: Tiger Shark no longer thinks about the ransom amount but about how to cooperate with the Saudis... That ship will become a calling card and a gift representing sincerity.”

He’s taking back what he stole as a gift, and yet often receives gratitude.

Maybe because losing then regaining something is a rare surprise.

“Tiger Shark is suspicious and cautious by nature. Pressuring him to decide often backfires.”

The fourth day.

For some unknown reason, the sky was a dull yellow-gray early in the morning. Wade went up to the deck and saw many pirates leaning on the railing, shading their eyes, looking out into the distance.

There, the swirling ochre clouds were thicker.

He asked several people, but no one understood. Finally, he found Shadi, who was gobbling down a boiled fish and said, “Probably a sandstorm.”

Another sandstorm?

Wade’s scalp tingled. “What do we do?”

Shadi thought he was overreacting. “Sandstorms in the Red Sea can last a whole month. Every day we clean the ship of sand. You wake up to a thick layer; just cleaned and there’s another.”

“Will the waves be big?”

“Maybe,” Shadi shrugged, grinning, “but rarely capsizing—the small boats have got us covered.”

Do pirates always comfort like this? Wade was speechless. After soaking in seawater once, he was not keen to repeat.

Unlike the previous decisive talks, today’s negotiations dragged on.

Tiger Shark’s usual ruthless decisiveness turned into hesitation, repetition, and anxiety in the small dining room. This fierce pirate paced, muttering like a confused old woman.

“Kimberly, if, if something unexpected happens, if it’s not as smooth as you said, what do I do?”

Kimberly was drawing, with more than a dozen pencils scattered nearby—deliberate. On day four, according to plan, she should appear distracted and Tiger Shark should be restless.

She replied, “True, nothing is guaranteed—people can even die in their sleep.”

As she spoke, her pencil dragged, sketching a menacing hundred-meter sand wall: a full page of sandstorm, with a broken-window car in the lower left. The picture couldn’t fully show it, but she knew two people were inside.

She glanced at Wade, who noticed and smiled back.

It was nice—some things just need one look, and he understands.

Tiger Shark paced like a trapped beast.

“Just giving the ship back to the Saudis without a cent, how do I explain that to others?”

Kimberly blew the pencil shavings off the paper. “Who said you give it back for free? You still want ransom—you’re not going empty-handed abroad, right?”

Tiger Shark brightened but then anxiety returned.

“But if I take money, will the Saudis be angry? If so, won’t they stop helping me? And if they go back on their word after getting the ship...”

He hesitated again. “Maybe better to ask for more money. Money is real, but a good life feels too fragile.”

Kimberly started drawing something delicate. “So, see what else you can offer them. You can’t force their help—you have to make them eager and willing, even desperate to make it happen.”

“That’s nonsense! The Saudis hate me; how could they do all that?”

Sweat appeared on Tiger Shark’s back. His inner fire threatened to burst out but he suppressed it, then suddenly smiled and leaned toward Kimberly.

“Kimberly, give me a hint. Don’t beat around the bush—we’re friends.”

Wade thought: flexible and resilient—that’s how Tiger Shark became a pirate leader. Shamelessness is a skill not everyone can master.

Kimberly glanced at Tiger Shark. “Think carefully. What else can you do for them?”

Tiger Shark scratched his head in frustration.

“What else... I could stop hijacking their ships, but so many pirates—if I don’t, someone else will.”

Kimberly said, “No, you should hijack, but not hijack.”

She pulled the drawing aside and handed it to Wade, eyes on Tiger Shark.

Wade looked at the paper and grimaced. Kimberly had drawn a terrified little bee, with a note: “Wade’s cherished little bee.”

Women really hold grudges.

Kimberly smiled slightly. “Pirates have an unspoken rule: first come, first served. If you target a ship first, others consider themselves unlucky and won’t touch it. From now on, when Saudi ships enter the Gulf of Aden, you send ships to watch and follow, but for various reasons, never attack... get it?”

Tiger Shark stared at her, mouth slowly opening. “You mean...”

Kimberly smoothed a new sheet. “What’s safer than pirate escort? The Saudis have thousands of ships passing the Gulf of Aden yearly. Receiving this gift, do you think they won’t be overjoyed?”

Surprisingly, the pirates weren’t united; each had their own schemes.

“I’m only negotiating for the Sirius.”

“I’m done.”

Like a little girl seeking praise.

Her voice was barely a whisper. “No. This month isn’t complete. It’s not over yet...”

She lay down, curling up in a vulnerable sleeping position.

Strangely, the celebratory mood suddenly felt heavy.

Kimberly screamed.

“Same old phrase. In real life, your insults really lack variety.”

Kimberly didn’t understand.

She blinked, met Wade’s eyes, and gradually realized. “Now?”

Suddenly embarrassed, she propped herself up on the bed.

Wade said, “On a pirate ship in the Red Sea, in the middle of a sandstorm, a man and woman, neither strangers nor close, have gone too far—moments like this are rare.”

Kimberly bit her lip. The ship tilted again. Wade steadied her waist.

The fishing lamp flickered, casting a pale yellow glow on her face. Strands of half-dry hair lazily lifted in the light. Her gaze flickered, hiding an unknown world inside.

It felt like a deep black sea without end, soft and warm, meeting his eyes and slowly stirring unexpected heat.

She reached out, slowly moving to the edge of her veil.

Said, “I hope this storm blows even harder.”
