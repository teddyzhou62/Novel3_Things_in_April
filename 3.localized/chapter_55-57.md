# Chapter 55

Wade was silent for a moment. He poured himself some wine, only to find the bottle light—almost empty. He had been so engrossed in listening that he hadn’t noticed how much Kimberly had already drunk.

Kimberly’s drunkenness was creeping up on her, nudging him to leave.

Wade smiled. “You want me to go that badly?”

Kimberly smiled back. “I’m not giving you a choice. I’m just sending you off—everyone else’s gone, only you left.”

She rested her chin on the table, watching the candle wax drip and pool at the edge. She picked off the drips one by one, as if counting. “I’ve planned it all out. The lease on the villa runs until April. Those people I feel tied to—whether they still remember me or not—I’m cutting ties with them...”

Life was absurd. As the countdown to the end ticked down, the narrowing path suddenly brought him face to face with her—always just a little too late or too early. She never expected to fall in love with someone in such a short time.

She propped herself up on her arms and staggered toward the bed, mumbling softly, “And my dress... such a shame. It was so beautiful, but I can’t wear it. I won’t even get to dress up...”

She collapsed onto the bed, murmuring as she curled into a ball.

Wade asked, “What will the Hand of God do to you?”

Kimberly muffled her ears with a pillow, voice dull and impatient. “I don’t know. A trial, I guess. Like court—you present one piece of evidence, I present another...”

She gradually fell asleep.

In her saddest moment, she dreamed a sweet dream.

She was a tree, dense with leaves that represented all her attachments. Then came the harsh northern wind and the heavy snow, breaking branches and scattering leaves. She was left with bare, gnarled limbs, ashamed like a plucked duck.

Far away, a line of woodcutters marched forward, their gleaming axes flashing cold light under the pale sun, ready to chop her into firewood.

Suddenly, there was a sound beneath the tree. She looked down and saw Wade holding a paint bucket, painting her branches green one by one.

Curious, she asked, “What are you doing?”

Wade said, “Shh, don’t say a word. I’m dressing you up as a Christmas tree. This way, no one will hurt you.”

She said, “Aren’t you the Christmas tree?”

Wade picked up a small gift and carefully tied it to her dangling branch. “You are, too.”

...

At that moment, the sound of a car came.

Kimberly opened her eyes, dazed for a few seconds. The room was empty, the door half-open, the sky just before dawn, misty and damp from rain drifting outside.

She suddenly realized and stumbled out of bed, rushing to the door.

The jeep that had been parked there was gone—just like her mood at that moment, relieved yet empty.

Kimberly sat cross-legged by the door until voices stirred and the innkeeper came by with morning coffee.

The innkeeper glanced at her, then peeked inside the room with a puzzled look. Kimberly ignored him, took both cups of coffee, no sugar, and gulped down one after the other.

Wiping her mouth with the back of her hand, she said, “I’m checking out today.”

---

Her luggage was still there. She rummaged through it but found no clothes to swap. Unexpectedly, she found a necklace with a small shell pendant. It opened to reveal a roughly made lipstick.

Kimberly smiled. He took her evening gown but returned a modified shirt. He took many of her cosmetics but gave back a crudely made lipstick.

Surprisingly, she felt a small joy, thinking this was a fair trade.

She smoothed the wrinkles on her clothes, carefully combed her hair in front of the mirror, and gently applied the lipstick, tinting her lips bit by bit.

When Scar came in, she had been waiting a while, holding an empty wine glass and softly knocking it against another, eyes closed, listening to the delicate clink of thin glass.

A chill hit her brow. A gun barrel pressed against her.

Kimberly smiled, opening her eyes to look at Scar. “Is this your usual trick? You think pointing a gun at my head will scare me into folding and confessing?”

She pushed Scar’s hand away.

“My interest in your Hand of God isn’t small. I noticed almost as soon as the rumors started.”

Scar sneered coldly. “Guilty conscience, huh?”

Kimberly ignored his sarcasm. “I heard you pride yourselves on being ‘fair, just, not angry, not blind, sparing the innocent, punishing the guilty.’ You give trials, and only after a confession and solid evidence do you punish.”

“Yes.”

Kimberly said, “Really? At first, I thought so too. I figured a trial might be good, since it’s just about me and wouldn’t drag anyone else down.”

Her gaze sharpened. “But what about my bodyguard? What crime did he commit? You didn’t even ask; you just hired snipers to kill him! You blew up the speedboat on the high seas. Did I get a trial for that? Even if you had plenty of evidence, did you listen to my defense? Did I confess?”

Scar was momentarily speechless.

After a pause, he said, “Let me explain, Ms. Lee. You might not know, but your case is special. The higher-ups have mandated you must be tried, meaning my task is to bring you back to Mwenda. I never intended to kill you. The explosives on the speedboat were meant as a warning, but things happened too fast. AK was inexperienced and panicked…”

“As for Mr. Wade… I’m very sorry. Fortunately, it didn’t lead to serious consequences. That was my personal overreach. Afterward, I’ll report truthfully to my superiors and accept any punishment.”

“Ms. Lee, we have different pursuit squads chasing different war criminals. Even the most professional law enforcement can’t guarantee perfect execution. Please don’t doubt the whole organization because of my mistakes. We may stray sometimes, but that’s entirely different from your sanctuary turning into a den of harm.”

Kimberly laughed. “Not bad—sticking to the point, always trying to trap me into a confession. If you get fired from the Hand of God, you might have a future as a negotiator.”

“So, I’m being taken back to Mwenda?”

That was fine. She hadn’t been back in over three years.

Standing up, she asked, “Why is my case special?”

“Because the person accusing you is very important.”

Kimberly chuckled. “The president? He’s embarrassed about sending me the wrong medal and wants to cover it up?”

Suddenly, she had another thought. “I feel like your attitude has changed since before.”

Scar answered, “Because Mr. Wade came to see me at dawn.”

Kimberly’s mind blanked for a moment.

She steadied herself on the table, feeling like a plastic doll with a slit cut in her side. The courage she had mustered earlier with Scar drained away. She felt weak and weightless.

Even her voice was faint. “He’s not gone yet?”

“He told me another version of the sanctuary’s story. I don’t believe it, but I can’t rule out the possibility.”

“Also, Mr. Wade questioned our fairness, for the same reasons you mentioned—blowing up the speedboat on the high seas and hiring snipers to shoot him. He says unless he’s with you the whole time, he has reason to suspect all trials are rigged.”

Kimberly couldn’t focus: Wade still hasn’t left?

“...He promised not to bring weapons. We agreed to let him go to Mwenda. Ms. Lee, pack up. The car’s waiting outside.”

---

Kimberly followed Scar out of the inn. Near the door were two white vans; farther away was the convertible jeep.

She walked over.

The palm mat covering the jeep was lifted—soaked from the long rain. Wade was bent over the hood, fiddling with something. Then he stood, slammed the hood shut, and looked up.

He saw her.

Wade smiled. “Did you sleep well?”

Kimberly whispered, “Why didn’t you leave?”

“I did. Didn’t I drive away? The action of leaving is done. So, how did you feel watching me go?”

Feelings?

She didn’t want to remember. All she knew was that seeing him smile and talk to her like this made everything else in the world meaningless.

Kimberly said, “So that’s what you call ‘dropping the burden and leaving’? You leave with one foot and come right back with the other.”

“Why come back?”

Wade said, “Last night, after you fell asleep, I thought a lot. I finally understood why you were so determined six years ago to have me save you.”

“We both know going back six years is impossible—but I can’t miss both six years ago and now.”

“You don’t want to live, the Hand of God wants you dead. If I leave, it all ends here. Only by staying is there hope.”

“Of course, I could have lied to Scar and helped you escape. But even if you escaped, you might not be happy. I think maybe a trial is good for you. Once it’s over, your heart can open.”

Kimberly warned, “What if the trial’s outcome is terrible?”

“Kimberly, if the accusations aren’t true, why confess out of desperation? I talked to Scar. If your story is true, you’re a victim. You know more about historical politics than I do: in World War II, not all Class A war criminals were executed. Why should you die?”

Kimberly whispered, “Because there’s no evidence. Jeremy died, Serge died—no one can testify. I could be a scheming woman, making up stories and blaming the dead.”

Wade shrugged. “Then find the evidence. No evidence isn’t the end of the world. How about a promise?”

He reached out his hand. Seeing Kimberly hesitate, he hooked her pinky finger firmly.

“Like this.”

“No matter what lies ahead, I’ll stay with you until we can’t go any further. No evidence isn’t scary. There are only a few possibilities: you live, I take care of you; you go to prison, I’m with you; you die, I handle your body. No matter life or death, I’m in charge, okay?”

Kimberly smiled and instinctively tightened her pinky.

Scar’s men honked the horn, probably signaling it was time to go. Wade waved his hand. “Soon.”

When he withdrew his hand, it lingered on her neck, tracing the necklace. Suddenly, with one strong tug, he broke it and threw it hard into the woods behind them.

Kimberly looked at him in surprise.

Wade said, “Don’t rush to condemn yourself. If it were someone else, they might not do any better in that situation.”

He helped Kimberly into the car. As the engine started, she softly said, “Wade?”

“Hm?”

“That necklace was platinum.”

The engine quieted. Wade frowned. “Expensive?”

“A little.”

He paused, then said, “Better go get it back.”

Kimberly watched him jump out of the car.

She couldn’t help laughing aloud—laughing until tears came.

She looked up at the sky washed clean by rain.

No matter what the road ahead held, no matter the trial or the evidence... it seemed not so important anymore.

# Chapter 56

Mwenda lay southwest of Egau, with no need to backtrack. The winding road never doubled back, and Mwenda should be the halfway point.

The journey was slow. Wade’s injury hadn’t healed in the past two days and seemed to be worsening. When tense, he didn’t feel pain, but relaxing brought sharp discomfort. At noon, Kimberly helped rebandage him. By afternoon, she insisted he lie in the back seat while she drove.

Wade agreed—it was safer. Who knew if there’d be trouble ahead? The more he recovered, the better his chances.

At night, they entered South Sudan. CoCo warned it was even more chaotic here—and she wasn’t exaggerating. While setting camp, gunfire rang out for several seconds, then abruptly stopped. The silence was unsettling, as if a boot still hovered, waiting to fall.

Scar ordered no fires and instructed that if they encountered trouble, not to fight but let him negotiate. Different countries, different organizations—clear communication usually led to peace.

Wade found Scar for a chat. They sat in the dark, unable to light a cigarette, eating some dry rations. Scar handed him water. Wade tilted his head back, poured some into his mouth, then passed it back.

Scar sighed, “Yesterday, I wanted you dead. Today, we’re eating together...”

Wade said, “Depends on the situation and interests.”

Scar smiled. “Don’t try to befriend me; I can’t save your Ms. Lee.”

He took off his sunglasses—useless in the dark.

Wade asked, “If my story is true, how would Kimberly be sentenced?”

Scar was silent.

Wade smiled. “Sometimes I think it’s unfair. After the April Massacre, the international community withdrew, letting things spiral. Those who left or just watched weren’t punished. Those who stayed are hunted.”

Scar shot him a sideways glance. “Don’t twist things. Kimberly’s wanted not because she stayed. It’s like volunteering at an orphanage—commendable. But if you use that as cover to traffic children for profit, you’re punished. Different matters.”

Wade said, “You still didn’t answer.”

Scar thought. “I’m not a judge, but if she’s telling the truth, the sentence would be light. Extraordinary times demand considering many factors. If I were in her shoes, I’d have no better solution. If she’d died then, she’d be just another skeleton, no use. Alive... at least a witness.”

He recalled, “Did you know, over three years ago, before the Hand of God formed, Jeremy returned to Mwenda as an investor and philanthropist? He was warmly received by officials, even greeted by citizens at his hotel. Had the scandal not broken, he’d be a hero, remembered fondly.”

“Do you believe Kimberly’s story?”

Scar shook his head. “No.”

“Mr. Wade, the Hand of God has been around three years. I’ve dealt with many criminals. All claim innocence with even more moving stories than Kimberly’s. So what?”

“The court rules on evidence, not sympathy. Don’t think going back to Mwenda for trial means hope—most face the death penalty. Before Serge died, he accused her. Without evidence, she’s still the main culprit.”

He stood, patted Wade’s shoulder. “If you want to help her, find evidence. So far, all you’ve given me is a fanciful tale.”

Before sleep, Wade and Kimberly talked about evidence. Hope was slim, but maybe... key clues would appear. It often takes stubbornness.

But the closer to home, the more hopeless it felt.

Kimberly urged him to rest. He refused.

“You left Mwenda six years ago. Jeremy was murdered three years ago. You visited his place then, meaning you had contact—didn’t you keep any evidence, like recordings?”

Kimberly corrected him. “I had no contact. Three years ago, we crossed paths only because it was the third anniversary of the April Massacre.”

She had returned alone once, unable to explain why. She visited many places: schoolyards with flags flying and children reading aloud, the lush riverbank with boats coming and going.

This wounded country was trying to move forward, while she remained shrouded in past fog.

---

She quit the African aid organization. Her boss tried to keep her, saying her resume was rare.

She laughed bitterly—a single event could have many faces: wealth to Jeremy and others, a touching story to outsiders, medals to the president, capital to her boss, and a nightmare to her.

---

Psychotherapy never helped. In dreams, she heard the UN convoy leaving. Mornings she woke shedding clumps of hair, spirit broken. She chose a less stressful editorial job. The editor frowned at her drafts, saying, “Miss, your emotions must be intense, your pen sharp, pointing out social issues. You must be a fighter to move readers.”

She wasn’t a fighter—cowering inside, her secret festering into a part of her body like a pus-filled wound.

---

Someone suggested love and family could heal trauma, so she had Ming Jiang. He filled much of her time, talking endlessly about the environment, papers, scholarships, research topics. She always listened from start to finish, feeling less alone.

That became a reason for his proposal: “You never find me annoying. You listen seriously and never interrupt. Kimberly, you’re the most understanding girlfriend I’ve ever had.”

That night by the woods, Jeremy pinned her on a dead body, telling her to return to the North and live the life she wanted.

But she had no life left.

Back at the inn, she sat on the bed and turned on the TV.

One channel showed the president speaking: “This is a country rebuilding. We must seize opportunities, attract investment, and revitalize the economy. Development is our future.”

Another channel showed protests, police firing tear gas, young organizers shouting hoarsely: “How can the government cut funding for war criminal hunts? Is this condoning impunity? Those who died deserve justice! Just because criminals fled abroad, does that mean we do nothing?”

The last channel froze her.

It showed Jeremy smiling, wearing a flower garland, speaking to a crowd in the square: “I have deep friendship with the people of Mwenda, before and after the war. I will do my best…”

Kimberly grabbed a pillow and threw it at the TV.

Kimberly smiled. “Postwar Mwenda attracted investment with incentives. The government almost lost money for years to thank medal recipients. Jeremy isn’t in it for charity. What do you think he’s really after?”

Kimberly nodded.

In Mwenda’s state guest hotel, Kimberly met Jeremy. He was cautious, had her searched before letting her in.

Jeremy’s words lingered.

Jeremy laughed. “No big ambitions. He blew through the money and came to me broke. I gave him money to stay hidden. He’d do my dirty work. If I go down, he’ll come for you. You’ll be finished, like that tire in the sanctuary, punctured. Whether it’s you or not, it’s you.”

The next morning, the convoy set off again. By noon, they entered Mwenda.

Mwenda was the most beautiful place so far—not sandy like Sudan or unpredictable like Egau. Rolling hills dotted with forests and rivers. In the valley, golden monkeys and gorillas wandered near the road.

As they rounded another mountain pass, a circle of white buildings appeared below.

The entrance gate bore the sign “Sanatorium.” The car stopped, and two local women waited.

Scar told Wade, “Inside, you and Ms. Lee will be separated. She has a different status and will be held separately. The trial is public. We’ll notify you of the time.”

He thought this was the secret HQ of the Hand of God, but after getting off the car, he saw it was really a sanatorium.

Many amputees sat around the courtyard. Passing a room, the door opened like a class dismissal. The first to come out had no legs, walking on hands. Seeing Scar, he looked up and greeted him.

“You put your HQ in a sanatorium?”

Scar said, “It’s an asset of the Hand of God.”

He pointed to the amputees. “The April Massacre left not only bodies but countless survivors maimed in body and mind. I, missing an eye, am considered lucky.”

“You might not know, many survivors outlasted the war but not what followed—despair, disabilities, no livelihood. Society’s patience and attention are limited, but they live long. These problems will haunt them.”

“Just now was a handicraft class, embroidery and such. Those with hands can learn skills and support themselves. Starting this year, we’re shifting focus to help them. It’s not abandoning war criminal pursuits, but…”

“We believe hate is not food. You can’t live on it. Priorities matter. The dead won’t return, but the living must go on.”

He remembered something. “Ms. Lee’s trial should start tomorrow. We’re not a formal court, but we have judges, prosecutors, and a jury. The jury includes refugees and some international members to avoid bias. You can join; we don’t mind.”

Wade was silent.

Privately, he hoped the Hand of God wasn’t too official, maybe even reckless. That way, if the verdict was bad, he’d feel less guilty about taking extreme actions.

Scar stopped at a room. “You’ll stay here.”

“My room?”

“Shared.”

Wade was stunned, then realized. “You’re watching me?”

Scar didn’t deny it. “Mr. Wade, given your past, it’s hard to say if you’d react extremely if Ms. Lee was sentenced to death. So we thought it necessary to assign someone to watch you.”

Wade laughed and strode up the steps. “What, haven’t you learned from the sniper? With my past, you think just anyone can handle me now?”

His voice cut off abruptly.

Inside were two single beds. One was cluttered with clothes and belongings. Hanging above it was a dried shark’s tooth about the size of a swim ring.

# Chapter 57

Before sleeping, Wade went to see Kimberly.

There was a guard at the door, changing shifts. The room was plain, almost like his place in Helsinki: basic living facilities.

The only difference—so suffocating—was a wall filled floor to ceiling with writing.

Different handwriting styles, mostly English, some other languages. Like final confessions, prayers, drawings, long messages. Wade’s pressure rose sharply.

Kimberly seemed to sense his thoughts. “This room is for those on trial. One comes, one goes. Now it’s my turn.”

By the wall was a table with various pens. Wade smirked at the thoroughness.

He took Kimberly’s hand and looked at the wall.

Someone had written “sorry” dozens of times in messy strokes, ending with “May God forgive me.”

Some apologies were to family, confessing sins and the pain they caused, asking wives not to tell the children.

Some were hysterical: “I didn’t kill! The devil possessed me! The real me didn’t kill!”

Some cursed: “Without the war, I wouldn’t have killed! The instigators should be punished, why me?”

Others were angry: “I only killed this many. XX should die more. Why not arrest him?”

Wade murmured, “What kind of mindset is this?”

Kimberly said, “The ‘I’m not afraid of poverty, just afraid you’re poorer than me’ mindset.”

They laughed together, then fell silent.

A flat wall, flat writing, but behind them a grand, complex world. Beyond victim and perpetrator, they were all human—emotions, attachments, friends, family. Each thread pulled made one sigh.

Wade asked, “If it were you, what would you write?”

Kimberly picked up a pen, searched the wall for a gap, stood on tiptoe, and wrote:

*May Wade live in peace all his life.*

Signed: Kimberly.

Wade smiled. “You can’t write Chinese well. Your ‘Kim’ character is always choppy…”

His eyes stung, and he hesitated before laughing. “You’re so immoral, you know that?”

Kimberly said, “I know I shouldn’t get sentimental now and deepen your attachment. Maybe I should act cold, send you away, say I never loved you and was just teasing. But…”

Her voice lowered. “I’m afraid I really don’t have much time left. I want to leave you my true feelings.”

“Without you, this would be the most freeing moment of my life. Death isn’t scary—I’ve been ready for a long time.”

She hugged Wade, resting her head gently on his chest.

“My only wish is for you to be well. No matter what happens, you have to be well. We promised. Live well, eat well, sleep well. Send me flowers on anniversaries. And no matter who you like in the future, don’t compare them to me. If they’re gentler or prettier, get lost—no comparisons allowed.”

Wade chuckled. He wrapped one arm around her and took the pen from her hand with the other. Looking at her words, he circled “Wade” and drew an arrow to “Kimberly,” adding two characters:

*May we live in peace all our lives.*

Signed: Kimberly & Wade.

Reindeer was earnest. “Wade, it’s not that I’m opposing you. I talked to the other side at length—there’s only one demand: evidence against evidence. You have to respect the verdict.”

Wade asked, “Do you believe Kimberly? Honestly.”

Reindeer paused. “You know, I always thought she was strange. She’s clever; making up a nearly flawless story wouldn’t be hard.”

Wade smiled bitterly. “Alright, before the verdict, help me. Look through Kimberly’s editorials. She supposedly changed style. I want to know when exactly. Also, I want more details about Jeremy’s murder.”

After hanging up, CoCo glanced sideways. “Will it help?”

Wade said, “It’s like digging a well. If you stop at two meters, you’ll never get water.”

“If you keep digging, maybe no water yet, but if the shovel never stops, hope is always there.”

Before hope dries up, he wasn’t giving up.

At six, the trial took place in an unremarkable room at the sanatorium’s corner. The layout resembled a standard court.

The jury had about a dozen people; two or three wore masks and hats to avoid being recognized. The others seemed used to it and uninterested.

A corner was sectioned off as a special observation area with one-way mirrors—people inside could see out, but no one outside could see in.

Wade signaled CoCo. “That VIP is probably inside there.”

CoCo warned, “Wade, don’t think about kidnapping someone as a hostage.”

Before Wade could reply, Kimberly walked in.

She looked decent, expressionless, eyes briefly scanning him before sitting down.

The opening procedures bored Wade. He was irritated by the many court rules.

The prosecutor was a middle-aged woman, polite and articulate. She read the indictment, summarizing the sanctuary’s history. Before she finished, murmurs rose in the gallery.

Kimberly sat still, as if deaf to whispers.

When it was Kimberly’s turn, her tone was calm. She offered an alternative version, denying the indictment’s false claims one by one.

During cross-examination, CoCo yawned twice and nudged Wade. “So boring. A fight would be more straightforward.”

Wade thought: that’s because you don’t care.

He caught every word, his scalp tingling.

The prosecutor’s questions were slow and deliberate, mostly yes-or-no.

—“Did you establish the sanctuary?”

—“After your colleagues disappeared, did you cooperate with Jeremy and Serge?”

—“Did you gather a small group of refugees and tell them about the escape boat?”

—“Did you know it was a death route?”

...

Kimberly kept answering “yes,” her voice growing softer, pauses longer. Wade nearly lost it but was helpless.

A female witness, one of 175 survivors, was called.

The judge asked, “Who do you think was really in charge in the sanctuary?”

The witness looked at Kimberly. “Kim. We knew she worked for an international organization. When the UN convoy evacuated, she was allowed on. Jeremy and Serge joined later. We didn’t know them. Kim said they were volunteers. We trusted Kim, so we trusted them.”

Kimberly shuddered.

As expected, the most devastating part was the evidence.

The prosecutor first showed a list: “This is a roster of 292 sanctuary residents. Six years ago, Jeremy handed over the original list of 175, kept at the national archives. We compared and confirmed 175 match the original, 117 are missing.”

She didn’t say where it came from, only that it was from a key figure in the Hand of God.

“Because he gave us a letter exposing the sanctuary’s secret and the list, we began to doubt Jeremy’s halo. Otherwise, the truth might have stayed buried longer.”

Wade’s gaze fell on the observation area. Was it Ennu? He shouldn’t have been in the sanctuary then, or the media would have dug it up. Maybe he had relatives who died there and was especially focused on Kimberly’s case.

The second type of evidence was letters and diaries from sanctuary refugees.

The prosecutor read critical passages.

—“Including me, only eight people stayed in Kim’s room. She said there was a boat on the big river. Tickets were expensive, but no one thought that mattered compared to life.”

—“I noticed several times Kim sent out workers at night, got up before dawn waiting. They huddled, talking happily. I asked Kim once; she said they were moving some people to nearby sanctuaries.”

Photos and bank records from Serge proved Kimberly had contact with Hukas leaders and that she received the most money.

Most unexpectedly, Serge’s deathbed recording played.

The room was eerily silent. The tape spun slowly. Serge’s terrified voice filled the air.

He said, “She really was in charge. Jeremy and I just followed her. We were gold seekers; we didn’t know much. She was brilliant and taught us. We just did as told…”

“Jeremy feared she’d silence us. We planned escape. I kept a low profile so she couldn’t find me. After Jeremy died, I approached her. She said it was the work of Mwenda’s Avengers and told me to run…”

The tape stopped.

The judge asked Kimberly, “Did you have such a conversation with Serge, accusing Jeremy’s death on the Hand of God and telling him to run?”

Kimberly was silent, then said, “Yes.”

Wade’s heart sank.

The prosecutor suddenly stood, voice angry, “I ask the court not to accept the defendant’s self-defense. She is lying. We have enough evidence to prove Jeremy did not die at the hands of the Hand of God. He was already dead before we approached him.”

...

The courtroom erupted in chaos, voices rising wave after wave.

CoCo leaned over. “Do you still believe her?”
