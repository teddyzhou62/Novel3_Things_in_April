# Chapter 22

Kimberly remained fairly calm. “Dust storm. Get in the car, quickly.”

Wade stuck his dagger back into the leather sheath at his waist, tossed the melon into the woven basket, shut the car door first, then jumped in and slammed the canopy down with a loud bang.

Outside, it had grown even darker—a hazy, ginger-colored gloom. Fine sand grains pelted the windshield. Wade drove the car into an open clearing, then stopped and turned on both the front and rear lights.

He knew the emergency protocol during a sandstorm: avoid the road, turn on the lights to stay visible, so that cars trying to rush through wouldn’t crash into you.

Kimberly grabbed a protective cover to shield the satellite phone and asked Wade to help tear several large plastic bags. Using transparent tape, they wrapped the air conditioner unit tightly.

Her priorities were clear: first, communication; second, air conditioning.

Wade thought she was overreacting. “The car doors are already closed.”

He had never experienced a sandstorm himself, but had seen news coverage: When a sandstorm hits, stay indoors, close all doors and windows, and wait it out.

Kimberly sneered. “Up north in Africa, it’s the Sahara Desert. But this dust storm here is the largest in the world—visible even on satellite cloud maps.”

Wade cursed silently.

He didn’t need her to explain; he could see it.

Straight ahead, a towering wall of sand rolled forward—a giant mushroom cloud stacking into a nearly gray-black barrier, rapidly approaching, blotting out the sky like an apocalyptic scene from a movie.

The car looked like a fragile sprout before this massive sand wall.

Wade asked, “Will people die?”

“If you’re unlucky, yes.”

Before Kimberly finished, the roof, hood, and windshield suddenly rang with sharp pattering sounds—thick, sticky yellowish rain like paint splattered, sliding down the glass.

Kimberly explained softly, “That bright line at the top of the storm means there’s rain, but it’s too dry here to fall heavily.”

Sure enough, the dirty rain stopped quickly, replaced by dense, fine sand grains whipped by fierce winds, rattling the car’s sides and roof with a rustling noise like rodents gnawing furiously.

The sound made Wade’s scalp tingle.

“If I try to drive through, can I make it?”

He had once charged through a rainstorm—a memorable experience—breaking through a black curtain of violent rain in the blink of an eye into bright sunlight.

Kimberly shook her head. “If the sandstorm is large, it might take over fifteen minutes to get through. Visibility is so low, headlights won’t help. Hitting an obstacle is suicide. And with strong winds, a fast-moving car can easily flip.”

“So we just wait?”

“You can also pray, if that helps.”

Wade gave a bitter smile. As total darkness descended, his hands clenched involuntarily, ears ringing briefly with a chaotic noise.

The car was surely swallowed by the storm’s heart. The lights were useless—he couldn’t see a thing, waving his hands in front of his eyes, total blackness.

His nostrils filled with the smell of dust. Touching his face, he found a layer of fine sand had stuck to his skin without him noticing.

In that instant, the image of the melon flashed through his mind.

Damn, definitely not edible now.

Then he realized something was off: it was too quiet, as if he were the only person left in the world.

“Kimberly?”

From the darkness, she whispered, “Here.”

Wade exhaled.

“Isn’t this a sandstorm? Why is there no sound at all?”

He could handle violent storms and flying sand, but this eerie silence gave him chills.

Kimberly smiled. “Nervous?”

“Just a little.”

“Maybe it’s a dry desert fog. Visibility drops to zero; even camels lose their way. It should be temporary—the storm is moving forward, the strong winds are coming soon... Don’t you think it’s like sitting in a movie theater in complete darkness?”

At a moment like this, she could still think of a cinema!

He only cared if the car would hold up, and about that little bee on the car antenna…

Kimberly seemed to sense his thoughts. “This is a natural disaster. Worrying won’t help. Save your energy.”

Her indifferent tone made Wade want to open the door and push her out.

But, yeah, worrying probably wouldn’t help.

Wade leaned back against the seat. The headrest was hard and digging into his neck, making it ache.

What was he saying? Oh, movies.

That had been his childhood dream.

“When I was scraping by in Chinatown, I heard people talk about cinemas—how big the screens were, how many rows of seats. I always wanted to see one. But I had no money, just eating scraps here and there.”

Kimberly’s breathing was light; he knew she was listening.

“Later, someone taught me how to sneak in. That theater was messy; ticket checks weren’t strict. They told me to act confident.”

A bang on the door—the wind had tossed a stone against it.

The wind had arrived.

Suddenly, everything changed. Countless grains of sand pelted the car, the scratching sound relentless, like it would never stop. The headlights struggled through the swirling sand, like light through a sieve, tossed by the wind.

Several times, the car felt momentarily light, Wade’s heart jumped, then the tires hit the ground again.

“I mingled with the crowd, head held high, pretending to be rich and proud... Maybe I overdid it—you know, no one’s that proud just to see a movie.”

Kimberly chuckled softly.

“The ticket checker suddenly shouted behind me, ‘Stop!’ I ran—the theater was on the third floor. I dashed down the stairs, heart pounding... then slipped and tumbled to the bottom. When I stood up and wiped the blood from my face, I realized I’d broken my nose.”

“That’s when I noticed no one was chasing me. For just one ticket, the checker was too lazy to chase me down three flights.”

“Then why did you run?”

Kimberly seemed to think he was the type to surrender easily, smile, and cooperate with the police, saying, “Thanks for your hard work.”

Wade said, “I was too embarrassed to get caught.”

“It’s one thing to embarrass yourself—getting slapped or kicked, being called a nobody... That’s fine, I wasn’t raised by anyone either. But being called a thief just because you’re Chinese? That’s humiliating. One person’s shame drags down so many others.”

He turned to Kimberly. “What about you? In Scandinavia, with all the welfare, you must have it good. Do you go to the movies often?”

After all, she thought of cinemas during a sandstorm.

Kimberly shook her head. “I don’t go. There are no Chinese films there. When I first arrived abroad, I couldn’t understand the language, couldn’t read books or watch TV—felt like an idiot.”

“My adoptive parents worried I’d be lonely, so they set up a TV and DVD player in my room, bought me lots of Chinese DVDs.”

Another bang—the sand hit the car window.

Wade suddenly worried: the car body was sturdy, but the windows were weak points. If they shattered...

He fumbled for duct tape to cover all the windows.

Kimberly remained calm.

“At that time, most overseas DVDs were from Hong Kong. The same few stars kept appearing—Jackie Chan, Chow Yun-fat, Stephen Chow…”

Right, Chinatown had specialty video stores, stacks of discs, small square TVs, mostly in Cantonese. He had seen plenty of costume dramas and modern stories.

“When I liked something, I watched it over and over. ‘A Chinese Odyssey’ was one I saw many times. I still remember a line from it.”

Wade found the tape and ripped it open, sticking a long strip across the windshield.

A line? Was it “Love you for ten thousand years”?

He recalled a scene: a restaurant owner caught cheating by his wife, who threw his clothes and shoes from the second floor. The man knelt below, crying, begging for one more chance, professing his eternal love.

The Chinese onlookers laughed uproariously, while the cheating man sobbed uncontrollably.

Kimberly muttered softly, almost to herself, “My ideal man is a peerless hero.”

That line?

Such poetic words suddenly brought into reality made Wade both embarrassed and amused. No matter who a woman is—even someone like Kimberly—every girl dreams of her “ideal man.”

Another strip of tape went on, preserving the glass. At least dozens of strips would be needed per window.

“In my darkest times, he would descend from the heavens to save me.”

Wade frowned.

Was that really the original line?

“But I never waited.”

Wade stopped, looking at her.

Kimberly raised her chin slightly, lips curling in a strange mix of charm and emptiness under the faint car lights.

“So, I won’t wait anymore.”

Wade’s expression changed.

Cracks spiderwebbed across the side window by her face, fine white fractures spreading like a dense spider’s net.

“Get down!”

Without thinking, Wade grabbed her waist, flipped over, pressing her down, trying to get as low as possible. At that moment, the glass shattered with a crash.

The sandstorm burst into the car, the interior filled with the clattering chaos of sand blades flying at high speed.

Wade gasped, crouching lower, his right arm tightening around her waist, left hand reaching into the basket to find the satellite phone.

He found it and breathed a sigh of relief.

The two most important things were safe—mission accomplished.

As for the air conditioner, the melon, and the little bee… let them go with the sand.

Once the initial chaos passed, Kimberly coughed uneasily, sand stinging her nose. She leaned her forehead against his neck.

Wade lowered his head, arching his shoulders to give her room.

She whispered, “Are you hurt?”

“Maybe… I don’t know.”

He couldn’t say for sure. There were unavoidable scrapes, and maybe glass shards had grazed his back. But exposed to the storm, his body quickly went numb—no pain.

“How long will the storm last?”

He felt the car shifting slightly, moving sideways. Now strong winds blew through both side windows—they were broken.

“About an hour. It keeps moving forward. The second half will weaken, with less wind and sand.”

An hour?

He needed to cover himself somehow—another hour like this and his back would be raw.

Wade looked at Kimberly.

“Help me take off my shirt.”

“I’ve got a knife at my waist. Pull my shirt up, cut it near my neck with the knife, and wrap my head and face. I’m going to get the tent from the back.”

Kimberly nodded and reached from the outside. Wade warned her, “Go through my shirt. There’s sand outside; it’ll cut your hands.”

She withdrew her hand, lifted his shirt hem, and slipped it around his strong abdomen to his back.

Going through the shirt.

Wherever she touched, the fabric was nearly torn—strips and threads everywhere. One wound was sticky, gritty with sand.

She didn’t say a word, pulled the dagger from his back, then slowly withdrew.

Wade heard the sound of fabric being cut and torn—but not on him.

She wrapped the torn half of her shirt around his back, tying it tight.

Then she bent slightly, pulled the other half of her shirt from behind.

“Lower your head.”

He lowered it.

Another shirt owed to her.

The tab was never going to be settled.

# Chapter 23

Wade was glad visibility inside the car was low—Kimberly must have wrapped him up looking ridiculous.

Slowly, he pulled his arm free from her waist.

“When I get over there, you immediately duck under the seat and curl up tight, protect your head and face. Got it?”

“Got it. I’ve dodged bullets before; you don’t have to tell me.”

Wade smiled and exhaled, bracing himself to slip into the rear compartment.

“You’re young; don’t be so pessimistic. If he doesn’t come, wait longer. Like waiting for a bus—you’ll get it eventually.”

“Huh?”

She had blanked out.

“The world isn’t safe. Maybe they’re delayed—ship hijacked, sandstorm hitting. Be patient. Don’t grit your teeth and say ‘I won’t wait anymore.’ That’s childish.”

Before he finished, his eyes hardened and he dashed out.

The moment he left, Kimberly’s protective calm vanished. The wind and sand grew louder. Without thinking, she ducked down, hair whipped by the wind, scalp aching.

A man who plays with paper planes calling her childish.

Seconds later, a cheerful whistle sounded from the rear compartment. Wade flipped down from the car roof, pulling something open.

It was a large canvas tent that covered the front seats. Sand instantly pounded the tent, sounding like a heavy rain.

Kimberly looked up, eyes wide.

Above her eyebrows, a faint snapping sound, then a pale green glow appeared—a glow stick.

Above the light, Wade’s smiling eyes appeared.

He greeted her, “Hi.”

Kimberly sat up, annoyed.

Wade sat beside her, handing over a bag.

“Your shawl—take it out and wrap yourself.”

Purely out of kindness, grateful she had sacrificed a shirt to help him.

She refused. “Am I that indecent?”

She wore a black tube top, shoulders and neck exposed, a fair stretch of waist showing, collarbones with two shallow curves—definitely presentable.

“Have you been to a beach?”

Wade nodded, of course.

“Those bikini girls wear less than me, and you stare at them; I dress like this and you want me to wrap a shawl? It’s a nuisance.”

Life was full of mysteries. How did she know he stared at those beach girls?

Wade quickly handed her the first aid kit to change the subject.

“Can you help me?”

He turned his back, grabbed the ragged shirt hem, pulled it over his head, then untied the bandages she’d tied.

Kimberly studied the glow stick’s light.

Many small scrapes, two bleeding cuts with exposed flesh, sand caked in the wounds—hard to look at.

She stuck the glow stick in the gap beside the seat and soaked gauze in alcohol, carefully cleaning his wounds.

Wade asked, “You okay with this?”

“Even if my motives for going to Mwenda weren’t pure, my emergency skills were solid—I even helped deliver Tiger Shark’s head. If you doubt me, you do it yourself.”

Wade smiled. His broad shoulders rose and fell with each breath, skin hot to the touch.

Men’s bodies seemed naturally warm, unlike women’s, who often felt cool.

Kimberly lowered her eyes, twisting the cap of the skin adhesive.

Wade suddenly asked, “What about the radio?”

That man—he remembered everything, then asked questions at unexpected times, like that day at the Turkish airport in front of fashion magazines: “Why choose me?”

Kimberly fell silent.

After a moment, she looked down and pressed the adhesive carefully onto his wound.

Some strands of hair brushed his back, tingling and itchy.

“During the Mwenda massacre, the Hukas activated radio broadcasts simultaneously—24/7 messages calling to kill the Kwatis, calling them enemies, bugs, cockroaches.”

“We set up a safe zone in a primary school to shelter Kwati refugees. One by one, Hukas surrounded the school in cars, broadcasting loudspeakers, shouting threats: ‘We will storm in soon, kill the cockroaches. We will kill you all; blood will flow like rivers.’”

“That voice was always in our ears. Sometimes it stopped briefly, but as soon as you caught your breath, the scratching sounds returned—day and night, even in dreams. Everywhere.”

She stopped, staring blankly at the adhesive.

That voice echoed again, overwhelming, mixed with mad laughter and clashing blades.

—“We will kill you all; blood will flow like rivers. We must wipe out all cockroaches and those who protect them...”

Wade said softly, “Hey.”

He didn’t know when he’d turned around.

Kimberly looked up; a person’s voice can change, becoming warm and deep.

“Is it hard to forget? Hard to recover? Even therapy doesn’t help?”

Kimberly asked him back, “What does recovery mean?”

She raised her left arm, showing a healing claw-shaped scar, dark scabs forming.

“Is this recovery? You always know the skin there isn’t the same.”

“I want to get back to normal, to put life back on track. I made plans—exercise, study, socialize, date, watch comedies. I read many psychology books, but they didn’t work. So I went to a therapist.”

She laughed bitterly.

“I watched his lips; I could predict every sentence he’d say. I could give all his advice better than him. I’m more persuasive.”

Wade reached out, cradling her left arm, running his fingers gently along the wound edges. Not bad—healed well.

He said, “Kimberly, I’m not qualified to tell you to let go or be strong, or that no obstacle is insurmountable—your experience, I haven’t lived it, most people haven’t.”

Like war, trauma takes generations to heal.

“So I can only say, if you ever need help, come find me.”

“I won’t charge you. I hope you… offer willingly.”

Kimberly looked at him, neither smiling nor speaking.

Wade felt awkward. After a long pause, he whispered, pleading:

“Can you at least smile a little? I thought you would… otherwise I can’t save face.”

“Then stay on stage longer. You’ve got a good figure—broad shoulders, narrow waist—and aren’t afraid of being seen.”

She turned away, curled into the seat, burying her head, hiding a faint smile.

If only he had met her earlier—much earlier—maybe things would be different.

Strangely, Wade glanced down at his own abs.

Not bad… yeah, he thought so too.

Outside, the sandstorm seemed to have lessened. The fine, dense sand noise was strangely soothing.

He sighed, relaxed, despite the storm, broken windows, his aching back, and the car filled with sand.

Relaxation was all about the mind.

Wade looked at Kimberly.

The glow stick’s light was fading. She curled quietly in the seat, looking small.

She wasn’t short—only about ten centimeters shorter than him—but when he held her, he could cover her completely. Her waist was slim; one arm was more than enough to hold her.

She mentioned Mwenda many times.

If he’d met her earlier, would he have saved her?

He ran the possibilities through his mind.

Probably yes. He didn’t have many friends, but if Erinn or the Reindeer were in trouble, could he ignore it? She was a woman in such danger—just thinking about it made his heart ache.

If she called him, choking on tears, he wouldn’t be able to bear it—even if she gave less money…

Wait, better remove the money part… no, she wasn’t family. Helping her without pay wasn’t right; it couldn’t be explained.

Better keep track, then.

So, he would go for her. Even though war zones were dangerous, CoCo said the worse the place, the more useful people like him were.

He would go.

Time passed. The glow stick was out of light. In the darkness, Kimberly suddenly called.

“Wade?”

“Yeah?”

“The storm seems to have passed.”

Wade sat up, listening carefully. Then, with a whoosh, he pulled down the tent cover.

---

Inside and outside the car, the world was a sea of reddish sand haze.

Saying the storm had passed wasn’t quite right—it had just moved on to wreak havoc elsewhere, beginning another round of chaos.

But where it had raged, the world was quiet as the edge of the earth.

Visibility was just over ten meters. The car was parked on sandy ground, tires crushing a patch of saltbush. Not far off, a dead tree leaned, as if a person had twisted their back and couldn’t stand.

The car’s roof canopy was stuck by sand. Wade pushed hard, finally freeing it, only to get a cascade of sand poured over his head.

He didn’t mind, brushing off his hair and spitting out sand from his mouth.

There was still much to do.

— Kimberly was mostly fine, barely injured.

He laid out the tent groundcloth beside the car, helping her sit. “This is your activity area. Don’t wander.”

— The satellite phone was fine, thankfully wrapped in a protective cover.

He opened a small gap, pulled out and extended the antenna, starting the automatic satellite search, then placed it atop the car roof.

— The air conditioner.

The taped areas were intact, but the plastic bag covering was shattered by sand. He patted the metal casing; sand poured down.

This kind of appliance was doomed with that much sand inside.

Air conditioner—dead.

— The bottled water and most of the dry food in the rear compartment, though half-buried in sand, were mostly unharmed. Some consolation.

— Watermelon—dead; tomatoes—dead; dates…

The dates were okay. Wade scooped some, blew off the dust, rinsed them in a plastic bag filled with water, then transferred them to another bag.

He looked at her. “Want some dates?”

Kimberly nodded. “Bring them over. My bodyguard said this cloth marks my activity area—I can’t stray.”

Wade didn’t move. “Your bodyguard said you should get them yourself…”

He suddenly stopped.

A beeping sound echoed, hanging in the reddish sand haze.

Kimberly raised her eyes, whispering, “Answer the phone.”

# Chapter 24

The Saudis had left; CoCo had returned to the southern province. The Reindeer said, “Got you a military satellite phone. Tiger Shark wants to contact you directly.”

Kimberly stood, her eyes deepening, retreating to the first time they met—expression cold and definitive, like a black-and-white painting.

Wade answered the phone, spoke a few words, then handed it to her. “Tiger Shark wants to talk to you.”

She refused. “Is that Tiger Shark himself? Tell them I only talk to Tiger Shark.”

Clearly, it wasn’t.

Wade didn’t mind—as long as they gave clear instructions, whether it was Tiger Shark or a dried shark’s mouth made no difference.

After the call, the plan was clearer.

“Tiger Shark’s people are already on the high seas. Negotiations will take place on a large ocean fishing vessel. The pirates on board are disguised as fishermen.”

Kimberly was unsurprised.

This was a common pirate trick—posing as ordinary fishing boats. Once they target a cargo ship, they send armed speedboats to seize it.

The industry called this the “mother and child ship” tactic—the mother ship provided lookout and cover. If needed, they’d fire shoulder-launched rockets at the cargo ship, causing chaos to aid the assault.

“Let’s get to the coast quickly—the more remote the village, the better. Once there, use the satellite phone to send GPS coordinates. Someone will come by speedboat to pick us up.”

He felt uneasy.

It felt like a game, with a player controlling everything, hidden like the Man in the Iron Mask, while they were transparent—everything exposed, including their movements.

Kimberly laughed at him. “In times like these, equality is a joke. To be frank, those are kidnappers.”

“Is Tiger Shark reliable? Could he turn against you?”

“I don’t know him well. I don’t trust him.”

Wade frowned.

He sat down on the groundcloth.

Kimberly asked, “What’s wrong?”

“I don’t like boats. Negotiating on a ship, in the open sea—water all around, sky above. If something goes wrong, it’s a dead end.”

He could hide in jungles, survive in mountains, live in snowfields, but the sea...

In the ocean, you hold no control over your fate, drifting with the waves.

“If—just if—I can’t protect you, I’ll probably be dead already. If you fall into pirate hands, what then?”

Kimberly glanced at him. “As your client’s bodyguard, is it appropriate to say such gloomy things? For that, I’ll tell the Saudis to deduct a thousand euros from your pay.”

Wade stared at her.

Couldn’t she tell he was worried?

He was crazy to have bought her dates as a treat.

“Eat sand.”

He got up with a stern face, ignoring her, clearing sand from the car, rearranging gear, smoothing and sealing jagged broken glass edges, testing the car.

As he wiped the windshield, Kimberly came over, laughing.

“Hey.”

“Miss Lee, please move; you’re in my way.”

Kimberly opened the door and sat in the driver’s seat.

“Don’t worry about my safety. I’m almost worthless to Tiger Shark.”

Wade sneered. Who said he was worried?

He kept wiping.

“First, the pirates want money. An oil tanker in their hands is a hot potato—can’t sail, can’t sell the cargo, have to feed hostages on board. Every extra day costs them. So they’re eager to act, and they pin more hope on me than the Saudis.”

Wade shook the rag, flinging sand everywhere.

“Second, pirates run a ‘business.’ Hijacking ships is their livelihood. If they attack negotiators, no one will negotiate with them next time. So when Tiger Shark lost his temper during a failed negotiation, he shot hostages, but not the negotiators.”

None of this concerned Wade. He was a bodyguard, not a negotiator or hostage. He just wanted to clean the windows.

“Third, I once saved Tiger Shark’s life. That’s a fact and my guarantee. Reliable or not, he’ll give me face.”

Wade forcefully opened the hood, peered inside. Some sand got in, but nothing major.

“Fourth, the deduction thing was a joke.”

He slammed the hood shut.

Smiling like nothing had happened, he said, “A few broken windows and the air conditioner, but the car’s basically fine. If you’re hot, we can drive at night—desert nights cool down a lot.”

“We’ll rest here a bit longer, then head out. Water supply’s enough—you can take a quick shower. It’s uncomfortable with all the sand on you.”

Kimberly got out, staring at him for a long time.

“You change your attitude so much when you say no deductions—aren’t you embarrassed?”

Wade looked puzzled. “What?”

“You were serious and silent before, didn’t want to talk to me.”

“Oh, that. I was in a bad mood, but don’t take it personally.”

He gestured toward the car’s rear.

“Look.”

“Look at what?”

Wade sighed. “The little bee’s gone.”

“CoCo bought it for me—a precious farewell gift. You know, we hadn’t seen each other for a long time. It’s the first thing he ever gave me. I treasure it.”

Kimberly looked at the antenna.

If he hadn’t said anything, she wouldn’t have known the car had a modified antenna or what the little bee was.

She smiled understandingly. “You really treasured it?”

“Yeah.”

“You usually hang your treasures on the car antenna?”

Wade coughed. “Not the best idea…”

Kimberly said, “Condolences. I’m going to shower.”

Wade quickly set up the tent for her.

Maybe because the sand haze lingered, it got dark early. He laid the groundcloth at the tent entrance, lay down to rest, keeping watch.

He had imagined this before—guarding a pretty girl showering, romantic, leading to something even more romantic.

But now, he just felt like a bathhouse guard.

Inside the tent, water flowed softly.

Wade asked, “Is it dark in there? Can you see?”

“Getting darker.”

He fumbled, pulled a glow stick from his pack, tapped it twice on the tent frame, and slipped it under the door.

Kimberly caught it; her hand brushed his. He pulled back, noticing foam from shampoo on his wrist—pleasantly fragrant.

Squinting, Wade watched the tiny bubbles pop.

Suddenly, he asked, “Why didn’t you answer that call?”

She replied, “Negotiations need presence. I’m going to talk to Tiger Shark. Why waste time with his subordinates?”

“Isn’t it the same?”

“No. Better to let them think I’m difficult, troublesome, prickly, hard to deal with, than to let them think any of them can boss me around.”

She emerged from the tent, wrapped in the shawl, wet hair dripping.

“If I let them think I’m easy, they’ll just push Tiger Shark around. You have to focus on one person, understand him deeply, and force him to decide. Otherwise, his deputies and confidants all chime in with different opinions and decisions—negotiations become impossible.”

Like when the Saudis first came—Said and Yanus acting in tandem, speaking back and forth.

She smoked, lazily flicking the cigarette butt.

Then said, “Sorry, what did you say? Two people talking at once is confusing. Pick one who can speak effectively and repeat it to me.”

Said’s face flushed red instantly; Yanus’s eyes flashed with anger.

But Kimberly didn’t care.

With those only after money, why bother with friendship? Besides, she had already stopped weaving “friendship” ties long ago.

She looked down at Wade. “Are you going to shower?”

Wade propped himself up on his arms. “Yeah.”

After stretching, he quickly took down the tent.

“Not going to shower inside?”

He answered, “Men don’t need it to be complicated.”

Kimberly got into the car, crouching low, changing clothes behind the door’s cover, occasionally glancing at Wade showering.

Was his shower really that rough?

He wore only shorts, rinsed dates in a plastic bag with some water, scooped them, dunked his head in, shook it around, then repeated with shampoo, rinsed again, and was done.

His body routine was simpler—dust off sand with a towel, wet it, wipe, finished.

Watching, she thought he was like a child needing care—dressing, eating, sleeping, bathing, folding blankets, making beds.

Had anyone ever taken care of him like that?

As she stood, she accidentally knocked his bag, spilling a small notebook.

Was it for keeping accounts?

She picked it up. It was brand new, mostly blank except for a few words on the first page.

Strange…

Suddenly, her hand was empty.

Looking up, Wade held the notebook, asking sharply, “Why are you snooping?”

Kimberly said, “Is that snooping? Just a few words, nothing important.”

Wade grabbed his bag, shoved the notebook deep inside, as if to prevent her from taking it again. Then he pulled out a black T-shirt, stretched it on.

Kimberly was puzzled and curious, resting her arm on the window frame, chin in hand.

“You raise ladybugs?”

The first page read: Ladybug Life Observation Diary.

Wade paused, burying his face in the half-worn black T-shirt, murmuring.

“Why raise those… bugs?”

Ladybugs—tiny, with fine antennae—just thinking about them made her uneasy.

Wade pulled the T-shirt down with force, stretching it over his body. “A personal hobby.”

He walked around, sat in the driver’s seat, and closed the door.

The temperature was just right, headlights on. Time to hit the road.

“Are they easy to raise?”

“Not really. You need patience.”

The car started moving.

“What’s fun about raising ladybugs?”

He replied, “At first, you might dislike them, think they’re full of problems.”

“But after a while, you find them… quite charming. So you keep raising them.”
