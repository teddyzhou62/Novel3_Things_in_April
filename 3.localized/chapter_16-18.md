# Chapter 16

After enduring the announcements, boarding, the noisy crowd, flight reminders, takeoff, and the plane stabilizing, the cabin finally went dark to avoid disturbing the passengers.

The moment the lights went out, Wade let out a long sigh, feeling that only now had the world truly quieted down.

He lifted the window shade. Outside, it wasn’t pitch black; instead, a translucent midnight blue spread out, with clouds like torn wisps of thin cotton.

The plane felt like a ship drifting on another kind of “sea.”

He waited patiently for a while, letting his eyes adjust to the dim light inside. Kimberly was asleep, breathing lightly. As the employer and the one paying, she had every right to rest soundly.

But the bodyguard couldn’t relax; routine checks had to be done.

Wade unbuckled his seatbelt and stood up.

During boarding, he had observed most passengers and was fairly certain there were no problems. Still, to be safe, he needed to screen them once more.

He first approached the first-class flight attendant:

“I’m going to the rear cabin to see a friend. I’ll be back quickly. My girlfriend just had surgery. Could you watch over her for me? If anything happens, please call me immediately.”

The attendant smiled, a hint of envy in her tone: “You’re really good to your girlfriend.”

Wade smiled back: How could I not be? If something happened to her, not only would I lose the payment, but I’d also lose my ‘ace’ reputation.

He walked toward the rear cabin, checking business class first, then economy. Economy was large but not full; some passengers were still awake, with small reading lights on above them, flickering like scattered fireflies in the wild.

He quickly scanned the area and found nothing unusual. As he turned to return, he reached to lift the curtain separating the cabins when something gently nudged his foot.

Looking down, he saw a small rolling ball, still moving slightly.

From the dim first row seats, a young girl’s voice sounded: “Excuse me?”

Wade crouched, picked up the ball, and in the narrow strip of light from the window, he saw a small figure.

Ah, it was the little black girl with tiny dreadlocks he’d seen during boarding.

Sitting beside her was presumably her father, lost in thought until the noise pulled him back, looking somewhat dazed. Wade handed the ball to the girl, who took it, and the father finally came to his senses and thanked him.

At the same time, the girl handed him something:

“Thank you for picking up my ball.”

It was a piece of gum.

This exchange sparked the beginning of a friendship. Wade couldn’t just turn and leave, so he accepted the gum and asked her, “Where are you from?”

“Kalon.”

“Kalon?”

The father caught the surprise in Wade’s voice: “You’re thinking of the massacre, aren’t you?”

“Our Kalon isn’t that famous, unlike Sierra Leone with diamonds or Congo with gold. The only reason people know about Kalon is because of the ‘April Tragedy.’”

Wade thought for a few seconds before realizing what the ‘April Tragedy’ referred to.

“You call that massacre the ‘April Tragedy’?”

“Because it happened in April. Later, a local writer published a book called *April Tragedy*, which sold well, so everyone started calling it that.”

Hidden in the dim light, faces blurred and indistinct, Wade found himself unusually interested in Kalon, which gave the father a chance to open up.

- “When it happened, our family was on vacation abroad. But many relatives back home perished.”

- “We have since immigrated, but every year around this time, we return for the anniversary.”

- “Thinking about it makes it impossible to sleep...”

“Did some foreign volunteers help you then?”

“Yes, we’re very grateful. They truly risked their lives—remember, the militants even shot peacekeepers.”

Wade worried about Kimberly and didn’t want to prolong the conversation. He soon ended it.

Returning to his seat, everything was normal. The attendant stayed dutifully by Kimberly’s side and whispered to Wade as he approached, “Nothing's wrong; she’s sleeping well.”

Good.

Wade lay back, feeling his bones finally stretch after the journey. He pulled out the notebook from his back pocket and flipped through it quickly in the darkness; the scent of paper drifting near his nose.

What to write today?

Actually, Kimberly wasn’t bad as an employer. Compared to the arrogant, bloated, overbearing rich people he’d dealt with—those who thought cruelty was personality and wanted the whole world to bend to them—she was far above average.

In fact, he quite liked her character: decisive on big matters, casual on small ones.

Kimberly turned over.

— “They truly risked their lives back then—remember, the militants even shot peacekeepers...”

What kind of chaos was that? How did she survive? Wade couldn’t imagine. For most people in the world, war ended with World War II—the rest were just “conflicts” in the news, unrelated to them.

Her breathing grew heavier.

Wade frowned and listened carefully for a while before sitting up quickly and moving to her side, bending down.

Her hands occasionally jerked, grasping at nothing, and her eyes darted wildly beneath closed lids.

She was having a nightmare.

Wade whispered, “Ms. Lee?”

Calling twice, no response. He lowered his head, placed a hand on her shoulder, and gently shook her.

This time it worked. For a moment, he felt her body suddenly relax, then she opened her eyes.

Wade always thought there was a whole world hidden deep in her eyes.

Perhaps the haze of waking dropped her guard, or she was still trapped in the dream, forgetting who she was. At that moment, her eyes were bright, but her gaze was soft—like a newborn baby seeing the world, without love or anger.

She looked into Wade’s eyes.

Wade met her gaze.

They’d never held eye contact this long before.

Suddenly, the darkness of the cabin felt just right: no need to see her clothes, decorations, complexion, gestures, or microexpressions, sparing him from the clutter of distracting details.

He’d attended special training courses where they dissected how to observe targets’ clothing, habits, accessories, subtle mouth movements, eyelid contractions—down to every hair—to uncover their true nature.

Why had no one ever taught him to look into a person’s eyes?

Wade said softly, “You had a nightmare.”

She nodded.

“Want some water?”

She shook her head. “Got any wine?”

First class had red wine. Wade pressed the call button and ordered a glass. Kimberly took it and drank it like water, all at once.

The dim air filled with a faint scent of wine.

Wade smiled and sat down nearby. Sometimes, a nightmare was more exhausting than narrowly escaping death. At times like this, she might not want to move or be disturbed, but she definitely wouldn’t want to be alone.

The plane jolted slightly—probably hitting turbulence.

Kimberly asked, “Have you ever had nightmares?”

“Yes, often as a child.”

He squinted, staring at the seatbacks in front, as if looking through them into his early dreams.

“I’d dream that seawater flooded the deck and drowned me. I floated belly-up in the cabin like a fish, covered in moss.”

A cruel dream. Worse was waking up to a sewing machine’s clatter and biting into hard bread crust that could cut lips. Back then, he thought if he could survive, he’d surely make something of himself.

Now, that “something” was just mediocre.

He asked, “What about you? What did you dream?”

“Kalon.”

“After leaving Kalon, I saw a psychologist for a long time.”

Wade recalled what Reindeer had said:

— “Many who withdraw from war zones suffer severe psychological trauma.”

Bodies and minds were fragile; exposed to the sharpness and hardness of the world, they naturally got hurt. But the good news was, healing was possible.

Wade wanted to comfort her: “Back in the rear cabin, I met a Kalon family. They were very grateful to the volunteers who helped back then. Your choice was admirable.”

He admitted to himself he couldn’t have done the same.

Kimberly smiled—a low, cold laugh that soon slipped into something like disbelief, as if hearing a wild joke.

“You think I went to Kalon out of compassion, idealism, or to save those in dire straits?”

Not really... but hearing her tone, definitely not.

“I majored in International Political Relations in college, hoping to enter politics.”

“But as a person of color, it’s not easy. If I joined government, starting low, maybe by thirty or forty, I’d only be a senior assistant, secretary, or hold a nominal title.”

“I wanted a shortcut, an opportunity to add some glory to my resume. I chose the most dangerous place because I believed: the greater the danger, the greater the reward.”

She tilted her neck back, her gaze resting on the ceiling, laughing softly: “Turns out, I was unlucky. Or maybe I deserved it.”

Wade was silent.

She had said her train had long since derailed.

Reindeer also said that after Kalon, Kimberly completely withdrew from aid organizations in Africa.

Probably because severe psychological trauma shattered her carefully laid plans.

But that shouldn’t be called “deserved.”

Wade said, “Ms. Lee, I think the purpose behind actions can be complex.”

“Like studying—it can be for academics, degrees, easier jobs, or to make friends, or to escape society. Risking so much to go to Kalon, even if for personal gain, isn’t shameful.”

“Besides, you saved so many lives.”

...

After a long pause, Wade looked down: “Fallen asleep?”

No. She was looking at him, her expression complicated. As he lowered his head, she naturally reached up, wrapped her arms around his neck, and kissed him.

Soft, cool, with a sweet scent of wine.

Completely unexpected, a tingle ran from his wrist to the inside of his elbow.

Wade’s mind was clearer than ever. Holding her shoulder, he said, “Ms. Lee.”

She tilted her chin up, breath brushing his lips: “Hmm?”

“People’s willpower is weakest at night. You just drank and had a nightmare.”

“Think carefully if this is just an impulse seeking comfort—after all, we still have to see each other in daylight.”

After a brief silence, Kimberly looked into his eyes and said, “I don’t remember what just happened.”

Wade smiled.

“Me neither.”

Lying back down, Wade felt a twinge of regret.

If she weren’t a client, he probably wouldn’t have tried to be a gentleman.

After all, with the right time, place, people, and feeling, opportunities like this were rare in life.

# Chapter 17

After a long sleep, Wade woke just in time. After freshening up, the flight served meals: appetizer, main course, dessert, and thick soup—a dazzling spread.

Checking the flight info on the screen, they were just a knuckle’s distance from their connecting stop in Turkey. If the transfer went smoothly, they’d reach Khartoum before sunset.

He wondered what Africa would be like—would it be as in the movies, dry heat waves rolling over red earth, cradling a blood-red sunset?

He hadn’t talked much with Kimberly since boarding. At dinner, when she dropped her fork, Wade picked it up. She thanked him, and he replied casually.

The exchange was natural, not awkward. One benefit of maturity was seeing things more lightly—being able to pick up and put down with courtesy, unlike young lovers, where a change of heart could mean war.

They landed on schedule.

The second flight was delayed. Wade accompanied Kimberly through duty-free shops. Passing the airport bookstore, he noticed a magazine with a frowning Saudi man’s large face on the cover and a tiny oil tanker in the bottom right corner.

The headline read: *The Disappearing Oil Tanker—How to Break the Current Deadlock.*

Flipping through, it was a journalist’s interviews with international negotiation experts from various angles. Wade thought it might help Kimberly and bought a copy.

He found Kimberly browsing the latest fashion magazine, glossy and overflowing with jewels.

A quick glance caught a phrase: “This winter’s trending elements...”

The fashion world was baffling—still winter, already predicting what women would wear next winter.

Kimberly said, “This article says fashion is cyclical. This winter, modern plaids and leopard prints will be back. I wonder how designers will innovate on gowns.”

Such an odd focus—it was hard to believe she was here for negotiations.

Wade handed her the magazine: “You might need this.”

She glanced at the cover but didn’t take it: “Oh, that ship again.”

Wade chuckled: “You don’t seem interested in that tanker at all.”

“Not a big deal.”

Not a big deal? The news, TV, and papers were all abuzz. The Saudis had paid a huge sum for her to come here.

And she said it wasn’t a big deal.

Wade smiled: “Looks like you’re confident. You and Tiger Shark... close?”

“Not really.” She ran a finger along a row of magazines’ spines, then picked another up. “When the rebels shot refugees, we treated dozens of seriously injured in the local hospital. I was busy coordinating medical resources and writing loss and situation reports—no time to make friends with the wounded.”

“But I remember Tiger Shark. He had neck injuries, head and shoulders wrapped in bandages, lying in a hallway corner like a mummy. He only said one thing to me—when I checked on patients, he said, ‘Thank you.’”

With such limited connection, how could the ransom be cut by so much? Besides, friendship rarely translated well into money.

“What do you consider important then?”

Kimberly smiled: “You’ll know someday... if you get the chance.”

Wade smiled too, then shifted tone: “Why did you choose me?”

“Hm?”

“You know I’d ask. In that interview, I wasn’t the best candidate by any measure.”

“Don’t say it’s because we’re both Chinese and communication’s easier—I’m not that stupid.”

A brief silence. The airport announcement called their Khartoum flight.

Kimberly said, “Time to board.”

As they brushed past, she gently pulled the magazine from his hand and smiled warmly: “Because we’re both Chinese, communication’s easier.”

Wade’s face darkened. Suddenly, he grabbed her waist firmly and pushed her in. She lost balance, stumbling into him.

His body was rigid.

Kimberly quickly steadied herself and looked up at him.

Only now did he notice his eyes could strip away charm and warmth—when he looked at her, it was like staring at a lifeless body in a smuggler’s boat.

He said, “Ms. Lee, I know you’re a master planner. But don’t include me in your schemes or try to use me—otherwise, I won’t let you off.”

Kimberly smiled: “Then don’t.”

She leaned close to his ear, voice low as breath, warm and teasing, reminding him of the two sluggish jellyfish in Erinn’s aquarium.

“Lots of people don’t let me off. Want to get in line?”

She flicked dust off his shoulder, as if brushing off a speck.

“Fighting people takes energy. We have no real conflicts—I suggest we get along.”

“That day in the greenhouse, after you bargained with the white robe, didn’t you also say you wanted to get along?”

He remembered the interview—those two exchanged polite, friendly looks.

“Once things are settled, we can be friends and get along. If conflicts come up later, we can fight then.”

Wade stayed silent. After a moment, the cold edge in his eyes faded, replaced by familiar charm, politeness, cooperation, even liking.

“Alright, let’s get along.”

Because of the delay, they missed the imagined blood-red sunset.

By arrival, the sun had almost fully set. Night spread like an inverted pot, with a thin sliver of light where the lid didn’t fit tightly. The plane squeezed through that light, landing on the heat-steamed East African land.

When the cabin door opened, Wade felt like he was back in a Helsinki sauna.

It was April; daytime temperatures hovered around 40°C, with surface heat reaching 70°C.

Entering the airport hall, they shed all outerwear. Sweat stuck to their backs between clothes and skin, heat wrapping around them.

The capital’s airport hall was barely the size of a small town bus station, poorly managed, and full of complicated characters.

When Kimberly went to change in the restroom, Wade had to guard the door outside, enduring many glaring stares from local women.

She came out quickly: black tank top, light gray plaid shirt tied at the hem, denim shorts, hair loosely tied up with wisps stuck to her sweaty neck. She fanned herself with the magazine.

Wade said, “Once we see CoCo and get settled, it’ll be fine.”

Kimberly flicked the magazine loudly: “I suggest you not be too optimistic.”

At the exit, Wade spotted CoCo waiting.

Some people were naturally conspicuous, like deities surrounded by mortals. Amid a sea of brightly colored pants, sweatshirts lifted to fan themselves, and traditional Arab clothes, no one could miss CoCo unless they were blind.

He wore a suit, tie, polished black shoes, white shirt cuffs with cufflinks carefully exposed, and a gleaming Jaeger-LeCoultre watch on his wrist.

Wade deliberately delayed, wanting to see if CoCo would succumb to the heat.

But CoCo had already spotted him, grinning widely and shouting, “Wade! My Christmas tree!”

Still, Wade didn’t move. Kimberly nudged him from behind: “Christmas tree, he’s calling you.”

CoCo was mixed-race, pale-skinned with typical black curls. His father was probably some Western playboy journalist who had a brief affair with a black woman, producing CoCo, who was then abandoned in the gold-mining woods.

He grew up mining, cooking, scouting, then as a mercenary, until Reindeer’s endless chatter moved him to become a full-time bodyguard.

At their first meeting, CoCo told Wade: “You know, before I was eight, I never wore underwear! My first pair came from a drunk old man—I swear it was stinky! I washed it by the river and vowed to wear only the best, most expensive clothes from then on!”

Such sincerity on first meeting made Wade his friend.

CoCo lived that vow:

- Food and tools could be shoddy, but clothes had to be branded, top-notch, and envy-inducing.

- When meeting strangers, dress to flaunt wealth and status.

- When reuniting with old friends, dress to show how well you’ve done.

Wade approached.

They glanced at each other for several seconds, laughed almost simultaneously, then bumped fists and patted each other’s shoulders hard.

CoCo greeted Kimberly warmly: “Hello!”

Wade asked, “How’s the situation here?”

“Terrible. Worse in the south—probably going to war. I protect people there, many military and political figures with bodyguards...”

Wasn’t it said, “South is fighting, north is singing”? Wade thought they wouldn’t go south.

“Forget the south, what about here?”

“Bad too. A few days ago, a Spanish diplomat was stabbed to death in his apartment. Earlier, seven Asian engineering workers were kidnapped; negotiations failed, government troops clashed with rebels, rescue failed, three hostages died. A few months ago, a plane crashed at this airport...”

Wade interrupted, tugging his collar, feeling even more frustrated.

Damn frustrating.

CoCo looked at him, then suddenly burst out laughing, showing uneven white teeth.

“Wade! I was just messing with you!”

“What are you scared of? The worse the place, the better for us.”

“Those kidnappings and murders are political—no one targets small guys like you.”

Wade ignored him. CoCo was the type to treat bullets flying around like sound effects.

“Did you drive here? Parked outside?”

“Yes. But the car had problems.”

CoCo explained he had a decent off-road driver, but the car was requisitioned, so he had to borrow a simpler one in Khartoum.

“Does the car have air conditioning?”

As long as it cooled him down, simple was fine.

“No, but it has ventilation.”

Sounds good. Wade agreed: “Let’s go.”

Five minutes later, outside the airport on dusty dirt, Wade saw the simple vehicle.

A tuk-tuk—domestically called an electric tricycle.

No roof; the top was a piece of cardboard propped behind the seat. No doors on the sides, allowing natural ventilation.
