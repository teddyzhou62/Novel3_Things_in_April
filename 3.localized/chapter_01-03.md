# Chapter 1

The moment Wade woke from the cold, a thought flashed through his mind: Enough is enough—I’m heading south today!

He had spent four months inside the Arctic Circle. By then, he had retreated from the Arctic Ocean’s edge into the dense forests of Lapland, curling up inside an abandoned kota—a tent used by the indigenous Sami people. The kota resembled a Native American tipi, a conical peak with layers of reindeer and bear pelts, plus felt blankets wrapped tightly around the sides to keep out the cold. Wrapped in animal skins, Wade lay on a half-foot-thick bed of ashes. He had lit a campfire before sleeping, so there was still some warmth when he lay down. Now, reaching out to touch the ashes, he found them cold and biting, like snapping jaws. Suddenly, the chill bit into half his arm, numb and biting.

It was time to head south. Four months alone, especially the last half, with barely a soul in sight—less than a handful of people. They say long-term isolation in extreme environments causes hallucinations. Just yesterday, he was sure he saw a reindeer sitting cross-legged, applying bright red Chanel lipstick—shade 99, pure red. After applying it, the reindeer turned to him, puckering its lips as if asking for a kiss.

Wade even critiqued its makeup: “You should line your lips.”

Then he crouched down, head in hands. If he didn’t leave soon, he’d probably lose his mind.

He tightened his animal skins and crawled out of the kota. The night’s blizzard had passed, and now everything was eerily silent. A ghostly green aurora twisted sinuously through the orange-red sky. Towering red pines were bent low and heavy under layers of ice and snow, their bulky forms like giants, spirits, and rows of white bones stretching to the horizon.

The Sami believe a fire fox runs through the night sky, its tail striking snowflakes, creating the aurora.

To the Chinese, such celestial colors are auspicious omens.

Chinese culture cherishes auspicious timing. Whether arranging marriages or building houses, they pick the perfect day. The day Wade chose to head south was filled with these auspicious signs—a good omen.

——

Wading through knee-deep snow, Wade trekked south, eventually leaving the Lapland forest. When luck smiled, he caught a ride on a husky-pulled sled.

Relieved not to die in the snow, his biological clock fell out of sync. His mind was foggy, like a woman who’s just given birth and is scatterbrained for three years. His meals alternated between rough pizza, stale Italian dishes, reindeer meat, and ice-cold beer. When he finally reached Helsinki, the capital, he clearly remembered only two things.

First, passing through Rovaniemi’s Santa Claus Village, he bowed to the Arctic Circle marker and said goodbye. Tourists hiding nearby whispered he was a wild man.

Second, he hitched a ride on a massive timber truck loaded with Norwegian spruce. Finland, a country of five million lumberjacks, often sees such trucks. The cab was full, so Wade wrapped himself in skins and climbed into the truck bed. Surrounded by the sharp scent of fresh-cut wood, he lay down. Late at night, the driver came back, tapping him, saying they could only go so far. Wade heard but was too tired to open his eyes or move. Mumbled, “Just leave me here.”

The driver had no choice. With a nod to his companion, they hoisted Wade out like a lifeless sack and dumped him by the roadside. Half his face pressed to the mud, he slept until dawn.

But when he saw Helsinki’s pale Lutheran church from afar, his blood surged anew.

His senses sharpened—eyes clear, mind alert, nose catching the scent of freshly baked burgers from afar. His blood boiled like water in a sauna.

He was home.

Some hated the city, calling it cold and dim, like “the Soviet Union before opening up.” Others loved it, seeing pastoral charm in this Baltic-encircled city.

It was late March, and Helsinki still clung to winter’s tail—chilly and gloomy. Wade wrapped his filthy animal skins tighter, passed concrete apartment blocks, dusty storefronts, adult shops, and Thai massage parlors.

The streets were empty; no one stared. He walked straight into a basement bar run by Erlen.

——

The bar’s name was *We Care About the World*.

An all-English name—no Finnish or Swedish in sight. Faces from all corners of the globe came and went, with many discreet deals made in the shadows. Reindeer said this bar was a vortex on Helsinki’s skin; the uninitiated avoided it, while those in the know naturally entered.

Wade pushed open the door.

By day, the bar had no customers, lit only by a wall lamp casting dim light on a miniature jellyfish tank at the bar. Inside floated two translucent moon jellyfish, glowing green under the light, their long tentacles drifting like phosphorescent ghosts.

Behind the tank, a face distorted by water, light, and glass looked up in surprise.

It was Erlen.

Erlen was a young German woman with fiery red hair, resembling the lead in the famous German film *Run Lola Run*. Around her neck was a thin tattoo of a king cobra coiled once, its tongue flicking at her throat. When she spoke, it seemed the snake hissed along.

But beneath her aggressive exterior, Erlen was surprisingly gentle.

She eyed Wade with suspicion and caution, one hand reaching under the bar for a Russian Makarov pistol.

Wade knew she hadn’t recognized him or had mistaken him for a homeless vagrant. His hair was a tangled mess, his beard untrimmed for days, merging like two thickets on his face. Dirt stains were etched into his skin, impossible to wash off. His clothes were mismatched, the rancid smell of animal skins mixed with blood reminding him of the raw, primal days when fire was too risky.

His Adam’s apple bobbed as he said, “It’s me.”

Erlen’s eyes widened. “David’s coming?”

——

Wade was his Chinese name; his English name was David. His agent, Reindeer, loved China passionately. After studying his name, Reindeer said that in Chinese, “来” means “come.” When they say “David’s coming,” they’re not just stating a fact—they’re calling his full Chinese name.

So Erlen was calling his name.

Wade nodded. “The key.”

His apartment was Reindeer’s property, on the top floor of this building. Usually, he left the key with Erlen for safekeeping—just safekeeping. She never tidied or cleaned, despite claiming to love him.

Erlen, still stunned, pinched the key between two fingers and handed it over. When Wade leaned in, her face twisted with complex disdain, as if afraid to be touched, practically tossing the key at him.

Wade caught it.

Erlen said, “How did you get like this?”

Wade replied, “You’d be the same if you spent four months up north.”

That wasn’t true—Erlen wouldn’t last four days there.

He turned and left. The building’s interior was less bitter than outside. As he walked, he shed his animal skins.

Erlen called after him, “Wade!”

He looked back. She approached but recoiled from the smell. Her expression was serious, almost angry.

“Wade, you better get back to your old self. You know I love you—mainly because of your handsome face and body…”

She hesitated on “handsome,” feeling those words were an insult to the face before her.

“…But I can’t love you like this.”

——

The elevator was at the end of a narrow hallway, passing the security office. The building had only one guard, a German named Mark. Bald and enormously fat, he could barely squeeze through the door. Most of the time, he sat behind the glass window, either napping or eating.

Wade passed by as Mark sharpened his fork on a plate of Bavarian white sausage. Wade sensed a dark shape pass the window and, out of duty, greeted without looking up, “Moi!”

The slurred greeting, full of spittle, landed on the sausage.

Wade figured that whether it was a murderer, a brown bear, an alien, or a ghost passing by, Mark wouldn’t notice. He was just part of the furniture, a psychological comfort for residents.

In his long security career, Mark had only “stood up” once.

On Christmas night, two men murdered someone on the third floor. They didn’t care; they poured beer on the corpse and carried it out like a drunken friend. The body wore one shoe; the other foot scraped the floor, leaving a bloody trail mixed with beer.

Mark wasn’t so fat then. Seeing people approach, wanting a festive mood, he suddenly stuck his head out and shouted, “Merry Christmas!”

His Christmas gift: one of the murderers stabbed him.

That wound extended his contract because Mark claimed he bravely confronted the killer to protect tenants.

He could say whatever he wanted—the killer was never caught.

The elevator was old and narrow, with a manual wire gate. In the corner lay a rolled-up newspaper, trampled many times, revealing bold black letters with an exclamation mark:

**Ransom!**

Probably another robbery.

After four months without news, the world had likely lost many lives, gained new ones, and seen money flow from some hands to others.

Under the sun, nothing was new.

# Chapter 2

The door opened, releasing the scent of abandonment.

Wade never decorated his apartment. Only the essentials were inside, enough to live without attachment. As he put it, he wouldn’t feel sad leaving, nor would he miss it.

Who would miss an almost empty home?

He closed the door, stripped off his layers, leaving them piled on the floor—once his second skin, now limp and discarded like trash no vagrant would pick up.

In the bathroom, he turned on the shower. The pipes buzzed first, like a stomach upset, then hot water gushed out.

Pure bliss. The last time he bathed was in an icy lake.

His first layer of shaving foam didn’t lather. Black water ran down his cheeks and chin. Looking down, dirty streaks spread over his body, swirling into the drain.

Shaving and trimming his hair with clippers, black mud had embedded itself into his skin’s creases. Only a brush dipped in soap could scrub it away. The water flowed continuously, and only after three washes did the grime fade. He wondered how he endured it.

Then he remembered—he didn’t have a choice in that environment.

Turning off the water, the bathroom grew quiet. Steam dissipated, and despite the heater, cold wrapped around him instantly. Wade wrapped a towel around his waist and approached the mirror, wiping away the fog.

A man’s face stared back—sharp features, a chin tinged blue from shaving, bare shoulders and neck, muscles firm and hard.

His eyes were cold, likely from the cold itself.

But they were bright, clear—not clouded. Fish eyes are clear, maybe from all the fish he’d eaten from the icy lake.

His thin lips pressed tight. They say thin-lipped men are heartless—not quite true. He wasn’t very warm to anything, just not deeply attached.

He had to admit—he looked better now. Erlen might fall for him again.

——

Wade bagged his discarded clothes and tossed them down the garbage chute. As the door slammed shut, he pressed his ear to the wall, hearing the muffled thud of trash falling.

It was like a declaration—everything marked or recorded washed away or thrown out. A chapter closed.

Back in his room, he pulled the curtain and lay down. The moment he hit the bed, his phone buzzed. A message from Reindeer:

*“10:30 p.m. tomorrow, the usual place.”*

He replied “Okay,” as if Reindeer could hear him, then turned off his phone. His eyelids grew heavy, and he sank into a sweet, heavy sleep.

Outside, Helsinki was caught in another freezing rain.

He slept over 24 hours. When he awoke, dusk hung over the city, with only some white edges uncovered.

Wade pulled down the aluminum folding ladder attached to the ceiling window and climbed to the attic. Dust coated the floor, along with the empty beer can he’d finished before leaving last time. The attic’s skylight was double-paned for insulation and soundproofing. He pushed it open and climbed onto the sloped roof.

The city’s noise rolled in like a tidal wave. Wade took a few steps on the shingles, then sat down on the cold, damp slope and lit a cigarette.

Looking down, Helsinki resembled a steaming pot with its lid just lifted, bustling with life.

Wade had his own definition of “life energy”: most people are under two meters tall. People emit smells and breaths, speak, fight, express feelings, go hysterical, or fight for life—all these require energy. All that energy mingles, flows, boils, and churns roughly two meters above ground. So the atmosphere should be divided into layers: ground air, human air, and pure air.

Reindeer and CoCo had joined him on rooftops to observe “human air” and asked the same question:

—What can you see?

Wade answered, “Lots of stories—happening, brewing, fading.”

CoCo said, “Nonsense.”

Reindeer said, “You Chinese are just so amazing.”

Night fell. To the northeast, the giant globe lamp held by the statue at Helsinki Central Station lit up. Wade stubbed out his cigarette, climbed back inside.

——

He pushed open the bar door again at 9 p.m. Death metal band *Killing Me Killing You* played inside. An old man rolled a joint in the corner; a young man nearby waited eagerly, eyes burning.

Wade walked straight to Erlen at the bar.

Sure enough, her eyes lit up with surprise and delight. Her smile was wide, and her call of “Wade” was full of affection, softening even the king cobra tattoo on her neck into something tender.

Wade dragged a bar stool and sat, pulling his wallet from his pocket. “Croissant, ice beer, vodka, red wine.”

Erlen poured the beer first. As she slid it over, Wade shook his wallet upside down hard—

Only one coin fell out, rolling straight across the bar, hitting the jellyfish tank, and flipping over in defeat.

A 50 Euro cent coin, worth less than four Chinese yuan.

Erlen’s guard rose; she paused mid-pour.

Wade said, “On credit.”

“Where’s your money?”

“Spent it.”

“So much money!”

“Spent it.” Wade listed his expenses. “I hired an icebreaker to crack open the frozen harbor. It was spectacular, like a giant wedge in the Arctic Ocean. I even took pictures to show you, but at minus thirty degrees, my camera froze.”

He smiled and patted Erlen’s hand. “Don’t you love me? Cut me some slack.”

Erlen was principled. “Loving you is one thing. Money is another.”

Wade thought Chinese lovers were better—love so intense it ripped out your heart and liver. Gritting his teeth, he said, “I don’t see where your love is.”

——

When Wade first met Erlen, she hadn’t opened the bar yet. The first thing she said to him was: “Are you Japanese?”

He remembered his face darkened; after a pause, he said, “Chinese.”

China? Where’s that? Her world map only included Germany, the Nordics, and the surrounding seas. She only knew yellow-skinned people as Japanese or Native Americans.

To understand Wade better, she asked a music store owner that night, “Any famous Chinese movies? Something recent?”

The owner rummaged through a box and handed her a DVD, confidently saying, “This one, very famous.”

It was Zhang Yimou’s film *Not One Less*, about rural poverty and illiteracy. It had won many European awards.

Erlen watched it twice, thinking she understood China well enough. The next day, she asked Wade, “How many mountains did you have to cross to get to school?”

He was smoking, silent for a long moment. The cigarette butt fell into his beer, ashes scattering.

Then he looked at her and said slowly, “You really should watch the news. Care about the world.”

——

Erlen agreed to extend credit for two reasons.

One, Wade had good credit—never really owed money.

Two, he said he’d be working that night.

Work meant money. The last job brought a fat wad of cash, but after an expensive, twisted Arctic vacation, he was broke again.

This wasn’t a normal lifestyle, and Erlen worried. She watched him through the smoky bar, determined to find a chance to talk.

Wade grabbed a croissant, dipped it in salt on his plate, and popped it in his mouth. A seductive woman in a tight black skirt and heavy smoky gold eye makeup—like Cleopatra—sidled up.

Her voice was husky and sexy. “Won’t you buy me a drink?”

Wade said, “Sure.”

Cleopatra smiled, swayed her hips, and slid into his lap. The lace neckline plunged low, revealing a creamy cleavage that caught his eye like the wedge cracked open by the icebreaker.

She looped her arms around his neck, red lips near his face. Just as she was about to kiss him, Wade stopped her. “Wait. Are you wearing Chanel lipstick?”

Shade 99, pure red. How was it so much like the reindeer’s lips he saw in the Lapland forest?

……

Erlen watched coldly, expecting them to leave together. Five minutes later, Cleopatra left with a drink, hunting a new target.

Erlen smiled secretly and brought over a slice of cheesecake. “On the house.”

“Not interested?”

Wade said, “I’m out of sorts.”

Erlen leaned in, curious.

He lowered his voice. “I froze pretty bad up north. With a woman like that in my arms, I barely react. I need to get used to it again.”

The ancestors were right: when well-fed and warm, lust returns. After four months of hunger and cold, he barely thought about women. Cleopatra’s kind only sparked images of Finnish landscapes in his head.

Erlen snapped, “Maybe you’re frozen to death.”

Wade wiped the last salt off his plate with his croissant. “What good would that do you?”

Before she could answer, the clock on the wall struck ten. Three LCD screens flickered on around the bar.

——

The bar’s name, *We Care About the World*, was no accident. Every night at ten, world news was broadcast.

Regulars knew the rule and complied. Whether chasing women or drowning sorrows, all stopped to watch the news.

Most probably never watched news outside the bar.

Wade savored it. After four months without news, each headline was a lifeline pumping reality into his parched veins.

Japan earthquake, Indonesian volcano spewing smoke, U.S. school shootings, Chechen terrorist leader killed by Russia…

Another story came on.

“Today marks the seventh day since the Saudi oil tanker Sirius was hijacked by Somali pirates. The 25 hostages remain unaccounted for. Sources reveal pirates demand a $20 million ransom…”

Twenty million dollars!

Wade couldn’t help but think of his 0.5 euro coin.

Really… maybe he should become a pirate.

# Chapter 3

Approaching the appointed time, Wade left the bar. Erlen caught up with him in the dim hallway.

“Wade.”

She was different—no teasing, no anger, just serious, with a trace of helplessness and sadness.

“You can’t go on like this.”

Women are born persuaders. Her youthful face spoke with the wisdom of a century.

“Don’t you have plans? Save money, marry a girl you like, buy a big house, live steadily. I want to see you well. After all, you’re the only man I’ve ever loved.”

Erlen spoke honestly. Somewhere in her love for Wade, she had a revelation: she actually liked women. No trigger, no build-up—just late awakening.

Wade pondered. To end a topic firmly, one must be sincere.

He replied, “I know diligence and stability are universal values, but the world is vast. You have to allow some to go off track.”

He stepped back, bowed politely, and turned away.

Even strangers who care deserve gratitude.

He had no plans, lived day to day, scattered riches and regained them, happily off the rails, not wanting to disturb those who lived seriously on track.

——

Outside the apartment building, he walked straight down the street, turned left at the end. The pavement gleamed under streetlights. A battered Volkswagen stood under a lamp post.

Reindeer waited by the car, eyes bright, almost lunging: “David’s coming! My Christmas tree!”

“Christmas tree” was Wade’s nickname.

Wade strode over, grabbed Reindeer’s head mid-approach, spun him around, then slipped into the passenger seat.

The car was warm—perfect for talking or napping.

Reindeer climbed in excitedly.

“Wade! You’re back safe! God knows, I watched *Into the Wild* three times! One night, I dreamed you died—I cried like crazy! I swear, I wasn’t that upset when Eve died!”

Wade had no reply. Eve was Reindeer’s wife, mother of their two kids. But that wasn’t the point. Eve was alive and healthy, likely to live another 30 or 40 years.

——

Reindeer was Wade’s agent.

A 35-year-old African American with a rapper’s look—talkative, tireless, and wildly in love with China. He claimed Chinese dumplings were the most delicious food because dumplings could have ten thousand flavors!

His language skills were good, especially his Chinese, which he studied hard recently. Wade rarely spoke Chinese, but with Reindeer, they often mixed English and Chinese, with Reindeer throwing in slang phrases—sometimes correctly, sometimes not, but always warmly familiar.

Once he asked Wade, “Chinese say, ‘Nothing tastes better than dumplings, nothing’s more fun than sister-in-law.’ I get dumplings, but why sister-in-law is fun?”

Wade was silent for a moment. “You dirty old man.”

Another time, “You seem to dislike ‘brother-in-law loves aunt,’ but brother-in-law and aunt are family. Shouldn’t they love each other?”

Wade was silent again. “You dirty old man.”

…

Reindeer’s Chinese and understanding grew strong amid Wade’s curses.

After four months apart, Reindeer’s care hit like Lapland’s sudden snowstorm, relentless. Wade ignored his rambling, eyes on the brown paper envelope standing on the dashboard.

“Client info?”

Reindeer kept client files in leather envelopes with string clasps.

Wade reached for it, but Reindeer stopped him. “No, this one.”

He pulled another envelope from under the seat, handing it over solemnly. It looked the same but felt thicker—like a photo.

Wade didn’t open it immediately. “Chosen especially for me?”

“I know you Chinese. Fellow countrymen shed tears when they meet.”

Got it. The client was probably Chinese or at least Chinese descent.

Wade untied the string. “You don’t know us well. We have a saying: ‘killing the familiar’—people exploit their own without mercy.”

He pulled out the photo.

The dim car light made it hard to see, but when he drew it out, Wade felt a flicker of brightness.

He said, “Beautiful.”

The photo showed a 26 or 27-year-old Chinese woman smoking on stairs. Her shoulder-length hair curled at the ends. Her expression was blank, eyes meeting the camera’s gaze.

Her eyes hid a world of depth.

Two characters were written in marker on the blank space: *Kimberly*.

Reindeer glanced sideways. “Be careful. Men first fall for a dimple, then bring the whole woman home.”

Wade stared at the photo. “You underestimate me. First, she’s not pretty enough to distract me; second, I have professional ethics. Once I take a case, she’s a client—I don’t mix business with anything else.”

He paused, then said, “Her gaze isn’t soft. She’s been through some things.”

He propped Kimberly’s photo on the dashboard.

Streetlights outside spilled in, dimming the woman’s face. Wade asked, “What’s Miss Kimberly like?”

——

Reindeer was one of the most influential private security agents, with two top operatives: Christmas Tree and CoCo.

Top agents could pick clients and set personal rules, no matter how bizarre—for example, CoCo refused any client with a mole between their hairline and navel.

Weird. Who cares about moles?

Compared to CoCo, Wade was easier—one rule: no protecting scumbags.

Why? Because sweating, bleeding, even dying to protect scum was defying heaven, against Chinese respect for the natural order.

Everything Chinese was good, Reindeer nodded fervently.

Now Wade asked about Kimberly’s character, signaling interest in taking the case.

Reindeer had prepared: “Wade, people are complicated. Do you want to hear the good or the bad first?”

“The bad.”

“Be patient. No matter what you hear, you’ll definitely take the case.”

Wade smiled.

Why definitely? Love isn’t eternal, passion fades, nothing is absolute.

The street outside was deserted. No one passed by for a long time.

“Miss Kimberly had a fiancé. On the eve of their wedding, she was caught cheating in a hotel. The wedding was called off. Her fiancé tried to overdose but was saved in time.”

That was private. Wade didn’t comment. Compared to Kimberly, he thought less of the fiancé: a real man wouldn’t worry about losing a woman like that. Better to leave her.

Reindeer brightened. “But God is fair. The fiancé met someone new in the hospital and married the next year. At the ceremony, he said he thanked God for not dying for the wrong person, so he could find true love.”

He handed over a photo: a tall, handsome, scholarly-looking Chinese man embracing a delicate woman, love in their eyes.

Wade gestured for more.

“Miss Kimberly is also a suspect in a murder case.”

Reindeer paused, baiting him to ask. Wade remained calm.

Reindeer continued, “Luckily, the evidence was insufficient, and she was cleared quickly.”

“What case?”

“A French tycoon died from a toxin injection. The safe was open, but it’s unclear what was stolen. Police suspect robbery and murder. Kimberly was involved only because she was one of the visitors that night.”

The phrase “only because” showed Reindeer’s effort to clean up Kimberly’s bad reputation—any stain was an accident.

Wade was more curious about the toxin: “What poison?”

“Pufferfish toxin.”

Wade was surprised.

Reindeer misunderstood. “I thought it was expensive. Pure pufferfish toxin goes for over $200,000 per gram internationally. Ordinary poisons are just as deadly. Why bother?”

Wade said, “Because it’s poison.”

Tetrodotoxin (TTX) is over 1,200 times more toxic than cyanide. It causes paralysis of nerves and reflexes, eventually paralyzing breathing muscles to death. Worse, TTX can’t cross the blood-brain barrier, so victims remain conscious, unable to speak or move, fully aware of their impending death.

Conscious until the end. Horrifying.

Kimberly probably had other “issues,” but Reindeer dismissed them as human flaws, nothing worth mentioning.

He was eager to highlight her bright side.

“Miss Kimberly was once part of an international aid group in Africa. During the Somali warlord conflicts, she helped the UN distribute food to refugees. Later, she went to Caron, which soon experienced a shocking genocide.”

Wade frowned. Caron genocide? He’d heard of it.

Reindeer sneered. “You don’t care. African wars, famines, conflicts, or massacres feel like alien matters to you.”

Maybe because he was black, Reindeer’s tone grew passionate.

Wade recalled that Caron was tiny—under 20,000 square kilometers—one of Africa’s smallest but most densely populated countries. Divided between the Huka and Kava tribes, facing frequent ethnic conflicts and past civil war.

“Was it the Caron genocide classified as a crime against humanity? That was six years ago, right? The UN even set a memorial day.”

Reindeer ground his teeth. “That’s the one. The UN did nothing. Western countries collectively turned blind. The media called it tribal conflict. The world abandoned Caron. In two months, over 200,000 Kava were killed. Only a few aid groups dared help refugees—Red Cross, Doctors Without Borders…”

Wade’s interest peaked. “Kimberly… didn’t leave?”

Reindeer nodded. “She stayed with a few volunteers, setting up a humanitarian safe zone in a small school. They confronted Huka militants for over a month, protecting 175 Kava lives. When she left, the president awarded her the National Friendship Medal.”

Wade sat straighter, shedding his loose demeanor.

He’d protected all sorts—industry leaders, heroes, fighters—but Kimberly’s background was the first of its kind.

“She needs protection?”

“Two days ago, she received a… dead person’s hand.”
