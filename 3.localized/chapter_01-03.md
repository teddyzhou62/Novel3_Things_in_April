# Chapter 1

The moment Wade woke up freezing, a thought flashed through his mind: "Enough is enough. Today, I’m heading south!"

This marked his fourth month inside the Arctic Circle. By then, he had retreated from the Arctic Ocean's edge into the dense forests of Lapland, curling up inside an abandoned kota—a traditional Sami tent. The kota resembled a Native American tipi, with a pointed cone shape. Around it were layers of tightly sewn reindeer hides, bear pelts, and felt to keep out the cold. Wrapped in animal skins, Wade lay on a half-foot-thick bed of ash. Before sleeping, he had burned a campfire, so there was still some warmth. But now, when he reached out, the ash was icy and biting, cold enough to make his arm numb.

It was time to head south. After four months—especially the last half—he’d seen fewer than ten people. They say those who live alone in extreme environments long enough start seeing things. Just yesterday, Wade was certain he saw a reindeer sitting cross-legged on the ground, applying lipstick. The brand was Chanel, shade 99—a bold red. After putting it on, the reindeer turned to him, puckered its lips as if asking for a kiss.

Wade actually critiqued its makeup: “You should line your lips.”

Then he crouched, holding his head. If he didn’t leave soon, his mind would crack.

He tightened his animal skins and crawled out of the kota. After a night of wind and snow, the world was eerily silent. A ghostly green aurora twisted through the sky, weaving into an orange-red horizon. Tall red pines, weighed down by layers of ice and snow, bowed their heads and bent their trunks. They looked swollen, like giants or spirits, rows of white bones stretching to the end of the earth.

The Sami believe a fire fox runs across the night sky, swatting snowflakes with its tail to create the northern lights.

For the Chinese, a sky ablaze with colors signifies auspicious omens.

The Chinese are particular about timing; they choose lucky days for everything—engagements, weddings, building homes. The day Wade decided to head south was filled with auspicious signs—a good omen.

---

Wading through knee-deep snow, Wade trekked south, leaving the Lapland forest behind on foot. When lucky, he caught rides on husky sleds.

Relieved to no longer risk dying on the snowfields, his biological clock went haywire. His mind was a haze, like a woman who’s given birth and lost three years of clarity. His speech and actions were foggy. Meals rotated between rough pizza, stale Italian dishes, reindeer meat, and ice-cold beer.

When he finally returned to Helsinki, the capital, only two things remained clear in his memory.

First, passing through Rovaniemi’s Santa Claus Village, he bowed to the Arctic Circle marker pole and whispered goodbye. Tourists hid nearby, spying on him, and someone muttered he looked like a wild man.

Second, he hitched a ride on a heavy timber truck loaded with Norwegian spruce. Finland, known as the land of five million loggers, had plenty of these trucks. The cabin was full, so Wade, wrapped in animal skins, climbed into the truck bed. Surrounded by the sharp scent of fresh wood, he lay down. Around midnight, the driver came back, patting him and saying they could only go so far. Wade heard but was too tired to open his eyes or get up. He mumbled, “Just leave me here.”

The driver had no choice. With help, they tossed him onto the roadside like a discarded body. Half his face pressed into the mud, he slept until dawn.

But upon seeing Helsinki’s pale Lutheran church on a hill, Wade felt his blood revive.

His hearing sharpened, sight cleared, thoughts quickened, and his nose caught the distant scent of freshly baked burgers. His blood boiled like the sauna’s hot water nearby.

He was back home.

Some hated this place, calling it cold and gloomy—like the Soviet Union before its opening. Others loved it, saying this city, cradled by the Baltic Sea, had a rustic charm.

It was late March; Helsinki was still clinging to winter’s tail, cold and dim. Wade wrapped his ragged, dirty animal skin tighter and walked past concrete apartment blocks, dusty shop windows, adult stores, and Thai massage parlors.

The streets were empty; no one gawked at him. He walked straight into a basement bar run by Erinn.

---

The bar was called *We Care About the World*.

The name was all in English, with no Finnish or Swedish signage. Faces from all over the world passed through here, and many transactions, some overt, some hidden, took place. Reindeer said this bar was a whirlpool on Helsinki’s surface—those who didn’t understand should avoid it, but those who did naturally came in.

Wade pushed open the door.

During the day, the bar was quiet, lit only by a wall lamp casting a dim glow over a mini jellyfish tank on the bar. Inside floated two translucent moon jellyfish, glowing faintly green, their long tentacles drifting like phosphorescent ghosts.

Behind the tank was a face distorted by water, light, and glass. The woman looked up, surprised to see Wade.

It was Erinn.

Erinn was a young German woman with a head of red hair, resembling the heroine from the famous German film *Run Lola Run*. Around her neck was a thin tattoo of a king cobra coiling once, its forked tongue flicking near her throat with every word she spoke.

But beneath her fierce exterior, Erinn was surprisingly gentle.

She eyed Wade cautiously, one hand reaching beneath the bar for a hidden Russian Makarov pistol.

Wade knew she hadn’t recognized him or had mistaken him for a homeless drifter: his hair was a tangled mess, his beard untrimmed and merging with his hair like two bushes meeting; his face was scratched and dirt-stained, the grime embedded deep; his clothes were mismatched, the rancid smell of the skins mixed with blood—a reminder of days he’d eaten raw meat, unable to light fires.

He cleared his throat. “It’s me.”

Erinn’s eyes widened. “Wade is coming?”

---

Wade nodded. “The key.”

His apartment belonged to Reindeer, on the top floor of this building. When he went out, he usually left the key with Erinn—just for safekeeping. Erinn never cleaned or tidied his place, even though she always insisted she loved him.

Still shocked, she held the key between two fingers and handed it over. When Wade leaned in, her face twisted in a mix of disdain and reluctance, as if afraid to touch him. She practically threw the key.

Wade caught it.

“Look at you,” Erinn said.

“You’d be the same after four months up north,” Wade replied.

That wasn’t entirely true—Erinn wouldn’t last four days up there.

He turned and left. Inside the building, it was less biting cold than outside. As he walked, he peeled off his animal skin.

“Wade!” Erinn called from behind.

He glanced back as she approached but had to recoil from the strong smell. Her expression was serious, almost angry.

“Wade, you better get back to your old self. You know I love you—mostly for your handsome face and body…”

She hesitated at “handsome,” feeling it was a mockery given his current state.

“…but like this, I can’t love you.”

---

The elevator was at the end of a narrow hallway, passing the security room. The building had only one guard—a German named Mark. Bald and impressively overweight, he struggled to squeeze through the security door, so he mostly stayed behind the glass, either asleep or eating.

As Wade passed, Mark was focused on slicing Bavarian white sausage. Wade sensed a dark shadow pass the window and, out of duty, muttered, “Moi!”

Without looking up, his slurred greeting landed in the sausage.

Wade figured Mark wouldn’t notice if it was a murderer, a bear, an alien, or a ghost passing by. Mark was just part of the building’s decor—a psychological comfort for residents.

In his long security career, Mark had only once “stepped up.”

It was Christmas Eve. At midnight, two men killed someone on the third floor. They didn’t care, pouring beer over the body, dragging the corpse out like a drunken friend.

The corpse wore one shoe; the other foot was bare, scraping the floor, leaving a trail of bloody beer.

Mark, less fat then, saw them coming and shouted, “Merry Christmas!” hoping to keep the festive spirit.

His Christmas gift was a stab.

That wound kept his job; Mark claimed he’d bravely confronted the killers to protect tenants. The truth was the murderers were never caught.

The old elevator was narrow, with a manual iron gate. A crumpled newspaper lay in the corner, trampled many times. The bold headline read:

**RANSOM!**

Another robbery, no doubt.

Four months without news—probably many deaths, new lives, and lots of money flowing from some hands to others.

Under the sun, nothing is truly new.

# Chapter 2

The door opened, releasing a smell of emptiness.

Wade never decorated his apartment. It had only the bare essentials—just enough to live. In his words, he wouldn’t feel any attachment leaving, nor would he miss it returning.

Who would miss an almost empty place?

He closed the door and stripped off his clothes, leaving them in layers on the floor. What was once his second skin had turned limp and worthless—trash even a homeless person wouldn’t pick up.

In the bathroom, he turned on the shower. The pipes groaned, like a stomach upset, then hot water flowed through the nozzle.

Pure comfort. The last time he bathed was in an icy lake.

His first layer of shaving foam didn’t lather. Black water ran down his cheeks and chin. Looking down, he saw dirty streaks spreading over his body, swirling into a vortex at the drain.

Shaving and trimming his short hair with clippers, black mud had embedded itself deep into his skin’s lines. Only a brush with soap could clean it. Water ran continuously, and it took three rounds of soap to wash away the grime. He was surprised he’d endured it.

Then again, he hadn’t really endured—it was just survival in that environment.

Turning off the shower, the bathroom fell silent. The steam faded, and despite the heater, a chill wrapped around him. Wade wrapped a towel around his waist and approached the mirror, wiping the fog away.

A man’s face stared back—sharp features, stubbly chin with a bluish tint from shaving, bare shoulders, hard, muscular.

His eyes were cold—probably from the cold itself.

But they were bright, clear. Fish can brighten eyes, and after so many days eating fish from the ice lake, it showed.

His thin lips pressed tight. They say men with thin lips are heartless—not true. Wade wasn’t entirely heartless, just not deeply emotional about anything.

He had to admit: he looked better now. Erinn might even fall for him again.

---

Wade bagged his discarded clothes and tossed them down the building’s garbage chute. As the gate closed, he pressed his ear to the wall, hearing the dull thud of trash hitting bottom.

It felt like a declaration—washed and discarded marks and proofs, a chapter closed.

Back in his room, he drew the curtains and lay down. The moment his head touched the pillow, his phone buzzed. A message from Reindeer:

*“10:30 tomorrow night, usual place.”*

He replied, “Okay,” as if Reindeer could hear him. Then he switched off his phone. His eyelids felt like heavy weights, and he sank into deep sleep.

Outside, Helsinki was drenched in freezing rain.

He slept over twenty-four hours. When he woke, twilight hung low over the city, with only some white edges still uncovered.

Wade pulled down the aluminum folding ladder attached to the skylight and climbed to the attic. Dust coated the floor, along with an empty beer can he’d left behind. The large skylight opened onto the sloped roof, double-glazed for cold and sound insulation. He pushed it open and climbed onto the slope.

The city’s roar washed over him. Wade took a few steps on the tiled roof, then sat down, lighting a cigarette.

Looking down, Helsinki looked like a steaming pot just uncovered, bustling with life.

Wade had his own theory about “human energy”: most people are under two meters tall. Humans emit body odor, breath, speak, fight, share emotions, go hysterical, or die—all using “qi,” or energy. All this energy swirls, mixes, and boils roughly two meters above the ground. So the atmosphere should be divided into: earth qi, human qi, and air.

Both Reindeer and CoCo had climbed high with him to observe this “human qi.” They always asked, “What do you see?”

Wade answered, “A lot of stories—happening, fermenting, fading away.”

CoCo called it nonsense.

Reindeer said, “You Chinese are just so fascinating.”

Night fell. To the northeast, the giant globe lamp held by the statue at Helsinki Central Station lit up. Wade stubbed out his cigarette and slipped back inside.

---

Back at the bar, it was 9 p.m. Death metal blasted *Killing Me Killing You*. In the corner near the door, an old man rolled marijuana, watched eagerly by a young man.

Wade walked straight to Erinn at the bar.

As expected, her eyes lit up with joy, smiling broadly. Her “Wade!” was full of affection. Even the king cobra tattoo on her neck softened, like a gentle spirit from a Jiangnan mist.

Wade dragged a tall bar stool and sat down, pulling his wallet from his pocket.

“Croissant, ice beer, vodka, red wine.”

Erinn started pouring the beer. Just as the glass slid toward him, Wade shook his wallet upside down sharply—

Only one coin fell out, rolling in a line across the bar and knocking into the jellyfish tank, toppling it.

A fifty-cent Euro coin, worth less than four yuan.

Erinn’s guard rose; she froze mid-pour.

Wade said, “Put it on my tab.”

“Where’s your money?”

“Spent it.”

“So much money!”

“Spent it.” Wade listed the expenses: “I hired an icebreaker to cut a channel through the frozen harbor. It was spectacular—a giant wedge carved into the Arctic Ocean. I took pictures to show you, but then it hit minus thirty degrees; my camera froze.”

He smiled, tapping Erinn’s hand. “You love me, right? Cut me some slack.”

Erinn was principled. “Loving you and money are different things.”

---

When Wade first met Erinn, she hadn’t opened the bar yet. Her first words to him were, “Are you Japanese?”

He remembered his face darkening, pausing before saying, “Chinese.”

“Chinese?” Erinn’s world map only included Germany, the Nordics, and the surrounding seas.

That night, passing a video store, she asked the clerk, “Any famous Chinese movies? New ones, preferably.”

The clerk rummaged through boxes and handed her a DVD. “This one’s very famous.”

It was Zhang Yimou’s *Not One Less*, about rural poverty and illiteracy, which had won several awards in Europe.

Erinn watched it twice, thinking she now understood China. The next day, she asked Wade casually, “How many mountains did you have to cross to get to school?”

Wade was smoking, silent for a long moment. His cigarette stub rested on a beer glass, ashes spilling in. Then he looked at her and said slowly, “You really should watch more news and care about the world.”

---

Erinn agreed to Wade’s tab for two reasons.

One, he had a good credit history—never actually owed money.

Two, he said he had a job coming tonight.

A job meant money. The last job brought a fat wad of cash, but after an expensive, twisted Arctic vacation, he was broke again.

It wasn’t a normal lifestyle. Erinn worried, watching Wade from across the smoky bar. She decided to find a chance to talk to him.

Wade tore off a piece of croissant, dipped it in salt on the plate, and popped it in his mouth. A seductive woman in a tight black skirt and heavy smoky gold eye makeup—like Cleopatra—sidled up.

Her voice was husky: “A drink for me?”

Wade said, “Sure.”

Cleopatra smiled, swayed her hips, and slid into his lap. The lace neckline plunged low, revealing a pale cleavage that drew his eyes—the same crack the icebreaker had carved.

The woman looped an arm around his neck, red lips near his face—but Wade suddenly stopped her.

“Wait. Are you wearing Chanel lipstick?”

Shade 99, bright red—the same as the reindeer’s lips in Lapland.

---

Erinn watched coldly, expecting a romantic exit. But five minutes later, Cleopatra left with a drink, seeking her next target.

Erinn smirked and brought Wade a slice of cheesecake. “On the house.”

She asked, “No luck?”

Wade whispered, “I’m out of sorts. The cold got to me. With a woman like that, I barely react. I need to warm up again.”

The ancients weren’t wrong: a full stomach sparks lust. After four months of hunger and cold, Wade hadn’t thought much about women. His mind replayed Finnish travel scenes instead of Cleopatra’s moves.

Erinn scolded, “Maybe the cold killed your desire.”

Wade wiped salt from the plate with croissant. “Why be so cruel? If I’m dead inside, what good does that do you?”

Erinn opened her mouth, but the clock on the wall chimed ten.

Three LCD screens hanging high in the bar switched on simultaneously.

---

The bar’s name, *We Care About the World*, wasn’t random. Every night at ten, world news was broadcast.

Regulars knew the rule and obeyed—whether chasing women or drinking, everything stopped at ten.

Most of them never watched news outside the bar.

Wade absorbed every headline like a transfusion, flooding his parched veins with the world’s pulse.

Japan earthquake, Indonesian volcano erupting, U.S. school shooting, Chechen terrorist leader killed by Russia…

Then another:

“Today marks the seventh day since Somali pirates hijacked the Saudi oil tanker Sirius. Twenty-five hostages remain missing. Insiders reveal the pirates demand a $20 million ransom…”

Twenty million dollars!

Wade couldn’t help but think of his 50-cent coin.

Maybe… he should become a pirate.

---

*Author’s Note:*

1) In November 2008, Somali pirates hijacked the Saudi oil tanker *Sirius* and 25 hostages. *Sirius* was the world’s largest, newest tanker at the time, with cargo and ship worth over $200 million. Pirates demanded $25 million ransom. After negotiations, reportedly $3 million was paid. Saudi officials credited diplomatic efforts for the release. (Usually, I fictionalize real names, but “Sirius” just sounds cool.)

2) The story begins in Finland, a Nordic country with a long, narrow landmass. Helsinki, the capital, lies in the south. The north stretches into the Arctic Circle, mostly dense forests called Lapland, home to many reindeer and the indigenous Sami. Traveling south from the Arctic, you pass Rovaniemi—the famous Santa Claus Village, known for sending postcards from Santa. Nordic countries are often gloomy and damp; Finland’s winter lasts eight months, with March-April still cold and unsettled.

# Chapter 3

Approaching the appointed time, Wade left the bar. Erinn caught up with him in the dim hallway.

“Wade.”

She was different—no teasing, no anger, just serious, with a hint of helplessness and sadness.

“You can’t keep living like this.”

Women are natural persuaders. Holding her youthful face, she spoke like she’d lived a hundred years:

“Don’t you have plans? Save money, marry a girl you like, buy a big house, live a stable life. I want to see you well. After all, you’re the only man I’ve ever loved.”

Erinn spoke honestly. During her love for Wade, she had a sudden awakening—she actually liked women. No trigger, no buildup—just a late realization.

Wade hesitated. To end such a topic sincerely, he had to be earnest.

“I know diligence, ambition, and stability are universal values. But the world’s big—you have to allow some people to go off track.”

He stepped back, bowed politely, then turned away.

Grateful for honest concern from a stranger.

He had no plans, lived day to day, happy to stray, unwilling to disturb those living serious, orderly lives.

---

Leaving the apartment, he walked straight down the street, turned left at the end. The pavement gleamed wet under streetlights. A battered Volkswagen waited under a lamp post.

Reindeer stood beside it, eyes shining, nearly lunging at him.

“Come here, Fir!”

‘Fir’ was Wade’s nickname.

Wade strode forward, grabbed Reindeer’s head, spun him around, then slipped into the passenger seat.

Inside, the temperature was just right—for talks or naps.

Reindeer climbed in excitedly.

“Wade! You’re back safe! God knows I watched *Into the Wild* three times! One night I dreamed you died—I cried so hard I thought I’d die too. I swear, when Eve died, I wasn’t this broken!”

Wade was speechless. Eve was Reindeer’s wife, mother of his two kids. That wasn’t the point. The point was Eve was alive and healthy, likely to live another 30–40 years.

---

Reindeer was Wade’s agent.

A 35-year-old African American, talkative and energetic. He believed the best food in the world was Chinese dumplings because “dumplings can taste a thousand ways!”

His language skills were good, especially in Chinese. Wade rarely spoke Chinese, but with Reindeer, they often mixed English and Chinese, with Reindeer sprinkling in local slang—even if sometimes incorrectly—making it feel warm and familiar.

---

Reindeer’s Chinese and comprehension grew under Wade’s frequent scolding.

After four months apart, Reindeer’s concern hit like Lapland’s sudden snowstorms. Wade ignored his chatter, eyes dropping on a leather envelope on the dashboard.

“Client files?”

Reindeer kept client info in leather envelopes sealed with twine.

Wade reached for it. Reindeer stopped him.

“No, this one.”

He pulled out another envelope from under the seat and handed it over solemnly.

It looked the same but was thicker—like a photo.

Wade didn’t open it yet.

“Specially picked for me?”

“I know you Chinese—’old friends meet old friends,’ tears in eyes.”

Got it. The client was probably Chinese or at least of Chinese descent.

Wade untied the twine.

“Not really. We have a saying—‘killing familiarity.’ We screw over our own without mercy.”

He pulled out the photo.

The cab was dimly lit. The moment Wade saw the photo, it seemed to brighten.

“Beautiful,” he murmured.

The photo showed a 26- or 27-year-old Chinese woman smoking on stairs. Her shoulder-length hair curled slightly at the ends. Her expression was blank, eyes locked with the camera.

Her eyes hid a deep world.

In the margin, written in marker: *Kimberly*.

Reindeer squinted at him. “Be careful. Men first fall for a dimple, then end up marrying the whole woman.”

Wade stared at the photo. “You underestimate me. First, she’s not pretty enough to make me lose my head. Second, I have professional ethics. Once I take a case, she’s a client—no relationships beyond money.”

He paused, then added, “Her gaze isn’t soft. She’s been through stuff.”

He set Kimberly’s photo against the windshield.

Streetlight filtered in, casting shadows on her face. Wade asked, “What’s she like?”

---

Reindeer was one of the top private security agents, with two stars under him: Fir and CoCo.

Stars could pick clients and set their own rules, however odd—like CoCo’s rule to never take jobs from clients with moles between their hairline and navel.

Weird. Why care about moles?

Compared to CoCo, Wade was easier—only one rule: no protecting scumbags.

Now that Wade asked about Kimberly’s character, he was considering the job.

Reindeer was ready with a script.

“People are complicated. Do you want to hear the good or the bad first?”

“The bad.”

“Be patient. No matter what, by the end, you’ll take the job.”

Wade smiled.

“Why ‘definitely’? Love isn’t eternal. Nothing is absolute.”

Outside, the street was empty. No one had passed by for a long time.

“Kimberly had a fiancé. On the eve of their wedding, he caught her cheating at a hotel. The wedding was off. Her fiancé tried to kill himself but was saved in time.”

That was private. Wade didn’t comment. Compared to Kimberly, he despised the fiancé more: a real man could find another wife; better to leave such a woman.

Reindeer brightened.

“But God is fair. Her fiancé met someone new in the hospital and married the next year. At the ceremony, he thanked God for not letting him die for the wrong person, so he could find true love.”

He handed Wade another photo—a handsome, scholarly Chinese man embracing a delicate wife, full of love and perfect together.

Wade nodded for more.

“Kimberly is also a suspect in a murder case.”

Reindeer paused, baiting him to ask. Wade stayed calm.

Reindeer continued.

“Luckily, evidence was insufficient; she was cleared quickly.”

“What case?”

“A French tycoon was poisoned by injection. The safe was open; unclear what was stolen. Police suspect robbery and murder. Kimberly was involved only because she was one of the visitors that night.”

“Only” already showed Reindeer’s stance—he tried to wash away negative rumors, even if true, they were collateral.

Wade was more curious about the poison.

“What poison?”

“Reportedly… tetrodotoxin.”

Wade was surprised.

Reindeer misunderstood. “I think it’s expensive. Pure tetrodotoxin sells for over $200,000 per gram. Ordinary poison can kill just as well—why bother?”

Wade said, “Because… it’s deadly.”

Tetrodotoxin (TTX) is over 1,200 times more toxic than cyanide. It causes nerve paralysis, loss of reflexes, and respiratory failure leading to death. Worse, TTX cannot cross the blood-brain barrier, so victims remain fully conscious but unable to move or speak during the dying process.

Fully conscious… just thinking about it sends chills down the spine.

Kimberly probably had other “issues,” but Reindeer thought they were common human flaws, not worth mentioning.

He was eager to highlight her brighter side.

“Kimberly was once part of an international African aid organization. During the Somali warlord conflicts, she helped the UN distribute relief food to refugees. Later, she went to Caron, where a shocking ethnic massacre occurred.”

Wade frowned. He’d heard of the Caron massacre.

Reindeer sneered, “You don’t care. Whether war, famine, conflict, or massacre in Africa, you treat it like it’s on another planet.”

Maybe because he was black, Reindeer spoke with righteous anger.

Wade recalled: Caron was a tiny country under 20,000 square kilometers, one of Africa’s smallest but most densely populated. It had two main ethnic groups, Huka and Kava, with frequent clashes and a civil war a few years back.

“Was that the Caron massacre labeled a crime against humanity? That was six years ago, right? I remember the UN even set a memorial day.”

Reindeer gritted his teeth.

“Exactly. The UN did nothing. Western countries turned blind. Media called it tribal conflict. The world abandoned Caron. In two months, over 200,000 Kava were killed. Only a few aid groups dared to help—Red Cross, Doctors Without Borders…”

Wade’s interest peaked.

“Kimberly… didn’t leave?”

Reindeer nodded.

“She stayed with a few volunteers at a small school, creating a humanitarian safe zone. They confronted Huka militants for over a month, saving 175 Kava lives. When she left Caron, the president awarded her the National Friendship Medal.”

Wade straightened, shedding his loose demeanor.

He had protected all kinds—industry leaders, heroes, fighters—but Kimberly’s background was something new.

“Does she need protection?”

“Two days ago, she received a… dead man’s hand.”
