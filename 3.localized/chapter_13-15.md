# Chapter 13

Wade drove the car up onto the riverbank and turned off the headlights. After a long while, the shimmering water and starlight filtered into the car. Wade used the faint light to open a packet of compressed biscuits, chewing them down with some water. Then he turned to Kimberly and asked for a cigarette.

“Do you smoke women’s cigarettes too?” he asked, surprised.

“Is there a difference? It’s all just tobacco,” she replied.

Kimberly handed him one and lit it for him. The instant the lighter’s flame flickered, a flicker of orange light glimmered in her eyes, his eyes, and on the surrounding glass — then vanished.

Wade rolled down the window, exhaled the first puff, and asked, “How did you figure it out?”

“You want to know?”

“Yeah.”

The more you know, the better — it might save your life one day, whether it’s your own or someone else’s.

Kimberly paused, then said, “Four points.”

Wade smiled wryly. He hadn’t noticed a single one.

“First, human trafficking has become an industry. The UNODC issues annual reports on trafficking routes, identifying source and destination countries. That ship, from Lithuania to Germany, fits the profile.”

“Second, the people on board spoke Albanian. Eastern European trafficking is controlled mainly by two gangs: the Russian mafia and the Albanian mafia. The Albanians dominate the underground sex trade across Europe.”

Wade was surprised. “You understand Albanian?”

“Just a few phrases. Remember when we went up on deck and that man laughed with someone in the cockpit, exchanging a few words?”

He did, but he hadn’t understood.

“The cockpit guy said: ‘New cargo?’ The man replied: ‘No, she’s too old.’”

Wade hesitated. “That ‘old’ — was he talking about you?”

“Yes.”

Kimberly shrugged indifferently. “Traffickers prefer younger women, especially minors, because a young body can endure more abuse. Women over twenty aren’t their first choice. I’ve written editorials on trafficking, so I learned some of their trading phrases.”

“New cargo, can’t be cheap, she’s too old, prime goods, deal done, pleasure doing business.”

“And the fourth one?”

“When that man opened the hatch, the cabin light was bright. On his tattooed arm, there were three claw marks with fresh blood — probably from a woman struggling.”

“Putting it all together, reporting them is justified. Even if I’m completely wrong, it’s definitely a black ship.”

Wade said nothing.

He was grateful to have Kimberly along — she specialized in this underground trade. If it had been him, even with a few more people, he wouldn’t have figured it out so quickly.

Now that he thought about it, Kimberly’s approach wasn’t exaggerated. Albanians are very suspicious. If they suddenly demanded to get off the ship, it would raise alarms.

Wade sighed deeply. “Alright, even if we have to change plans, it’s worth it.”

“No need to change. Tapiola said there’s another ship in four hours.”

“Back to the oil terminal?”

“Mr. Wade, you have to be thorough. The Albanians just lost a big shipment to the coast guard. Do you think they’ll let it go? Won’t those who got off the ship that night and never reappeared face suspicion and retaliation?”

She leaned in close, lowering her voice, a faint smile playing in the shadows of the car. “But if we rush back to take the ship, it changes things.”

“That means we really got sick suddenly; and we really are in a hurry.”

“If you want to perfect the story, you could get an emergency record from a hospital in Turku. But my current plan is enough to mess with the Albanians. They’ll be busy rooting out moles and spies. The ship is detained in international waters; news will be sealed off for a while. By the time chaos breaks out, we’ll already be on the pirate ship.”

Wade was silent for a moment, then laughed.

He stubbed out his cigarette on the window frame. “Impressive.”

Leaning back in his seat, he looked out at the distant night scene. His eyes adjusted to the darkness, revealing the rough, heavy boulders left over from road construction.

Wade mused, “Human trafficking is really an industry now?”

He’d thought it was just rampant crime.

“For the money. Low cost, high profit, huge demand, and it’s renewable.”

“Renewable?”

“Yeah, bullets run out, drugs get used up. But a girl of ten can be exploited year-round until thirty or forty. She can be sold again and again. When she has no more clients, she can even be sold for organs.”

Oh.

When he boarded the ship, he knew it was a black ship, but didn’t realize the cargo was people.

When it comes to people and lives, words like ‘worth it’ feel too light.

He turned to Kimberly. “Where’s the wound? Let me treat it — you who love wearing evening gowns so much.”

He turned the headlights back on as Kimberly pulled off a makeshift tourniquet.

Wade saw the wound on the inside of her left arm. If it was a normal blade, the cut would be neat and heal quickly. But the bear claw was cruel, harming both victim and wielder.

He cleaned the blood with bottled water, then used an alcohol swab to disinfect. After hesitating, he chose a small tube of skin adhesive.

“The wound’s not too deep. Stitching would be safer — with glue, you have to be careful, or it might leave a cavity under the skin, and the wound could tear.”

Kimberly gave a soft “hmm” and watched him carefully apply the ointment. Suddenly, she grew curious.

“Are you a newcomer, or naturalized?”

Wade smiled. “Hard to say. My dad probably owed debts back home, brought me over illegally, and sold me in Europe.”

“Sold to a foster family?”

“If only. Child labor.”

He cupped her arm and tilted his head, checking if the adhesive was evenly applied.

“Not even as tall as a machine, forced to operate sewing machines, stitch seams, attach buttons. One needle pierced through my finger. I thought I’d have a permanent hole on my fingertip to squint at the sun through. But it healed.”

“And then?”

“Kept doing buttons, rescued by humanitarian groups, spent years in Chinatown, trained with the Malaysian berets, failed and got kicked out. When I was about to sign up as a mercenary, I met Mwenda. He likes recruiting from there.”

He rested her arm on the dashboard. “Let it air out.”

“What about your plans?”

“None… you?”

Her turn.

Kimberly said, “I was an orphan, adopted by a Nordic couple and taken abroad. In high school, they died in a plane crash.”

“Tough to bear?”

A teenage girl, in a foreign land, parents dead, no relatives.

“Survival matters. No time to grieve. I had to figure out how to live with dignity in this white man’s world. So, I made a plan… up to age forty.”

Wade felt those words echo loudly in his head.

—I made a plan, up to forty.

He hadn’t even planned his next meal.

“What university to attend, what major, what social groups, which industry figures to network with, what skills to master, what internships to get, financial and career goals.”

He felt like he was reading some alien scripture.

After a long pause, he asked, “May I ask — is your current life part of that plan?”

Kimberly looked at her arm; the adhesive had hardened, the skin around it stretched tight.

“I’m 27.”

“According to the plan, I should be working in government, married to a lawyer, doctor, or professor — that kind of match is more suitable.”

“Financially comfortable, with property, car, savings, and insurance. I’d have a child already. A good family image helps political advancement.”

“Regular charity events, industry parties, meeting journalists, news workers, emerging business elites, and high society…”

Really? Reality seemed way off track.

Something must have happened along the way.

Wade said, “You should hurry and adjust.”

—

The car returned to the oil terminal at dawn.

Tapiola was sprawled out, asleep with an empty beer bottle. Wade shook him awake; he looked dazed for a while, then said, “Oh, you!”

Yawning, he sat up, flipped through the logbook, then checked the clock. “There’s a ship. Perfect timing.”

Of course it was perfect timing—they’d timed it to the minute.

When boarding, Tapiola glanced at Kimberly in the backseat, wrapped in a thick coat, pale and weakly smiling at him.

“Is she… okay?” he asked.

“Ulcer burst, stomach bleeding. She’s been to the hospital.”

“Will she hold up?”

The old man was kind-hearted.

Wade glanced at Kimberly. “She’s not important. In our line of work, you follow orders. Unless you’re dead, you show up no matter what—even crawling. You’ve seen enough to know.”

Tapiola sighed. “True.”

Coincidentally, this was another refrigerated ship, carrying fruits, vegetables, fish, meat, perishable goods.

The crew were scattered across the deck, some coming down to meet them.

Tapiola stayed by the car, waving as they boarded, yawning widely.

Wade supported Kimberly along. She was supposed to look “weak.”

Passing a crew member adjusting a radio by the railing, static crackled as a broadcast came through:

“All eyes worldwide remain fixed on the Sirius, this expensive oil tanker…”

Wade and Kimberly stopped in their tracks.

The crewman eyed them oddly, then quickly turned the dial aside.

The broadcast grew louder, drifting through the fog.

“The pirates remain defiant, rejecting the shipowner’s ransom negotiations. The Saudi negotiation team held a press conference in Mogadishu yesterday, not ruling out military intervention.”

“Experts warn the situation in the Gulf of Aden is complex, with a long history of piracy. Military action could paralyze the entire sea route with dire consequences…”

Wade couldn’t help but smile.

The world was funny — the Saudis on one side putting on a smoky show, eyes wide, sleeves rolled up, ready to brawl, directing a swirling crowd of reporters, experts, and analysts.

Everyone’s attention was glued there: Mogadishu, the Sirius, the Saudi negotiation team, the pirates.

No one knew that the most crucial person was boarding here, right now.

Wade looked back at the shore.

Tapiola sped off in his car.

The long gray line where land met water slowly receded.

The ship set sail.

# Chapter 14

After leaving the deck, the air was stuffy. The crew’s quarters had five or six cramped rooms, like old train compartments with sliding doors.

The crew had set aside one room for them. Inside, there were bunk beds on both sides; the narrow aisle barely allowed turning around.

They put their luggage on the top bunks. Wade and Kimberly sat opposite each other on the lower bunks. For a moment, no one spoke. The sudden familiarity forged overnight in crisis seemed to vanish with the dawn.

Probably due to her injury and fatigue, Kimberly pulled her hood up and, without a word, lay down and fell asleep again.

Wade propped the blanket and pillow behind his back, leaning against the wall, bored. He hoped not to fall asleep—he’d never slept on a smuggling ship before. He feared the dreams would be unpleasant.

He didn’t know how long passed before his eyelids grew heavy. Just as he feared, he slipped back into the dim cabin of that smuggling ship.

The air was thick and foul—body odor, urine, vomit, and moldy stench mingled and fermented in the closed space. People lay sprawled, disheveled and near death, all tangled together. In the darkness, you couldn’t tell men from women. In disaster, there is no gender.

He saw himself as a child, thin as a twig, propping himself up to ask his father, “Why did we leave home?”

There had been no warning. His father took him straight from elementary school to the ship. His backpack still had textbooks: Chinese, math, morality.

His father never answered, and never would.

He still didn’t understand: many people leave home thinking they’ll find clarity elsewhere, but it’s just confusion in a different place.

The ship rocked side to side. The voyage seemed endless.

Wade opened his eyes.

He was dazed for a moment. A faint rustle sounded nearby. He tried to sit up, then heard Kimberly speak.

“Don’t move.”

She must have woken earlier, sitting cross-legged on the opposite bunk, head down, drawing.

Was he her model?

Wade thought he’d cooperate. After last night, he felt a growing fondness for her.

He kept his waking posture, noticing how ungainly it was: one arm under his head, head tilted, one leg tucked under the bed, the other stretched outside.

He tried to comfort himself: maybe it made him look tall and slim.

Never been a drawing model before—how long do you have to hold still? Half an hour? Maybe they should chat; silence was stifling.

His forehead, calves, behind his ears, chest all started itching inexplicably.

But this angle gave a good view of Kimberly. She showed no expression as the pencil tip scratched above the paper’s edge, the sound soft and constant, a glint catching her neck.

She wore the same necklace.

The necklace must have special meaning. Who gave it to her? Ming Jiang?

Wade frowned: she listens to Ming Jiang’s lectures without emotion, burns holes in his shirts, saying it’s “closure.”

He couldn’t resist.

“Can I ask a personal question?”

“Go ahead.”

“What’s your relationship with Ming Jiang?”

The pencil paused briefly before continuing, as if nothing happened.

“Just a normal relationship.”

“Normal… what does that mean?”

“No disasters, no troubles, just getting along peacefully. When crisis hits, we go our separate ways.”

Oh.

Wade pictured a vast forest, countless birds fluttering chaotically in all directions.

Makes sense. Men and women today are restless; without crisis, everyone secretly plans to fly away.

“Did he ever wrong you?”

Otherwise, why betray him and burn his clothes?

“Not really… he said things I didn’t want to hear.”

Wade felt sorry. It’s not illegal to grumble after a breakup, but not exactly virtuous either.

“He spread rumors about you… betraying him?”

“No. At the wedding, he said, after surviving the ordeal caused by his ex, he thanked God he didn’t die for the wrong person.”

She lifted her eyelids, her gaze sharp over the paper’s edge. Slowly, she said, “He called me an ‘ordeal.’”

You were his ordeal all along.

A scholar’s biggest hardship might be missing a full scholarship. To commit suicide over your betrayal, nearly lose his life, and fail to save humanity…

And you don’t even let him call you his ordeal?

Wade held back, not defending Ming Jiang. Clearly, Kimberly could rescue strangers on a black ship but also hold grudges — he feared his clothes might get burned next.

His arm under his head went numb. Impatient, he asked, “Finished?”

She signed and dated it. “Just doodling. Not keeping it. Want to see?”

She handed him the paper. The moment he looked, he sat up sharply.

Pencil sketch style: several adorable little pigs, one leading, others following.

Wade gripped the paper’s edge. If it were an aluminum beer can, he’d have crushed it long ago.

Damn. Wasn’t this a drawing of him?

He held back asking; he could guess her answer: I told you not to move, didn’t say I was drawing you.

He smiled as best he could. “Why pigs?”

“Passed the refrigerated hold, saw the pig logo on the hatch, so I drew it.”

Wade returned the paper. “I draw sometimes too, but not this style.”

She lazily stretched her arm to toss the pen and paper back to the empty top bunk. “We’ll practice together sometime.”

Checking the time, they’d only covered half the journey.

They passed time eating sailor’s meals, using the bathroom, brewing instant coffee, reading old newspapers, and napping.

Finally, a crewman knocked: they’d arrived in port.

On deck, they could finally breathe Stockholm air. Kimberly felt relieved to have survived the voyage. She got up, packed her things, and started rolling up the drawing.

Halfway through, she hesitated and unrolled it.

Someone had added a few strokes.

—Actually, I draw sometimes too, but not in that sketch style.

Honest. His style was minimal but captured spirit. This drawing was clearly of her.

She was riding the lead pig.

From its snout extended reins like a horse’s.

One hand gripped the reins tightly; the other arm was raised high, as if rallying a call.

Three more pigs followed closely behind.

Wade grabbed both bags in one hand, hoisted them onto his shoulder, and said, “Let’s go.”

Acting casual.

Kimberly looked at him, folding the drawing carefully, nails scraping the paper with a sinister sound in the cramped space.

Wade watched her nails, guessing she might scratch him.

When she finished folding it into a neat square, she stuffed it into her coat pocket.

“Let’s go.”

—

On deck, the view opened wide.

Late afternoon in April. Like Helsinki, Stockholm bordered the Baltic Sea. Helsinki was gloomy and damp; here, the sun sparkled on the water — unusual. Usually, Stockholm and Helsinki shared bad weather like rivals.

After disembarking, they walked along the shore and saw a medieval multi-masted triangular sailboat flying many flags. The long, slender hull rose high at the bow like a beast’s horn.

Faint coffee aroma and violin music floated out from the boat — it was a café on a sailing ship.

Wade invited Kimberly, “Let’s rest and get a drink.”

But that wasn’t his real intention. When ships arrive, the dispatch gets the message; Tapiola would notify Mwenda that their “tickets” were confirmed. If the Saudis had new developments, Mwenda would call him.

Kimberly didn’t object. Wade thought she was mostly easygoing, except for occasional stubbornness — either sleeping or quietly following him.

They sat outside near the bow. A blond guy played a nyckelharpa, an odd wooden shoe-shaped instrument, its sound flowing with the breeze and flags.

When coffee, salad, and sandwiches arrived, Mwenda’s call came as expected.

“Wade, Tiger Shark has news.”

Wade stayed calm, picking a small potato from the salad. “What’s the update?”

“They only gave general directions, leading you step by step. No exact location—just said to meet in the Red Sea, international waters.”

Wade frowned. Geography wasn’t his strong suit. “The Red Sea? That long narrow one?”

“Right. We discussed it. You take Ms. Lee to the airport, Terminal 5 tourist center. Someone will give you your tickets. Fly tonight.”

Nonstop hustle. Wade rubbed his face wryly.

“Fly where?”

“Khartoum, Sudan’s capital. Long trip, no direct flight. You’ll have to transfer.”

Wade was silent.

Then said slowly, “Are you kidding? You think I don’t know Sudan’s at war?”

Kimberly heard him and quietly corrected, “Technically, it’s a localized armed conflict.”

Mwenda was ready.

“Wade, listen. First, a country is big. The south can be at war while the north sings. Sudan had a 22-year civil war but it’s basically over now. Khartoum is the capital and safe.”

“Second, look at the map. Sudan borders the Red Sea in the middle—easy access to international waters.”

“Third, and this is important: CoCo is protecting military and political figures there. He’ll meet you at the airport and arrange everything.”

Wade paused.

Whispered, “CoCo?”

That annoying guy with the receding hairline, designer clothes, braided hair, who he hadn’t seen in a while.

Mwenda heard the hesitation. “See? I told you, you can meet CoCo there…”

Wade laughed, ordered a dark beer.

Mwenda said something else; Wade didn’t hear. “What?”

“Wade! I’m asking, how’s it going with that ‘dull’ Ms. Lee?”

# Chapter 15

Wade thought he misheard.

He stood and took a few steps away. “Say that again?”

“How’s it going with that ‘dull’ Ms. Lee?”

Wade admired Mwenda from the bottom of his heart. “You even use four-character Chinese idioms!”

Mwenda was enthusiastic about slang but rarely spoke four-character idioms in Chinese.

His goal achieved, he was in a great mood. “Wade, I knew you’d get it! Idioms are hard! So, how do you get along with Ms. Lee?”

“Pretty well.”

“Pretty well!?”

“She’s not dull at all. Sometimes she surprises you — it’s a little scary.”

He looked down at his pants. The bloodstain was still there, but strangers might think it was some artistic or eccentric decoration.

“Getting along well… so, will you two get married?”

Where did that come from? Wade was speechless.

The blond guy smiled at Kimberly. No way, she’s marrying a doctor, lawyer, or professor — not a fiddler.

He lowered his voice. “I don’t see much hope.”

Mwenda was disappointed. “Can’t you try? Wade! You two really match. I even thought of your kid’s name.”

Wade’s vein twitched.

But he listened — Mwenda wouldn’t say this for no reason.

Sure enough—

“I just learned Chinese these past two days and realized!”

“Wade, your name means ‘future.’ Ms. Lee’s name means ‘past.’ If you have a kid, you can name them ‘now.’”

“Then your family would be Past, Future, and Now. I can even write a song: ‘Now’s naughty, Past’s beauty, Future’s responsibility…’”

Damn.

Wade felt his scalp tingle and stopped Mwenda before his musical imagination ran wild.

“Ms. Lee made plans in her teens, up to age forty. I can guarantee there’s no room for me in them, and there never will be.”

Her plan probably goes up to eighty now, maybe even includes her funeral.

Curious, he wanted to confirm.

After hanging up, Wade sat back. His dark beer had a thick white foam like overly black cola.

“Can I ask a question? Did you ever make plans beyond that? Like old age, funerals, who dies first…”

He felt ridiculous asking.

But she answered.

“I have. Ideally, I want my husband to die first because couples have many secrets. If I die first, he might spread rumors and ruin my reputation.”

“If he dies first, I get a peaceful old age to write memoirs…”

Wade wanted to drown himself in the beer.

A plan so precise sounded ridiculous at first, then terrifying, yet somehow admirable.

Still, he couldn’t help but ask, “Living so methodically… don’t you find it boring?”

“No.”

She sounded casual. “I’m just saying — my train’s already derailed… didn’t you notice?”

—

After resting, Wade hailed a taxi to the airport, motioning Kimberly to join him in the back seat.

On the way, he began cleaning up.

Kimberly helped shield him with her backpack as he dismantled a gun.

He handled it like a Rubik’s cube, calm and precise. A good gun turned into scattered parts: magazine, cartridge, firing pin, spring, broken anesthetic needle — a pitiful wreck in a paper bag.

None of it could go on the plane; it had to be disposed of.

Wade reached for her. “Bear Claw.”

Kimberly hesitated.

He understood — probably because the Bear Claw looked good. This one was especially small, coated in black Teflon, no sheath, just a finger ring for close combat. If it weren’t sharpened, it’d be a nice pendant.

Women dislike danger but often love beautiful and dangerous things — like Bear Claws, like handsome men.

He kept reaching. “Bear Claw.”

Still no move.

“This Bear Claw is new. The first to draw my blood. I raised it.”

True to her editorial-writing nature, she had imagination.

Wade said, “You raised it… what, expecting it to have babies?”

Not a hen sitting on eggs, breeding endlessly.

“It has meaning. This is the first knife to draw my blood in this life.”

No wonder. Once she attached meaning, things got complicated. She made him feel it was meaningful — behind this knife, there were a shipload of women who might not have been saved.

“Really want to keep it?”

His tone softened. Kimberly nodded.

“Then give it to me.”

A trick? She hesitated but finally handed over the Bear Claw.

Wade weighed it — actually quite small.

He looked up, smiled gently. “No, it won’t pass security.”

Kimberly turned to the window, every cell screaming: Don’t talk to me anymore.

At the airport, Wade had a plan: three steps, one by one.

First, circle around with Kimberly, tossing parts from the paper bag into different trash bins, like a farmer sowing seeds.

Bullets went down different drains, perfectly dismantled. The gun would never be whole again.

Second, he bought himself coffee.

Kimberly waited nearby. The barista laughed at his jokes and even gave him her number with a wink.

Wade came back. “Not bad.”

“Find fun wherever you can, or it gets boring.”

“If you focused on one, you’d have kids and grandkids by now.”

Wade leaned closer. “How can you say that? Kids are fine, but grandkids? Really?”

He looked into Kimberly’s eyes, slipping the little paper tube into her coat pocket.

“Your Bear Claw — after negotiations, call her to get it.”

…

Finally, they waited at the tourist center entrance, standing in the most visible spot.

Nordic men, especially, were tall and broad-shouldered, usually over 180 cm. Wade wasn’t lacking in that.

Kimberly looked at him for a moment and thought “clothes hanger” was a fitting description.

A blonde young woman passed by, even glanced back at him.

That counted as a small victory, despite their complicated nationalities.

Bored, Kimberly asked, “Just standing here waiting — will you really get the tickets?”

Wade asked, “You rarely do these meet-ups, right?”

He explained, “If I tell you to wait, you wait here. Mwenda’s arrangements are reasonable, the handoff will be natural and unnoticed. In our line of work, many details are invisible to outsiders…”

Before he finished, someone shouted behind them.

“Fir? Fir? Who’s Fir?”

Wade thought life was tough.

Kimberly looked at him.

Wade hoped she’d stay quiet.

Those who know better keep quiet, giving others face is a virtue.

A big guy stomped up, wearing work clothes, holding a mop bucket, hair spiked like a fool.

“Black-haired guy called Fir, with a girl. Is that you? Why don’t you answer?”

Then he slapped an envelope into Wade’s hands. “Your ticket.”

As he left, muttering about him being “dumb,” “not answering,” “idiot.”

Wade tried not to look at Kimberly, maintaining a calm face as he checked the ticket.

Kimberly kept watching him.

Wade hoped she’d stay quiet.

No such luck.

“The ‘reasonable’ arrangement means yelling, huh?”

Of course not. You can call burning clothes ‘closure’; I can call Mwenda’s plan unexpected and brilliant…

“Let’s go.”

She gave him no chance to argue, turning toward the terminal. As she entered, she raised her right arm and beckoned inward.

Like a summon, a guide, riding a pig, rallying a call…

Wade thought the metaphor was fitting — a silent, subtle insult, like spring rain.

He shifted his bag strap and followed happily.

Wait — raised arm, riding a pig — leading who?

---

Security and customs went smoothly. Wade only grumbled about the ticket: a red-eye flight.

But flying nearly 20 hours, there’d be some night segment. Besides, the Saudis were generous — first-class seats.

All that remained was waiting to board.

Being a bodyguard, the hardest part was waiting. You couldn’t keep chatting — clients would get annoyed.

Besides, Kimberly wasn’t talking either. She had her distraction: pencil and paper, sketching with eyes half-closed.

Wade multitasked, watching her and scanning the surroundings.

No danger. Maybe all as expected. The only threat to Kimberly was a creepy stalker.

Her lines began to take shape: a small school with a playground, a flagpole with a flag.

Groups gathered on the playground, making fires. Smoke mingled with clouds.

Behind the school gate, beds, desks, stones, and trucks blocked the way.

Just as he was intrigued, laughter mixed with rolling suitcase wheels and an unfamiliar language passed by the first-class lounge door.

Wade thought it normal — international airport, many languages.

But Kimberly’s pencil suddenly hesitated: the line thickened noticeably at one point.

Wade’s gaze flicked toward the passengers who just passed.

A large group with children and adults, bright robes under thick coats. Among them, a little girl with dirty dreadlocks tied with colorful beads, her head bouncing noisily.

Wade looked away. “The flight’s to Khartoum; there should be many African passengers.”

Kimberly said nothing. After a while, she resumed drawing.

No matter how detailed the sketch, that thick pencil mark remained.
