# Chapter 7

We had agreed to have a couple of drinks at Erinn’s bar. After dropping Wade off at his apartment, Mwenda suddenly switched into the role of a responsible family man and said, “Don’t stay out too late. Eve will be worried.”

It was already past midnight, for crying out loud.

The bar was nearly empty, sinking into the dead quiet of the late night. One cigarette and a glass of whiskey were enough to get through until dawn. Wade was too lazy to head upstairs, so he greeted Erinn and slumped onto the long sofa in the corner as if it were his own.

Erinn brought him a blanket, then pulled over the calculator and ledger to one side and began tallying the accounts, quietly muttering numbers to herself, sometimes aloud.

This was the warmest moment — lying there steady and calm, with sleep to catch later. Erinn was like a diligent younger sister, working hard to make ends meet.

Wade chatted with her intermittently.

“What about your girlfriend? The last time I saw her, she was Bulgarian, short, and when she smiled it looked like she was about to cry.”

“She went back home. Said she couldn’t find work here, then cut off contact.”

“Are you upset?”

Erinn thought for a moment. “Not really.”

“That’s good.”

“Soon I’m going back to Germany. My sister Sabina is getting married. Mom says it’s been too long since she’s seen me.”

“Going home is good.”

His eyes closed lightly, his words sounding like a sigh. Erinn hesitated, then asked, “Wade, do you remember your home?”

She knew Wade’s story. His father had taken him as a child onto a smuggler’s boat headed for Snakehead Island. They drifted at sea for a long time; an epidemic broke out on the boat, killing a third of the passengers. He survived the landing, only to be sold by his father afterward.

“No, I don’t remember.”

“Do you miss home?”

“Home doesn’t miss you. Why should I miss home?”

Erinn fell silent, pressing the calculator buttons gently. The March accounts were settled — neither good nor bad, just like most ordinary days in life.

Was April worth looking forward to? The temperature would rise slightly; snow and ice would slowly melt from south to north. April had Oktoberfest and Hat Day...

Wade had a dream.

He dreamed of the smuggler’s boat tossing in the waves. The stench of the sick passengers filled the air as they vomited. A small hatch on the deck opened, letting in light that fixed on a limp corpse being dragged out. The Snakehead stomped his foot on the deck and shouted angrily, “Throw him overboard! His body’s full of disease. It’s contagious!”

He shouldn’t have talked about this with Erinn just before sleep.

But that boat always crept into his dreams at times. People say that what you can let go of belongs to the past; what you can’t is fate. Wade felt that boat was his fate.

Even if he lived to eighty, the boat would still be battered by waves in his dreams, never reaching shore.

He climbed onto the deck as the crew shouted and heaved the corpse overboard. There was a splash, white foam blooming on the black water.

At the bow, Kimberly sat on a high stool, an easel before her. The long hem of her dress fluttered wildly in the sea breeze.

Wade was puzzled. “What are you doing here?”

Kimberly turned her head, and for a moment, the world seemed to shake.

...

No, it wasn’t the world shaking — it was Erinn nudging him awake. Morning had come. Nearby, an ashtray on a table still held the faint wisps of a dying cigarette. Erinn pointed at Wade’s phone on the table; its screen stubbornly flashed green.

Wade, still groggy and yawning, answered.

“Hello?”

“Wade! You got it! They chose you!”

“What?”

He sat up and rubbed his forehead. Just waking, reality felt as unreal as a dream. Erinn busied herself with the coffee machine, the hum and whirl of the grinder filling the air.

“I mean the Saudis. They called me to say it’s you.”

Wade recalled the image of Kimberly’s dress fluttering in the sea breeze. What was she painting at the bow?

“The Saudis wouldn’t pick me.”

“Yes, I heard they disagreed, but Ms. Lee ignored that. Wade, I think this is like marriage. No matter how much the parents object, the woman who shares your bed decides everything.”

What a ridiculous analogy.

Mwenda offered a price Wade couldn’t refuse, then cautiously asked, “Wade, will you take the job? If not, I’ll turn it down.”

Inside, Wade wanted to scream a thousand times: Please, say yes! Say you’re willing!

He paused.

Hadn’t she said a bodyguard was useless?

But afterward, she stopped him and said something else. Standing there, she looked like a stark black-and-white painting.

Erinn came over with a cup of freshly made coffee. Wade lifted it and drank it all in one go.

“I have conditions.”

Mwenda held her breath, listening.

“I’ll only do my job as a bodyguard. I’m not her lackey. If she’s polite, I’ll be polite. If she’s rude, don’t blame me for giving her a hard time.”

Mwenda said, “Of course. It’s not a slave society. She pays, you provide protection. It’s a fair exchange. She must respect your effort, and you respect her money. That’s the rule.”

It seemed everything was said, but Mwenda cleared her throat and chose her words carefully: “Ms. Lee also made a request...”

Knew it wouldn’t be simple.

“She said she wants you to write something every day... about your thoughts on her.”

Wade took a while to digest that.

He wasn’t angry but found it absurd. “If Ms. Lee thinks negotiating with Somali pirates is historically significant, she should get a documentary crew or a biographer. That’s not a bodyguard’s job, right?”

“It’s not that complicated! Wade, I checked. Even one sentence works — like: she’s annoying, her makeup’s bad, I don’t get along with her.”

That was acceptable?

Mwenda rambled, “Just one sentence, easy. Want to write more or less, up to you. Wade, bodyguarding is like modeling — a young person’s game. You should think about your future career. Maybe this will reveal a hidden talent for writing...”

Mwenda, Wade’s agent, always so passionate, lighting dreams wherever she went.

After the call, Erinn came over to collect the coffee cup and asked curiously, “Who’s the client this time?”

Wade said, “Looks like a ladybug.”

“Huh?”

“I’m supposed to write a diary about a ladybug’s life.”

Erinn immediately accepted and even encouraged him.

“That’s how rich people are. If I were rich, I’d hire you to protect my jellyfish. Writing a diary would be great — I want to know what they do when I’m not around.”

What could they possibly do? The jellyfish tank was tiny, always in the same spot.

Wade looked at the two translucent moon jellyfish drifting inside.

Maybe they were plotting: how to hijack a ship in the Gulf of Aden after they got out.

—

Later that day, Mwenda took Wade to sign the contract with the White Robes.

The White Robes stayed at the luxury Kanlap Hotel downtown — a 19th-century Eastern European-style building with first-class facilities, staff, and security. Yet, something went wrong here.

Two White Robes returned from dinner to find their door ajar. Inside was a mess.

A theft.

When Wade arrived, the younger White Robe, Saed, was loudly scolding the room manager. The police were still on their way. The older White Robe, Janusz, stood in the middle of the room frowning, hesitant to disturb the scene.

Mwenda showed concern: “Mr. Janusz, was anything valuable stolen?”

“Some money — a bit over two thousand euros, petty cash. No valuables were kept in the room.”

The room manager broke into a sweat, apologizing to Saed: “We’re as surprised as you. Someone hacked the door system, bypassed alarms and cameras... Luckily, no major losses. The hotel will fully cooperate with the police...”

Mwenda whispered beside them, “These White Robes, you know, they practically wear ‘I’m rich’ on their foreheads. Easy targets for thieves.”

Something felt off.

Wade stepped inside. Cabinets and drawers were wide open. A suitcase lay tipped over. Clothes were tossed everywhere. Papers scattered on the floor — one had a shoe print on the back.

European size 43 to 44, a man's shoe, common tread — no traceable value.

Wade crouched to pick up the papers. Janusz warned, “Don’t touch! Better to keep the scene intact until the police arrive.”

But Wade picked up a page from the bodyguard contracts pending signature.

“Do you usually bring many valuables with you when you travel?”

Janusz shook his head. They worked for the shipowner and were here on business.

Wade picked up more papers — besides contracts, there was an itinerary for him and Kimberly: Helsinki to Nairobi, Kenya’s capital, then direct into East Africa.

Wade stood. “Can we talk somewhere private?”

—

They went to the bathroom. Wade locked the door and quickly scanned the room. It was all marble, with built-in power outlets — no place for bugs.

Janusz looked nervous.

Wade said, “My guess might be wrong, but that’s not the point.”

“Kanlap Hotel is ranked among the world’s top 100. Guests include business magnates, political figures, celebrities. The Saudis blend right in. If it were a thief after money, stealing from them would be more profitable than you.”

“The security is solid, with multiple checkpoints upstairs and downstairs. Someone who can hack the door and avoid alarms wouldn’t do it just for two thousand euros. That’s not worth the trouble.”

Wade handed over the documents. “So careful, yet the cameras caught nothing. They left a footprint to show they don’t care about these papers. Isn’t that overacting?”

Janusz caught on. “You mean...”

“Ms. Lee has received death threats. If I were the other side, I’d want to know where she’s going next and where would be easiest to strike.”

He smiled. “Maybe I’m totally off, but a bodyguard should suspect everything. From now on, every anomaly will be treated as a threat to Ms. Lee.”

Janusz studied him for a long moment, suddenly feeling Kimberly might be better at reading people than himself.

“So?”

“So this itinerary can’t be used. At least... not the real plan.”

# Chapter 8

The sudden turn of events led the White Robes to bring Wade and company to meet Kimberly.

It was evening when they arrived. The cleaning lady opened the door. Inside the living room, a man was packing up to leave.

He was Asian, short and stocky, with a round face and a polite smile. The White Robes were conspicuous, but he kept looking at Wade, who looked back — both thinking the other was Chinese.

Up close, Wade caught a distinct smell — a mix of hot oil, dishwater, scallions, and ginger.

“Chinese? A cook?”

The man lit up. “A fellow countryman! I knew you looked Chinese.”

He quickly handed over a business card. “Come by sometime. Tell them I’m a friend — you’ll get a discount.”

He was indeed a chef: Yongfu Lin, head chef at Huaxia Tianfu.

Chinese restaurants overseas had grand names: China Tower, Dragon Hall, Great Shanghai. Mwenda peeked at the card and asked in perfect Mandarin, “Do you have dumplings?”

The chef widened his eyes, as if doubting a black man could speak Chinese.

Wade asked, “Do you know Ms. Lee?”

“Ms. Lee’s eaten at the restaurant a few times. She likes the food. When she orders, I go cook at her place.”

He jingled the bag he was carrying — bottles and jars of oil, salt, soy sauce, vinegar.

“Since when?”

“Just recently.”

Someone covered for him at the restaurant. Yongfu was in a hurry and couldn’t chat more. As he left, he waved at Mwenda from a distance: “We have dumplings and shumai!”

Wade asked the cleaning lady. Besides Yongfu, Kimberly also favored Western and Japanese cuisine. A Western chef and a Japanese head chef would come on call.

But all only recently.

The cleaning lady led them to the dining room.

The room was large and dim, decorated with marble. In the center stood a minimalist pure white long table. The corners had no legs; instead, two stylized human figures supported the tabletop on their shoulders, as if carrying the weight of the world.

Only a small light hung above the center, its glow floating over the table and Kimberly.

She wore a sea-blue satin one-shoulder evening gown, asymmetrical and elegant, the lines of her shoulder and collarbone delicate as a painting.

Her necklace was the same as before.

Hearing voices, she looked up. When she saw the White Robes, she quickly covered a fine white porcelain bowl on the table.

But Wade had already seen — inside the bowl was a small, glossy red piece wrapped carefully in thin white strings to prevent it from falling apart: Dongpo pork.

There was also a bowl of crab roe tofu, a small pot of Buddha Jumps Over the Wall soup, a dish of braised bamboo shoots, and a bowl of white rice.

Small portions, but exquisite — a feast for any Chinese diner. Chef Lin had put real effort into it.

The White Robes explained Wade’s proposal to Kimberly. She had no objections, simply said “Okay,” “No problem,” and signed the bodyguard contract — three copies.

Each party kept one. Wade flipped to the signature page. His and the White Robes’ copies had been signed at the hotel. Kimberly’s was freshly signed; the ink still wet. She signed with her Chinese name, but the last stroke of the character “今” was habitually paused, almost like the character “令.”

The contract took effect two days later — the day of departure.

Holding the documents, the White Robes seemed relieved. Wade, however, asked, “Is there a bodyguard arrangement at Ms. Lee’s place these two days?”

Janusz hesitated and shook his head.

“For that boat, I recommend two bodyguards. The cleaning lady works only four hours a day. At night, Kimberly is alone. It would be easy for something to happen.”

Janusz realized the oversight and quickly ordered Saed to arrange security.

Wade then asked Kimberly, “May I see your bedroom?”

She agreed and led him there.

The bedroom was also spacious. Wade looked out the window, then back at her bed.

There were plenty of sniper vantage points in the distance. Her bed was badly placed — late at night, with the right angle, any bullet through the window could hit the person in bed.

Wade drew the curtains and gave her some advice:

- Don’t open the curtains anymore. Enter the room as usual at night, but after lights out, sleep in another room.

- Lock all entrances except the front door.

- If possible, pay the cleaning lady extra to stay overnight as a companion.

Kimberly just said “Okay,” “Can do,” but her expression seemed perfunctory.

As he left, Wade asked, “Does Ms. Lee have any appointments today?”

“No, she didn’t expect you to come.”

On the way back, Wade asked Mwenda, “Don’t you think Ms. Lee is a bit strange?”

“Definitely.” Mwenda had been holding back, waiting for someone to bring it up, then burst out: “When I entered the dining room, she was sitting there, the light so dim, shining only on her. It startled me.”

For a moment, it felt like she was a quiet ghost — faint, unreal, lacking “life.”

The car stopped at a traffic light. Mwenda looked at the pedestrians: a golden-haired girl crying and arguing with her mother, a man coming out of a supermarket, dropping bags everywhere and crouching to pick them up in frustration.

That’s how people should live — rushing, shouting, messy. Kimberly seemed detached from the world.

Wade said, “Twice I’ve seen her, and both times she was in an evening gown. Don’t you find that odd?”

Odd? Mwenda thought the gowns were beautiful.

“Not just the gown — her makeup was exquisite too — but neither time was an important occasion. The first was an interview with many strangers, so that was barely acceptable. But today, she said she had no appointments.”

“Wasn’t the chef an appointment?”

A woman wouldn’t dress up so carefully just for a chef. Wade thought normal was like Erinn — lazy, no makeup, hair all messy.

Mwenda considered. “Maybe she actually has visitors but doesn’t want to tell you?”

Possible.

Wade was curious: what kind of visitor would make her dress up?

Probably a man.

—

The next two days, Wade stopped asking about Kimberly’s situation, leaving all communication to Mwenda — his habit. Once the contract took effect, everything had to move fast. Before then, he needed to relax completely.

He cleaned his place.

Visited the Rock Church, standing for a while beneath walls built from shattered rock, half-expecting the stones to fall and bury him — but they didn’t.

At the outdoor market at the South Pier, he ate salted fish, smoked ham slices, bought peaches and apples.

Took the ferry to the coastal fortress. This season, the island was cold and desolate.

He also ate at Huaxia Tianfu.

The restaurant used cheap materials but created a visually grand atmosphere. A golden Chinese dragon curled on the signboard. Inside was a statue of Lord Guan with a red face. At the entrance was a rock garden built from Taihu stones, with two old men playing chess on top and a small pond below with koi fish.

Staff were arranging fresh green plants by the rock garden.

Mountain, water, and greenery symbolized roots, wealth, and growth — hoping the business would flourish.

Wade ordered Mapo tofu, cold shredded vegetables, stir-fried shrimp, and pan-fried buns. It wasn’t peak time; few customers. Chef Yongfu enthusiastically kept him company.

“Do you like the food? Come often. Bring friends. Soon, fresh seasonal vegetables and meats will arrive — you have to try them. The flavor will be amazing.”

Wade regretted, “I won’t be around much. I’m going on a long trip.”

Yongfu was even more regretful. “Too bad. The rich, oily dishes are always here, but the seasonal freshness only lasts a short while.”

At checkout, Wade got a discount and a potted peace lily.

A small blue-and-white porcelain pot, soil-planted, with lush green leaves and two white spathes rising like small flames — resembling the crown of the Goddess of Mercy.

Yongfu said, “Extra ones, not valuable, but lucky. You’re going on a trip, right? This is called ‘smooth sailing.’ It’s a wish for a safe journey.”

Wade took it with a wry smile. “Isn’t it inconvenient to carry?”

“Not to carry. Leave it at home and have a friend care for it. Plants are mystical — if you’re safe, it grows well.”

He lowered his voice. “Going away is like flying a kite. You need something at home to hold the string — pulling it, pulling it, until it brings you back.”

Wade thanked him.

The pot was small. Wade held it in his palm, took a tram, then walked back to his apartment.

Yongfu’s words brought many thoughts.

— Back then, it was also a long trip. A smuggler’s boat drifted across the sea. The kite string must have broken long ago. That’s why he didn’t miss home; and home didn’t miss him.

— Maybe it was fate. There are two of them in this line of work. The peace lily happened to have two white spathes.

Back at the bar, Erinn took the peace lily, turning it over.

“Me? Take care of flowers? I don’t know how. What if I kill it?”

“If it dies, I die. Your call.”

Erinn was annoyed. “Nonsense.”

She placed the lily beside the jellyfish tank, resting her chin on her hand, studying it. The flower petals glowed pale green in the tank’s light. The moon jellyfish floated slowly, almost limping.

Wade said, “Taking care of plants isn’t hard. Take care of the jellyfish the same way.”

# Chapter 9

A few hours before departure, Wade packed and went to a nearby sauna for a Finnish bath.

Under a low wall at the entrance, many towel-clad men gathered, smoking and drinking beer. Wade stuffed his bag into a locker, quickly showered, then entered the sauna.

The air was hot and humid, surprisingly crowded. Pale, loose muscles glistened in the steamy wood-scented haze.

He picked a spot and soon sweat drenched him. One by one, people left, unable to stand the heat.

After a while, a familiar figure entered, carrying soaked birch branches.

Wade raised his arm to signal.

Mwenda sat next to him and handed over half the branches, waving them exaggeratedly to slap Wade’s body — and herself too. Nearby people seemed annoyed, moving away or going to other rooms.

The two of them selfishly took up most of the sauna.

They exchanged locker keys on their wrists. Mwenda gave instructions:

— “Everything’s arranged. I’ll take your luggage to the car. You’ll exit with Ms. Lee through the back door, walk down the driveway a bit. The car will be parked in the woods by the roadside.”

— “The Saudis have two lines: a public one in Mogadishu, Somalia’s capital, with a negotiation team who do interviews and press conferences; and a secret one — Ms. Lee’s — which they’re keeping quiet to avoid mistakes.”

— “They’ve accepted your suggestion and booked tickets according to the canceled itinerary. Nobody knows you’ve changed plans. You leave today.”

— “The locker has a phone and new SIM card. Only Mwenda, CoCo, the Saudis, and Tiger Shark know the number. Tiger Shark is nervous after pulling off such a big deal, hiding even more. The meeting place hasn’t been decided — waiting for his notice.”

...

Everything was ready. Wade endured the steam until the limit, then stood up and patted Mwenda’s shoulder. “See you.”

Last time he said “see you,” he was headed to Lapland for four months. This time, it would be shorter.

He went to the cold room, stood under the showerhead, turned it to “all cold.” The cold water poured over his head and face, his pores snapped shut instantly. The extreme chill coursed through him.

He dried off, opened his locker.

First, a card from Mwenda, full of good wishes scented heavily with perfume — probably Eve’s perfume that Mwenda had sneaked on.

Then a full set of new clothes, from underwear and socks to coats and belts. Custom-made, no brand, tailored precisely to his preferences and measurements.

Wade dressed, dried his hair, then grabbed a gift bag from the locker.

The bag wasn’t sealed. Inside were travel funds in dollars, euros, and krona; a phone; an invitation; and a thin iron mask shaped like a man’s face, which sounded metallic when flicked.

—

The third time at Kimberly’s place.

Night had fallen. The villa was brightly lit inside and out. Music drifted like fragrant mist down the sloping driveway.

Wade stood in the black shadows of trees, listening.

The song was old — Guns N’ Roses’ classic, “Don’t Cry.” Their music was angry and intense, like it wanted to smash the world. But this song was mournful, tender, and haunting — said to have made millions cry.

The broken-hearted always have their songs.

Closer, the music mixed with laughter, loud chatter, and instrument tuning, forming a chaotic stew.

At the door, someone stopped him, asking for the invitation.

When Wade handed it over, he noticed the ticket bore the poster of Verdi’s opera *Un Ballo in Maschera*, with a line of lyrics by Leon Russell:

*In this lonely masquerade, do we truly feel joy?*

The Saudis were meticulous — even their cover party had a finely made invitation.

He put on the mask and entered the hall. The lighting was dim. Shadows, noise, and colorful characters mingled near the self-serve bar: a WWII pilot with broken goggles, Darth Vader from Star Wars, and Marilyn Monroe wearing a golden wig.

Looking up, Kimberly leaned on the second-floor railing, dressed in a silver-gray strapless satin gown with a train, wearing a diamond shoulder chain. Behind her, a black curtain hung from the ceiling, blocking the upstairs rooms. On the curtain was Batman, his winged cape fluttering.

She held a slim black lady’s cigarette but rarely smoked it. Occasionally, she tapped it on the railing, invisible ashes falling onto a small Hitler mustache below.

Wade went upstairs. Passing Kimberly, she lowered her eyes and said, “Enter through the cape.”

The Batman cape wasn’t a full curtain. Wade parted a slit and slipped inside.

Behind the curtain was much quieter. A nearby room’s door was open, light spilling out.

Wade went over and saw Saed sitting on a sofa. A tall woman stood nearby, wearing only a tight tank top and shorts, her curves defined, hips narrow.

She was trying on a silver Venetian princess half-mask, decorated with filigree, pearls, diamonds, ribbons, and feathers.

Seeing Wade, she gasped in surprise.

Wade remembered his iron mask and took it off. The woman removed hers too.

She was a young Eastern European, very beautiful, with hazel eyes and shoulder-length black hair curled at the ends.

Wade said, “Very similar.”

The woman was sharp and immediately understood: “Not exactly the same. Eastern Asians are slimmer. I’ve been hungry for two days...”

She pointed at her slightly round shoulders. “Still not thinner. That’s why Ms. Lee picked a gown with shoulder chains. The light is dim; there’s a mask and decoration. I don’t think anyone will notice...”

While they spoke, Kimberly came in and motioned the woman to go change clothes inside.

Wade sat beside Saed, who handed him a paper.

“Boat ticket.”

Wade squinted. It was clearly torn from some notebook, the edges ragged like a dog had chewed it. The ink line drawn across made it impossible to tell if it was Finnish or English.

Saed whispered, “You’ll go to Turku harbor, take a boat to Stockholm, Sweden. There’s the second largest airport in Scandinavia.”

Wade folded the “ticket” and put it in his inner pocket.

Taking the boat was the slowest.

The Turku-Stockholm ferry took about ten hours — slowest and cheapest.

Saed nodded. “Time doesn’t matter. Secrecy is key.”

“When will it arrive?”

“As soon as possible. Valid for today and tomorrow. Once in Turku, go to the oil harbor and find a man named Tapio. He’ll arrange things.”

“And after Sweden?”

Saed smiled wryly. “We’re still weighing options... It’s hard to pick a perfectly safe route. We’ll let you know.”

True enough, Wade knew that Africa’s wars had only recently subsided. Even now, small flare-ups happened.

Sierra Leone fought a decade-long civil war over diamonds. Hollywood made the movie *Blood Diamond* about it. North and South Sudan fought over oil fields; Congo over gold mines. Mwenda’s homeland, the Huka, were caught in ethnic hatred. Somalia’s no better...

Wars damaged infrastructure. Post-war, many countries don’t even have national airlines.

Wade frowned. “Fly from Sweden to Kenya?”

Saed shook his head. “Kenya’s in the south. Somalia is a narrow triangle. Pirates’ stronghold is in northern Bosaso. From recent intel, negotiations might take place on the high seas...”

The inner room’s door opened.

The Eastern European woman came out first, dazzling in jewels. Her mask’s feathers trembled, the fish-eye glass so realistic it could pass for pearls.

Then Kimberly appeared, no longer in an evening gown but wearing a military green hooded canvas jacket, black jeans, and white sneakers. She looked more comfortable — softened, like she’d washed away the makeup.

The wheels of a suitcase clicked. She glanced back at Wade. “Could you...”

Wade stood, ready. He was prepared — she shouldn’t have to carry her suitcase on this journey.

When he got close, his head almost exploded.

That beast had to be at least 30 inches.

It could fit a person inside.

He’d only seen luggage that big at international airports, guessing the owner packed pots, pans, steamers, and strainers.

This trip would involve many transfers, not always by car. CoCo had said some jungle paths only allowed bicycles; some places required camels. He’d have to carry that suitcase all the way?

The suitcase started to slide outward. Wade’s reflexes kicked in; he pressed his knee against it.

Mistakes had to be stopped at the start.

Kimberly looked at him strangely. Wade smiled.

“Ms. Lee, do you really need to bring so much?”

“Essentials.”

The White Robes and the Eastern European woman glanced over, puzzled. Wade switched to Chinese — all Chinese here — “internal matters,” no need for outsiders to watch.

“Ms. Lee, would you mind getting a backpack? I can help you slim down the luggage.”

Behind the suitcase, he was firm.

His own bag was light as a kite. He could respect a woman’s heavier luggage, but not this much.

They’d be traveling together for many days. Either the east wind would overpower the west, or vice versa.

He wasn’t a White Robe, didn’t need to rely on her. If he gave in at the start with no principles, she might turn into a great roc, soaring high and impossible to bring down.

Kimberly looked at him a long time. Wade kept smiling, unmoved.

Finally, she went back inside to get a bag.

Wade sighed and dropped the suitcase. He unzipped it all the way.

What he saw made him swear silently inside.
