# Chapter 52

When Kimberly finished tidying up, Wade had just hung up the phone. His expression was grim.

Concerned, Kimberly asked, “Is the wound hurting? Any bad reaction? If you feel uncomfortable, you have to tell me.”

Wade said, “It’s too stuffy in here.”

Stuffy? Kimberly glanced back at the wide-open door.

Was it really stuffy, or was the phone call just... stifling?

She hesitated, then asked, “Who was on the phone?”

“Reindeer. He talked about some arrangements later on. I wasn’t interested.”

Wade propped himself up on his arms and sat up on the bed. Kimberly hurried over to support him, but Wade smiled, “It’s nothing. The injury’s on my shoulder; it’s not like I can’t move.”

He walked to the door and stood still.

The wound wasn’t painless—it hurt a lot—but he felt it wasn’t enough. If it hurt a bit more, maybe he wouldn’t have the energy to think about those sudden, frustrating problems.

His gaze fell on the wooden ladder by the wall leading up to the roof. This guest room had a rooftop terrace.

“I’m going up to sit for a while,” he said.

Kimberly was at a loss for words. “Wade, you’re injured…”

Wade always found a way to persuade her: “It’s really stuffy inside. Up there, the view’s better, the air’s fresher, and it’s more comfortable. Besides, standing high up and seeing far, with my gun, it’s like a sentry post, right? If anything happens, I’ll be prepared.”

The ladder was narrow. Kimberly went back inside to get him an umbrella. When she opened it, Wade didn’t wait for her or say anything—he was already climbing up.

Kimberly stood there for a moment, then returned inside to pack the sliced vegetables back into the basket. When she lifted it, it felt heavy, making her wrist ache.

As she went out, she said, “I’m going to cook now.”

The rain was pouring. Wade probably didn’t hear her and didn’t reply.

She held the umbrella, stepping through shallow puddles across the yard. At the door, the innkeeper came out to help carry the basket.

Kimberly handed it over and glanced back at the roof. She could vaguely see Wade sitting under the umbrella.

Curious, the innkeeper looked through the basket at the vegetables wrapped in large leaves and asked, “Your knife skills are great. Do you cook often?”

Kimberly said, “No, it’s my first time cooking for him.”

Maybe the last time, too.

---

Wade caressed his gun, listening to the rain thumping on the umbrella. He didn’t even know what he was thinking.

Until a blurry figure appeared in his sight.

It was pouring rain; almost no one was on the street. Only that person, holding an umbrella, walked past, turned down the street, and then entered the inn’s front door.

Wade grabbed his monocular and looked.

It was the scar-faced man, wearing sunglasses, pants rolled up, carrying a paper package wrapped in a plastic bag under his arm.

Wade chuckled. What kind of weather is this, still wearing sunglasses?

He raised his gun, aimed, and without hesitation, pulled the trigger.

Mud splashed by the scar-faced man’s right foot. From above, it looked like a small firecracker had exploded. The man stopped, looked up at Wade, and stood still for a long time, hesitating before moving forward again.

Wade shifted his gun to the other side and fired again.

This time, mud splashed by the scar-faced man’s left foot.

Wade thought the sound of gunfire in the rain was strange—the shots sounded like splashes, scattering in all directions, then pressed down by the dense rain, flowing into the drainage ditch outside the inn.

He bent down and blew on the muzzle. When he looked up again, the scar-faced man bit the paper package in his mouth, threw away the umbrella, hugged his head with both hands, and continued walking toward him.

Wade didn’t shoot again. After a while, he heard heavy footsteps on the wooden ladder. The scar-faced man climbed up, threw the paper package on the table, then sat in the other chair.

He was soaked through. In front of Wade, he removed his sunglasses and wiped the dripping clothes’ edge.

Wade looked away.

He guessed the eyes behind the scar-faced man’s sunglasses must be injured, but he hadn’t expected the wound to be so severe. Besides the sunglasses, there was no other cover—where the eye should have been was a sunken, gruesome blade wound. Anyone would find it horrifying.

After wiping, the scar-faced man put the sunglasses back on, as if knowing Wade’s thoughts.

“I was cut. When we fled, we were chased by mobs wielding knives. Running, running, suddenly more came ahead. We didn’t know whether to go forward or back. In the chaos, a blade came down. I fell, thinking I was dead.”

He laughed bitterly. “But I survived. My family, though, all dead. Sixteen people. We found fourteen bodies. One son, three years old then, never found. Still missing.”

Wade said nothing. From the front yard, a crooked chimney began to smoke. Was Kimberly cooking?

The scar-faced man continued.

“Last night, we got news your friend was asking around about us. That made me think maybe there was a misunderstanding between us before.”

“Between us?”

The scar-faced man smiled, first pointing to himself, then Wade. “Between us.”

Finally, he pointed to the front yard. “Not including her.”

Wade’s eyes narrowed. He grabbed his gun and pressed the muzzle firmly against the scar-faced man’s forehead.

The scar-faced man’s tone was calm. “I’m here to negotiate. Don’t worry, no one will touch her now. I can guarantee that. Besides, even if you kill me, it’s useless. I have companions.”

Negotiation? That word kept coming up. Strange how after all the violence and bloodshed, suddenly they want to sit down calmly to negotiate. Where were they before?

“We tried to tell your friend some things. Please ask him to pass it on—Mr. Wade, I think you already know who we are.”

“Sorry for treating you like an enemy before. When we first met, you were already close with Ms. Lee—not like a detached bodyguard.”

The first time?

Wade lowered his gun.

He remembered then, in front of the scar-faced man and the AK guy, he was cozying up to Kimberly, even saying, “You were wild last night. I was crazy for you.”

“Especially after the negotiation, you were still with her. We thought you were on the same side, so we had to include dealing with you in our plan.”

Wade asked, “What evidence do you have that Kimberly is a war criminal?”

The scar-faced man smiled. “Maybe you think only those who incite, plan, or start wars are war criminals. But to us, no matter if you’re a Huka or not, if you committed unforgivable crimes against the Kwatis during that catastrophe, you are.”

He reached out, tore the plastic off the paper package, opened it, and handed Wade a photo.

It was a picture of three people: two middle-aged white men and Kimberly. The man in the middle had his arm around Kimberly’s shoulder.

Kimberly wore a ponytail and smiled faintly. Tiger Shark was wrong—she was much thinner then than now.

The scar-faced man pointed to the other man. “This is Jeremy, a Frenchman.”

Then to the middle man, “This is Serge. Did you notice his hand resting on Ms. Lee’s shoulder?”

He handed a second photo. “This is a close-up of the first.”

Wade stared at the photo, focusing on the hand’s close-up: there was a bite mark on the webbing of the hand.

“We sent this hand to Ms. Lee. I think she already knew long ago who was coming for her—and why.”

Wade said, “Kimberly received a medal from your president. She protected 175 Kwatis’ lives.”

He felt the defense was weak, having to pull out grand words like “president” and “medal” for her.

The scar-faced man replied, “If the truth is twisted, even the president can be deceived.”

“We have a list. A total of 292 Kwatis entered the safe zone. But when the Kwatis Liberation Front fought back, only 175 remained.”

“Mr. Wade, ask Ms. Lee where the other 117 went.”

Wade pushed the photos aside. “Finished? Two photos, some numbers, and you want to convict her?”

The scar-faced man sneered, “Yeah, hard to accept at first. After all, she looks fine, right? Pretty, smart, oh, and a great actor—leading the front line of the righteous struggle, writing great social commentaries.”

Wade stared at him. “Friend, say what you mean. Don’t go off-topic.”

The scar-faced man laughed loudly. “Mr. Wade, haven’t you noticed Ms. Lee’s actions are very deliberate and planned?”

“Her commentaries are famous, but have you read all her earlier ones? Her early style was mild and smooth, then suddenly sharp, bold, and eye-catching—right after Jeremy died and ‘Hand of God’ was formed.”

“Don’t you think that timing is suspicious? Someone with a guilty conscience closely watching Mwenda’s moves, smelling danger, then hurriedly covering themselves with layers of excuses…”

Wade interrupted, “What do you want me to do?”

The scar-faced man bowed slightly.

“Our ‘Hand of God’ is mainly made up of the most unfortunate refugees who survived but lost their families, living almost without attachments. Their only support is revenge.”

“You probably noticed, we’re not as professional or well-trained as you. We suffered in the last two clashes. AK is still in the hospital. Yesterday you injured one of our companions; the hired sniper was also shot…”

He glanced at the bandage on Wade’s shoulder. “Not dead, but injured worse than you.”

“Until last night, after getting the news, we realized if Mr. Wade expressed a stance, things could have been resolved more peacefully, avoiding unnecessary casualties.”

“Express a stance?”

The scar-faced man turned to look at the smoking chimney.

“Mr. Wade, your car is in the yard. No one will stop you. You can leave anytime. But Ms. Lee must stay. Whatever she did, she must pay the price.”

Wade laughed. “Judges listen to both sides. You expect me to leave based on your one-sided story?”

The scar-faced man was prepared. “I can give you time to ask her. We received accusations against her and investigated. Not afraid of you asking. But Mr. Wade, we’ve shown goodwill. Please give a clear answer: if it’s true, will you promise not to interfere?”

Wade was silent for a long time, then nodded.

The scar-faced man sighed. “How much time do you need?”

“Give me… a day.”

---

Before leaving, the scar-faced man left the two photos, saying they might be useful during confrontation.

Wade didn’t move, coldly watching the splashed water gradually soak the photos.

The scar-faced man brought a wealth of information. At this moment, though, despite so many thoughts, memories, and deductions he could make, Wade didn’t. Only when the photos were nearly soaked through did he suddenly snatch one.

Kimberly was really thin then, probably because of the ponytail. She looked especially petite, standing widest among the three. She smiled, but her eyes were hollow—not like the other two, who were openly happy, even flashing peace signs.

Only when dusk fell did he remember to return to the room.

Candles were lit. The table was moved next to the bed, with several dishes laid out: tomato soup, beef stir-fried with green peppers, lettuce and potato salad, and an egg crepe.

The colors were lively and beautiful—but the food was already cold.

Wade smiled and asked Kimberly, sitting nearby, “Why didn’t you call me?”

Kimberly said nothing. She got up, pulled him, almost pushing him onto the bed, saying, “Don’t move.”

She unwrapped the bandage on his shoulder. Wade looked down and noticed it was almost soaked from the rain, with blood seeping through.

He explained, “The rain was heavy…”

Kimberly smiled. “From now on, if you’re upset or angry, you can throw things, swear, or lose your temper—but don’t abuse your body. If the wound gets infected, it hurts you. If there are complications, it’s you who suffers. I’m only saying this once. You can listen or not.”

She stopped talking, not even looking at him, carefully applying medicine and wrapping the bandage anew.

Suddenly, Wade couldn’t control himself. He embraced her tightly with one arm and buried his head in her chest.

After a moment of silence, Kimberly smiled.

She looked down and gently stroked his hair. “Wade, let’s eat properly first.”

“I’ve put so much effort into this. Don’t waste it.”

“At the table, no talking about business. If you want to talk, we’ll open a bottle of wine and chat slowly after dinner.”

# Chapter 53

The meal tasted bland in the mouth, but inside, emotions swirled.

Still, Wade remembered every topic they discussed: the flavors, the cooking time, the seasoning. They both agreed Yongfu Lin was truly skilled as a chef. Kimberly even complained about the high flame stir-frying, which left an oily smell on her clothes.

She turned sideways, smiling, and let him smell. Wade lowered his head and caught a faint scent of firewood and the saltiness of cooking.

For a moment, he was dazed. Many women had sprayed perfume on him before, but only this one carried the smell of home-cooked food.

After dinner, Kimberly quickly showered. When she came out, she wore the shirt he’d altered. Her hair was half-damp, tied up, with a few strands falling on her neck and shoulders. Water droplets ran down, wetting the skin. The small garnet on her platinum necklace gleamed brightly against her porcelain skin.

Wade asked, “Aren’t you cold like that?”

Kimberly shook her head, stacked the dishes back into the basket. Wade tried to help, but she refused, eventually carrying it herself to the front yard.

Wade kept watching her. The basket must have been heavy, weighing down her shoulder. As she opened the umbrella, she suddenly turned back and called, “Wade.”

The outdoor light filtered through the heavy rain and the faded yellow umbrella, casting a glow over her. A few strands of hair lifted in the light. Her smile was gentle, and in her eyes, there was no one else but him.

By the doorframe, she was like a painting in a frame. Wade smiled. If only time could stop at this moment—no past, no future.

Before the fireworks cooled, he wanted to hold onto this fleeting eternal moment.

---

When she returned, she held a bottle of opened red wine and two wine glasses.

“No brand,” she said. “You’re injured. Drink less.”

After putting down the wine, she sat across the table. Lifting her shirt, she pulled a pack of cigarettes from her panty waistband.

“Couldn’t hold it before, so I stuffed it here. They say it’s local tobacco with a hint of herbs.”

She took one out, lit it with the candle, her hand steady, not looking at him. Her thick eyelashes fluttered, giving her an aura of impenetrable melancholy.

The scene felt familiar.

Wade recalled: at their formal first meeting, in the interview room, she was just like this.

Kimberly inhaled deeply, raised her head, and slowly exhaled smoke.

Suddenly, she laughed. “Falling in love is strange. You don’t even recognize yourself. It’s like dreaming. Some are lucky to dream longer—that’s a lifetime.”

She paused, then softly said, “But I’m unlucky. Always just a little short. Back then… I stayed behind with three colleagues.”

---

Three men and one woman. Besides her, the other three were experienced. After the UN convoy left, they acted immediately.

— To put on a show.

They had to raise international organization flags—more, bigger, and more visible. In chaos, some flags and symbols were worth more than lives.

— Register the people.

After declaring they wouldn’t remove the Kwatis, many terrified refugees fled. About 200 remained, all registered.

— Inventory food and supplies.

With so many people, food and water were a big problem. The school had little stock; at most, it could last a day or two before running out.

...

The four held a meeting, clearly dividing tasks. Since women were more vulnerable in chaos, Kimberly’s role was limited to staying behind, calming refugees, medical aid, and internal management, with no external duties.

One man handled security and patrol, wearing leftover UN gear—vest, helmet, gun—almost indistinguishable. Huka men with knives lurked nearby but dared not approach.

Two others drove out for fieldwork: to buy food, coordinate with other scattered safe zones, and document events as witnesses of history.

After the meeting, Kimberly felt reassured. Everyone was optimistic. It wasn’t a closed era anymore; the whole world was watching. Surely the international community would intervene soon. Who would let such atrocities continue?

The next two days’ fieldwork progress was encouraging.

— They successfully bought flour, salt, potatoes, even some black tea.

— There were other safe zones. A French priest’s church sheltered over 3,000 Kwatis. The Red Cross operated normally, treating many wounded.

— They even met BBC reporters. Some photos had been sent back and would soon be public worldwide.

...

But hope gradually flickered out like a candle.

Emergency response was like disaster relief, with a 72-hour golden window. If the international community didn’t act decisively in the first days, it was seen as tacit approval, emboldening perpetrators.

Day after day passed.

Sun rose and stars set. Sometimes, Kimberly stared at her watch’s hands, feeling Mwenda was forgotten by the world.

Food brought back became less and less. Once, the car’s window was smashed. Each outing added more damage. Outside, the Huka were increasingly wild, setting up roadblocks and provoking them.

Broadcasts ran nonstop. After early incitement, they switched to messages like, “Quick, we found a large group of cockroaches near XX. Huka warriors, grab your knives and come!”—like a deadly game of calling friends to kill.

Kimberly’s nerves grew tense. She dreamed of the school’s name broadcasted, then countless Huka men with knives swarming from all sides...

One day, two colleagues sent out on fieldwork didn’t return.

Unease spread like a tide in the safe zone. After waiting all night, the security guy went out to look.

Kimberly waited another day, highly anxious.

---

She paused here, silent for a moment, flicking ash from her cigarette.

Wade asked, “Then?”

Kimberly smiled faintly. “Then they never came back. Damn it. Like a joke, suddenly, four became just me.”

“I stayed awake all night, staring into the dark, thinking, I’m done. No fieldwork, no security, no food. At dawn, if one more Huka came to test us, this safe zone would fall.”

But there’s always a way out. At dawn, she suddenly heard a car and people banging on the locked school gate, shouting, “Anyone there? Please open the door!”

“Through the window, I saw a white man shaking the gate. I was so relieved, like seeing a compatriot.”

It was Jeremy and Serge. They drove a van with a “Peace Aid” logo.

The van entered the yard. The canvas cover was lifted, revealing about ten refugees covered in blood.

“Jeremy said they were volunteers who had stayed behind. Their safe zone was breached, and they rescued these refugees on the way.”

Jeremy brought bad news.

First, the situation was worsening. The international community was collectively silent, with no short-term plans to intervene.

Second, safe zones were no longer safe. Just in two days, two had been breached.

Third, on the road, they heard two foreigners hid Kwatis refugees in their car, tried to break through roadblocks. Dozens of Huka vehicles chased them. The broadcasts called more to surround them. The car crashed off a bridge and exploded.

...

Kimberly felt those two foreigners might have been her colleagues.

Wade asked, “How did Jeremy and Serge know about the school?”

Kimberly said, “They said they met my colleague on the road, who pointed them here. They told my colleague about the crash, but he insisted on checking it out.”

She raised her glass, drank most of it, licking the foam from her lips. “My colleague is still missing.”

---

She didn’t even have time to mourn her colleague before discussing new plans with Jeremy and Serge.

Jeremy proposed: in extraordinary times, extraordinary measures. With safe zones falling one after another, old methods no longer worked. They needed new tactics.

“Jeremy said most of the mobs weren’t true fanatics. Most were opportunists who could be bribed. He heard some safe zones were safer because their leaders paid military commanders, who secretly made things easier.”

Wade asked, “Did you have money then?”

“No, but the Kwatis did.”

“Did you raise money from the Kwatis?”

Kimberly smiled. “Yes. I was in charge of internal management. Refugees trusted only me. I had to do it.”

The Kwatis fled in haste, carrying little cash. Trapped in the school, with no way to spend money, they eagerly gave what they had to buy convenience—a considerable sum. The money worked quickly.

“Jeremy and the others went out once, brought back lots of food and even beer. Their plan was to open a route, bribe all roadblocks on it, so travel would be smooth, and nearby Huka would be paid off and not harass the school. Then the safe zone would truly be protected.”

Kimberly drained her glass. “It worked better than my previous colleagues’ plans. I thought Jeremy was smart and flexible—that’s survival of the fittest.”

“They gradually rescued more refugees. The highest total reached 292.”

Wade asked, “Why ‘highest’? Did the number decrease later?”

---

New refugees inevitably brought rumors from outside.

Most were pessimistic: another safe zone was breached. Foreign faces no longer guaranteed safety. Volunteers died. The international community kept meeting but couldn’t agree, delaying action. Meanwhile, people died every second here.

Some news was uplifting: some escaped through waterways to Uda. At times like these, safe zones couldn’t be trusted. The safest place was outside Mwenda.

Jeremy verified this: a big river ran between Mwenda and Uda. There were boats, but with bribes at checkpoints and on the boats, passage was costly—a refugee profiteering scheme.

Wade was silent, recalling what CoCo had said.

— I remember once the river suddenly turned red. Many went to watch. Some even pulled bodies from the water.

— Later, they heard refugees tried to escape by river but had no boats. Huka caught them at the shore... slashing...

Wade asked, “Were there really boats on the river?”

Kimberly smiled. “I don’t know. I never left the safe zone, never saw anyone killed. It’s all hearsay.”

But the rumors spread quickly. Many refugees came to ask Kimberly. She consulted Jeremy, who said it was worth a try but too dangerous. Only tell a few people at first—no more than five.

Wade interrupted, “From start to finish, you were the one speaking out?”

Kimberly smiled nonchalantly. “Yes. I raised the money and spread the news. The others were out in the field. Internal management was my job.”

Wade was silent, then softly said, “Silly girl.”

Kimberly laughed. “Yeah, now I’m clever. Too bad I can’t give some of it back to my old self.”

Money was collected quickly. Some used bankbooks as collateral; others gave addresses of valuables for Jeremy to take care of. The Kwatis were relatively wealthy. Though survival came at a high price, they risked all.

The first group of five left at midnight. At dawn, Jeremy and Serge returned, signaling victory from afar.

Kimberly’s eyes moistened, relieved.

“Jeremy told me not to publicize the news. Too many mouths. If leaked, this lifeline would be cut off. So I was careful, limiting each evacuation to about ten people, arranging friends and family to leave together. If asked why fewer people were leaving, we said they were relocated to nearby safe zones.”

“They did this five or six times. One morning, as usual, I waited. Jeremy and Serge came back safely, as always.”

“Then they rested. Jeremy walked ahead, wearing a floral shirt. I noticed a bloodstain on his back.”

She looked into Wade’s eyes. “So I stood still. They all went inside, but I stayed put. I started recalling how they appeared, and then… I became afraid.”

# Chapter 54

Kimberly didn’t sleep all night.

She repeatedly told herself not to doubt her companions. That bloodstain was just an accident. But terrifying thoughts kept rolling over her like waves, plunging into darker depths.

At breakfast, she casually asked Jeremy if she could go on a trip with the car—so she could have firsthand experience for reports, interviews, and documentation after the war ended.

Jeremy refused, saying it was too dangerous for a woman, and with the three gone, the safe zone was a vacuum. Any mishap would be disastrous.

Kimberly smiled at Wade. “I thought and thought and came up with a bad idea.”

One midnight, she had refugees cover for her and sneaked onto the car.

Wade asked, “Didn’t you think it was dangerous?”

Kimberly looked distracted. “I did, but I couldn’t help it. I didn’t know what would happen once the car took people out. Maybe I was still optimistic because I’d never left the safe zone. My colleagues said BBC reporters were still moving around outside…I thought I was a foreigner, an international volunteer… So I snuck on.”

That journey was unforgettable.

From the safe zone gate, tension gripped the car. Ten or so refugees around silently prayed, crossing themselves repeatedly. The silence was eerie. Only the sound of the car scraping the ground and the engine echoed, matching the heartbeat’s rhythm, suffocating.

Mwenda’s nights shouldn’t have been so dead. Before the massacre, you’d see people drinking, dancing, hear singing and TV.

Now, it was a ghost town, the stench occasionally drifting. Near roadblocks, the Huka shouted and laughed drunkenly.

After some time, the car slowed. Outside, wind blew, and water flowed nearby. Lights suddenly turned on. Kimberly’s scalp tingled. She was used to dark nights; the safe zone forbade any light to avoid attracting unwanted eyes.

The canvas was suddenly pulled back. The closest person screamed and was dragged out. Before she could react, Kimberly was yanked out, struggling and screaming. Then someone grabbed her hair, tilted her face up, and shouted, “This one’s not Kwati!”

Silence fell.

In that silence, Kimberly saw everything.

They were at a riverside camp near the woods. No boats, but armed Huka men were there, some drinking around a fire. Jeremy and Serge laughed, opening beers, foam spraying on their faces.

On the other side, Kwatis from the car were dragged into the dark woods by rough Huka men.

That shout, “Not Kwati,” stunned everyone. A Kwati woman seized the moment, broke free, screaming, “Kim! Save me!”

The Huka chased and, just as she neared Kimberly, raised a knife.

Kimberly shuddered. Warm blood blurred her vision. Through the crimson haze, she saw the woman on the ground, struggling to lift her head, pointing at Kimberly, saying, “You…”

The woman wore a headscarf. Her eyes were sunken, filled with terror, despair, and fading hope.

Kimberly lost control, furious, charging at the Huka, wanting to tear his face apart. But she was dragged back. Serge said, “What are you crazy for?”

Kimberly, red-eyed, bit Serge’s hand hard.

He cried out and kicked her away. She rolled in pain. Gun cocking sounds rang near her head. The cold muzzle pressed against her forehead but was quickly pulled away. Jeremy said, “Stop. She’s still useful. Let me handle this.”

He grabbed Kimberly by the collar and dragged her into the woods. She stumbled, dragged along.

Deeper in the woods, she froze.

This was a slaughter site, bodies everywhere, swarming flies. A few Huka men finished their work, smoking and glancing sideways at the two.

Jeremy pressed Kimberly forward, pinning her face against a corpse’s cold face.

He said, “Kim, why did you run out? We feed you. You have food and drink. Isn’t that enough? The outside world is cruel.”

Kimberly’s voice was hoarse, tears streaming.

Jeremy said, “Look how many died. Over 100,000 by now. There are countless slaughter sites like this. It’s so hot, and when they rot, who knows if the bones left are Kwati or yours?”

“The safe zone’s doomed. That French priest’s church with 3,000 people is gone. Without me, your safe zone would be history too—I squeeze what I can from them. What’s wrong with that?”

“Kim, I’ll give you a choice. One: behave, clean up, go back, keep volunteering and cooperate. If lucky, you’ll still be a refugee hero and live your life in Northern Europe. Two: rot here, no one cares about your fate. You’re missing, just a statistic. During war, a few foreigners missing doesn’t matter. How tragic—traveling thousands of miles to volunteer, and gaining nothing—no money, no fame, no life…”

He lifted her up. “So, what do you say?”

Kimberly trembled, blood and tears mixing on her face, lips moving but no words. Jeremy grew impatient and said to the others, “Give this woman to you to play with.”

He pushed Kimberly forward.

The men yelled and lunged. Kimberly screamed hysterically, struggling and crawling. In the chaos, she clung to Jeremy’s leg like her only lifeline and nodded desperately.

Jeremy patted her head. “You’re obedient now?”

Kimberly nodded, tears falling like rain.

What followed was a blur: Jeremy took her back, found her new clothes. She hid in the car to change. Halfway through, nausea hit. She vomited leaning on the window until bile came out.

Jeremy combed her hair, wiped her face. “Don’t look like a corpse. Smile, just once.”

She forced a smile, reminding herself: smile, smile.

Jeremy finally approved and pushed her to the fire, handed her a beer. “Come on, cheers.”

Kimberly forced a smile. Opposite her, a burly Huka man smiled too, clinking his beer with hers.

A flash went off. She instinctively turned. Jeremy held a camera, praising her, “Natural smile.”

She didn’t know when the rain stopped or started again. Kimberly poured herself another drink and said to Wade, “I have nothing to explain. I did nod then.”

At dawn, they returned to the school. Some refugees waited. Kimberly got out, facing them with a forced smile, saying, “It’s okay, all good.”

Jeremy said, “Look, Kim even bought new clothes. People from Uda brought small goods to sell. They started shopping before their butts hit the seats.”

The refugees laughed. Kimberly smiled, then softly said, “I’m going to rest.”

She went to her room, barely closing the door before collapsing.

Sunlight pierced her eyes. Somehow, she found the strength to get up, blocking the windows with everything, taping them shut until the room was dark.

She curled up on the floor, expressionless and tearless.

The cigarette burned down, almost burning her hand. Wade wanted to take it, but she flipped her hand and clenched the butt tightly.

She asked, “Do you know what I was thinking then?”

“I didn’t have time to hate anyone. I was too weak. When people despair, they need dreams to hold onto.”

“I stared at the door, hoping someone would come to save me. My beloved—whether a hero or not—if he could descend from the sky and save me then, how wonderful.”

Wade reached for her hand, but Kimberly pulled away. “No, don’t drag this out. I’m not telling you for comfort. Just listen.”

She lay there through the hazy day.

At dusk, Serge knocked impatiently. “Kim, not showing up all day makes people suspicious.”

Kimberly got up, grabbed a basin, went to the washroom, splashed water on her face, then looked in the mirror.

She noticed a new mole near her collarbone.

She leaned closer and touched it, realizing it wasn’t a mole but a dried blood drop she hadn’t wiped off.

She washed it away.

Kimberly whispered, “But strangely, after washing it off, I panicked. Since then, I can’t stop touching that spot, feeling like the blood is still there and must be cleaned.”

Wade’s gaze fell on the small garnet on her platinum collarbone chain. It was tiny, like a cinnabar mole—more like a bloodstain.

Kimberly gently rubbed the garnet. “You don’t know my habit. If I don’t wear this necklace, I can’t stop…”

She was silent for a moment.

After that night, it was as if nothing happened. The safe zone’s clock hands kept moving steadily, unaware when it would stop.

She became afraid to talk to people, scared to see so many hopeful faces.

She found things to do. The school had plenty of leftover pencils and paper. She started drawing. At first, her drawings were poor but gradually improved. She didn’t need models. Faces, lines, details were etched in her eyes, seen whether eyes were open or closed.

Sometimes refugees came to watch, even kindly shielding others from bothering her. “Kim is drawing. Wait till she’s free.”

Sometimes, when unavoidable, she’d lower her eyes and whisper, “No rush. Take your time. Or you can be next.”

When lives were at stake, how could there be no urgency? People begged, “Kim, let me go first. I have a child…”

Her boldest act was slashing the van’s tire. Serge found her, didn’t ask a word, slapped her, saying, “Whether you did it or not, it’s on you. Next time, try it again.”

Kimberly drank her glass dry again.

She said, “I don’t know what to do. People are being killed everywhere. Should I let them escape? They’ll die if they run. At least here, they die slower.”

“Sometimes I wish Jeremy and Serge were dead. But ironically, without their dirty deals, the safe zone wouldn’t last a day. I’m useless. Food, water, medicine—I can’t get any.”

She grew more silent, sending people to the “boat” every two or three days. She watched the safe zone’s population dwindle, crossing off names from the registry. Sometimes she dreamed the safe zone was a huge swamp, everyone sinking day by day.

She waited for the day everyone would drown.

But the turning point came unexpectedly. After over a month of darkness—not because the international community finally ended endless meetings, but because the Kwatis Liberation Front fought back.

You can’t rely on anyone. You have to save yourself.

When the Liberation Front’s artillery boomed outside the city, the safe zone still had 175 refugees. Jeremy and Serge wore new faces.

They stopped fieldwork, guarding the gate with their stored supplies, patrolling, standing watch, overturning Huka attempting to climb over walls, even getting injured.

Refugees thanked Jeremy tearfully. He replied, “It’s nothing. The most important thing is everyone survived.”

But they started grumbling about Kimberly, saying she seemed changed—only drawing, silent when asked about matters.

The day finally came when the locked iron gate was opened without worry. Refugees embraced Kwati soldiers. War reporters snapped photos everywhere. Jeremy pulled Kimberly and Serge in front of the camera, meaningfully saying, “A souvenir.”

After the photo, Kimberly told Jeremy, “I want to go home.”

Two days later, Jeremy personally escorted her to the newly repaired airport. The runway was dirt-filled, no walls, just a big open space with many planes landing. Reporters who had left scrambled for the first news after peace.

The roaring engines stirred the air, ruffling her hair. Jeremy cupped her face.

“You’re so beautiful, girl. When you go back, forget all this. Plenty of men will like you, and you’ll have money.”

He leaned close to her ear. “We put a lot of money in your account.”

“Behave yourself. We have plenty of evidence—your photos, refugees’ diaries, letters not sent. If things come to light, you’re the main culprit.”

“We’re all in the same boat. Help each other. Don’t wish me dead. If I’m safe, you’re safe. If I die, you’re not far behind.”

Kimberly said, “You’re not really volunteers, are you?”

Jeremy grinned, showing crooked teeth. “No. We came to Africa for gold. Didn’t find any in the mines but struck it rich here. Miracles never cease, right, Kim?”

The candle burned out. Smoke drifted in the dense darkness.

The rain stopped, only occasional drips fell from the eaves.

Kimberly whispered, “Back in Mwenda, I told myself, going back to Northern Europe would be fine. Just a nightmare to forget. I could start over.”

“Coming back, I realized no. With Mwenda and Northern Europe as illusions to fall back on, coming back meant no fallback at all.”

“I developed serious psychological problems—chaotic life, nightmares, endlessly searching for the UN convoy in dreams. Faces of refugees flashed before me—the ones I sent onto the trucks and those who died before me…”

She looked at Wade, smiling. “I really was unlucky. In that situation, what choice did I have? If I didn’t nod, I’d die right there. If I did, I was an accomplice, a criminal. If ever investigated, I’d be finished.”

Wade didn’t know what to say.

Kimberly suddenly laughed, almost crying. “You believed me, didn’t you? I said it so emotionally, and you just believed it. People like you shouldn’t be judges.”

She lowered her head and lit a cigarette, shaking a match. The flame flickered. Her hand trembled.

She murmured, “Who would believe me? All evidence was made to kill me. Besides, I did compromise.”

Lighting the cigarette, she didn’t smoke it. She placed it on the table, watching the curling smoke rise.

“I knew about the ‘Hand of God’ long ago. Not afraid, not surprised. When I got Serge’s hand, I felt relieved. I was really tired. It was time to end the journey.”

“The only surprise was Tiger Shark hijacking the Sirius and the Saudis finding me. I didn’t care. More time meant helping them negotiate. Less time meant dying on the road. Whatever fate.”

“As for hiring a bodyguard, the Saudis were keen—interviews, selections. I wasn’t enthusiastic.”

“You always wondered why I chose you, right? Now I can answer.”

“Not because I wanted to oppose the Saudis, not because you looked good, or I liked you. When you entered the room, I barely noticed you. I thought the Saudis were boring, and so were you.”

“But you said one thing. Remember?”

“You said if Ms. Lee was seriously flawed or had secrets, better not hire me—I’d quit halfway.”

She looked gently into Wade’s eyes.

“What a coincidence. I do have secrets. I chose you, waiting for this moment—to see how you’d quit when you learned the truth.”
