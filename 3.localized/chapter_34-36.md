# Chapter 34

Wade was surprised himself at how long the kiss lasted.

After all, as a man, the things he took pride in when it came to relationships with women weren't usually about kissing.

As Reindeer put it, a man’s virility either roars on the battlefield of his profession or burns passionately in the bedchamber.

A few years ago, before Reindeer got married and CoCo was still training in Europe, when they were all young and reckless, their entertainment was wild—at least the more refined ones involved staying up all night eating popcorn and drinking cola, watching romantic “action” movies.

After watching too many and growing bored, they switched to fresh romantic films.

With painfully slow progress, they’d wait sixty minutes just for a kiss. CoCo would get so impatient she’d almost take off her pants, yelling at the screen’s male lead, “Can you do it or not? If not, I’ll do it!”

Wade said, “That’s crude!”

Reindeer asked, “Wade, what the hell keeps a kiss going for so long?”

Wade replied, “The director’s orders, probably. They get paid for it.”

By the 120th minute, tragedy struck.

CoCo said, “Wade, no one even got to the bed, and the guy dies for the girl? That’s not reasonable.”

Wade said, “You don’t get it. That’s loyalty!”

He himself thought of relationships like a movie—the ultimate scene is the main event, and everything before is just setup. Lighting, makeup, props—they could be sloppy.

What’s so interesting about kissing? He’d kissed women and men alike—in training, they’d play rowdy games at night. In a radius of ten miles, it was all men. They’d all steel themselves and kiss, then curse each other. Some cursed about unshaven faces, some about bad breath, and others about “Damn it, we agreed mouth to mouth, why’d you stick your tongue out, you freak?”

But now, he was strangely captivated.

Every sensitive nerve was on alert, sensing, noticing, caring about every detail.

—Her body grew heavier and softer in his arms.

—When his tongue lightly traced inside her lips, her neck suddenly tilted back, eyelashes wet, breath quickening.

—When his teeth gently bit her lip, her hand pressed on his chest curled tightly, fingers trembling slightly…

So kissing could be interesting—there was so much to explore.

Kimberly was probably right; he was serious about it.

Serious affection was more interesting than just getting into bed.

Getting into bed was like a wildfire, raging with wild tongues of flame, an earth-shaking battle of life and death.

Serious affection was like watching tender grass sprout, patiently waiting for varying shades of green to dye the distant hills. He used to disdain such things, but now he delighted in every tiny detail.

Then a knock on the door—a police officer said, “Hello, are you there?”

Wade released Kimberly.

She collapsed back into the chair, her chest heaving wildly, the half-open collar revealing pale pink skin. After a moment, she lowered her head and lightly wiped her lips with the back of her hand.

Wade asked, “What’s the matter?”

“My business is done. Since you’re foreigners, the village requires you to fill out a form and sign it.”

Business done? All the disputes lining up at the door were resolved? No wonder it was so quiet outside.

Wade went to open the door.

The officer held a folder and politely handed over the form—it was the one Wade had just carefully marked with a ruler.

A quick glance showed it was the usual: name, nationality, purpose of travel, contact info. The officer had no experience dealing with foreign visitors but was trying his best to be dutiful and follow protocol.

Wade felt hot all over and asked, “Is there water to wash up?”

The officer pointed to several large jars at the corner of the container: “Use whatever you want.”

Wade strode over, lifted the grass cover off one jar, and found a broken-handled plastic scoop. He dipped some water and poured it over his head.

Much better.

The officer looked at him blankly. Wade explained, “I know your water is precious… I’m from Northern Europe, it’s cold there. Here it’s too hot—I can’t take it.”

The officer suddenly understood, and his dark red face showed an apologetic expression, as if the country’s climate was his responsibility. “Yes, it’s pretty hot here… It’s fine, you can use it.”

…

Wade chatted briefly with the officer, filled out the form roughly, asked about the local situation, and even talked about pirates. The officer said, “We rarely have pirates here; they don’t dare come to big villages. Don’t worry. The most famous pirates in the Red Sea are Somali pirates, but they’re far away…”

So confident. Maybe four of them will come tonight, you know?

Wade shook his left arm, occasionally clenching his fist to ease the muscle. From palm to elbow, it tingled and went numb.

Out of the corner of his eye, he saw Kimberly come out silently. She fetched water, went back inside to wash, then returned to hang her washed clothes on the line.

Wade stared at the clothes on the line—she had washed his clothes too.

The officer said something Wade didn’t catch. “Huh?”

“I mean that room,” the officer pointed to the container’s far end, “is my dorm. Inside is just one bed, enough for you to sleep. I asked Ms. Lee; since you’re not married, you might want to sleep separately. I borrowed a palm mat for her.”

Isn’t that a bit… backwards?

Wade confirmed, “I sleep on the bed?”

“Yes. Ms. Lee can sleep in the phone room; the mat is laid on the floor. I stay in the office. Call me if you need anything.”

Got it. Here, men have higher status and get priority.

Wade smiled and patted the officer’s shoulder: “Alright, don’t worry about it. I’ll arrange it.”

—

Kimberly didn’t need his “arrangement.” She didn’t even think there was a bed for him—when he finished washing and entered the room, she was already lying down.

Wade turned off the light, laid the palm mat on the floor, and lay down.

It felt good—lying flat was definitely more comfortable than soaking in seawater.

The container had a small window, with two iron bars welded across. From the window, he could see the clothes swaying on the line.

Suddenly, he remembered Erinn’s words:

—“Don’t you have any plans for the future? You should save some money, marry a girl you like, buy a big house, live a stable life…”

What was a stable life? He didn’t know.

He felt his fate was like a broken ship, forever drifting on the water. The chaos and troubles of life were the big winds and waves crashing on deck—once past, they were past. He didn’t want to bother anyone or carry anyone aboard.

What was a stable life? Was it about throwing away clothes when you don’t want to wear them, knowing someone would wash and hang them? Or would he always think of going home because someone was waiting?

After a long time, he fell into a deep sleep.

He dreamed again of the ship drifting at sea.

On the deck, he saw Kimberly sitting on a high stool in front of an easel. She wasn’t wearing an evening gown but his shirt, barefoot, smiling back at him.

“You’re here again. What are you painting?”

Suddenly, the sky darkened. A huge wave roared from one side.

The ship tilted sharply. Kimberly fell off the stool onto the deck, rolling toward the railing.

His blood rushed to his head. He ran a few steps and grabbed her hand.

The wave crashed over them, an icy waterfall pounding down on his head. He struggled to open his eyes and saw her black hair tousled by the wind, her body swaying in midair.

He said, “Don’t be afraid. Raise your hand, hook it around my neck, like last time when we went to the rooftop to cool off…”

Kimberly didn’t raise her hand but smiled at him.

He suddenly noticed she was wearing lipstick.

A subtle wine-red color.

That lipstick—wasn’t it lost in the sea with her luggage?

…

Wade turned over and sat up. The moment he did, his back felt cold, as if the wave from the dream had really come.

He quickly went to the bed and called, “Kimberly?”

She was having a nightmare, just like on the plane—her body twitched slightly, hands reflexively raised and grasped at nothing. Wade heard her murmuring, “Where’s the car? I want to get in the car.”

He gripped her shoulders and gave a firm push.

After a few seconds, Kimberly slowly opened her eyes.

Wade said, “You had a nightmare.”

She didn’t speak, her gaze vacant.

“Dreaming of Mwenda again?”

Still silent.

“Was it the same dream?”

She finally regained composure, closed her eyes, and whispered, “Nightmares are exhausting, more tiring than being chased all the way.”

Wade smiled and slipped his arm around her waist, pulling her into his embrace. “Tell me about your dream.”

“If you don’t tell a nightmare, it stays in the dream forever.”

Kimberly still didn’t speak.

Outside the window, the moonlight shifted over the clothesline; the clothes hung stiffly, swaying like awkward, shy creatures.

After a long pause, she whispered, “Do you believe that, although my motives for going to Africa weren’t so pure, after I arrived and saw how hard their lives were, I really wanted to do something?”

Wade lowered his head, his chin brushing her lips.

He said, “I believe you.”

“When I arrived in Mwenda, the local situation was already tense. The Hukas were in power, and the Kwatis had an exiled liberation front. The two sides had fought several battles. The UN intervened to mediate and arranged negotiations in a neighboring country.”

“After the Huka president flew to negotiate, chaos erupted at home. Radicals shouted that the president was a traitor; they wouldn’t make peace with ‘cockroaches’ or share power with them.”

“Early that day, the radio announced major progress in negotiations and that peace was near. The president would return immediately to announce a plan.”

“Everyone gathered in the schoolyard. Phones were dead, no TV signal, then the power went out—no one knew what happened. Later, peacekeepers said it was probably a weapons depot explosion.”

She paused, lost in thought.

“By midnight, confirmed news came: the Huka president’s plane was hit by a rocket just before landing; no government personnel survived.”

“At first, I was shocked, but the peacekeepers’ faces changed. They stayed on duty all night. The atmosphere was tense. I heard them muttering many times: something’s going to happen.”

She shivered.

“At dawn, all radio stations broadcast simultaneously, filled with the Huka’s angry voices shouting, ‘The Kwatis killed our president! We cannot tolerate this!’”

Wade whispered, “Was it the Kwatis?”

She shook her head. “No one knows to this day.”

Until now, no one knew the real culprit. Both sides blamed each other: the Hukas said the Kwatis used negotiations as a cover for attack; the Kwatis said radicals in the Hukas assassinated the president to spark conflict.

Then, things happened.

After breakfast, the school with the international organization's and peacekeepers’ emblems saw the first wave of refugees. Families arrived with hastily packed bags, faces full of fear.

Some cried out, “They’re killing! The Hukas are killing people in the streets!”

Two peacekeepers went out for a patrol. When they returned, their vehicle’s windows were smashed, and they brought back a truckload of refugees covered in blood.

The vehicle rushed into the schoolyard; soldiers immediately closed the gates.

Panic spread. Kimberly, having just evacuated from Somali conflict, was relatively calm. She organized registration, soothed the crowd, sealed all entrances, and asked peacekeepers to patrol the refugee perimeter with guns.

A terrified woman clung to her clothes.

Kimberly knelt and pointed to the flag with the globe and olive branches flying high: “This is an international camp. No matter what happens outside, rest assured, you’re safe here.”

# Chapter 35 (Slightly Revised)

Wade sighed.

Refugees huddled on the playground, some wounded. Medical staff came to bandage them.

The injured spoke fearfully in broken sentences: “They gathered the knives… opened big boxes… long knives spilled everywhere… the broadcast told Hukas to take knives and kill cockroaches and anyone who shelters them…”

Countless Hukas flooded the streets to collect knives, raising them to the sky with fiery slogans. In the sunlight, the blades reflected a dazzling sea of light.

Wade was moved. “This was planned, right?”

How could they prepare weapons and broadcasts just hours after the crash?

No one expected this time it would be real, led by the military with full civilian participation, dragging Mwenda into a blood-soaked abyss.

That scene was unforgettable.

That’s when she started smoking.

Peacekeeping is usually multinational but with different proportions. Belgium had the largest and most formidable contingent in Mwenda.

They had already left.

Wade had an idea and told Kimberly, “Wait here.”

He grabbed the officer and returned to the village.

Local women wore colorful or sheer scarves from head to toe, per custom. Among so many, some might sell two scarves.

Luck was on his side—he got two, one black and one dark reddish-brown with golden patterns. When he tried to pay, the woman refused, nervously shouting something in the local language. The officer translated, “She’s begging you to leave quickly!”

Wade laughed and put the scarves in his bag.

The real pirates didn’t take a single stitch from the villagers, but Wade had his fill of free food and lodging.

When he saw Kimberly, she was curious. “Where did you go?”

Wade said nothing. After boarding the speedboat, he took out the reddish-brown scarf and said, “Cover up a bit. Don’t get sunburned.”

Kimberly opened it and looked up. The sunlight filtered through the scarf into soft golden threads.

She asked, “A gift?”

Wade said, “You’re wearing my clothes and taking my gift. You should think about how to repay me.”

Kimberly said, “I’m just wearing your clothes and taking your gift. I was thinking maybe one day I’ll take your body. I don’t know how to repay you—maybe I’ll owe you a debt. I’m already in debt anyway.”

Wade laughed heartily. The pirate chewing the Arabic tea leaves didn’t understand and looked puzzled as he started the engine.

In the blink of an eye, the village and coast were left far behind under the setting sun.

The speedboat was bigger than the last one, probably for Kimberly’s comfort. The speed was slower, and the ride less bumpy. Midway, they each got a can of cola.

Wade found it strange. Kimberly said, “Hold on to it. Here, drinking a bottle of cola is a luxury—it’s probably a gift from the Tiger Shark, a good start for the negotiation.”

Wade smiled, popped the can open, and gulped more than half down. The fizzy carbonation tingled his stomach, and his whole body felt a near-excitement.

…

He didn’t know how long they traveled or how the pirates navigated. Only when night fell did a dark fishing boat appear ahead.

No lights, no sound—like a ghost ship or a sea beast lurking just beneath the surface.

The pirate leader shouted a few words, then fired a burst into the sky.

Like a signal, lights came on the ship—fishing lamps, flashlights, and the ship’s own lamps. It was a common battered iron-hulled ship on the Red Sea. The mast lights flickered like sinister eyes in the dark.

As the speedboat neared, Wade saw at least twenty or thirty people on board—small groups of black men, some expressionless, some fierce. Some held heavy machine guns with golden belts coiled around their necks; others pinched fine sugar between fingers, letting it fall softly onto the deck.

An eleven or twelve-year-old pirate flashed his teeth threateningly at the speedboat but quickly got slapped by a big guy nearby—probably to keep him in line.

Wade smiled.

He had arrived at a new world he’d only heard of but never seen.
