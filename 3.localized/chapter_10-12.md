# Chapter 10

Kimberly pulled out a black shoulder bag.

Wade gathered five hangers together and, with a clatter, pulled them out with some force.

She had brought five evening gowns, all long dresses in different colors and styles, each in a sturdy plastic garment bag—quite heavy. Below them, five clear-lidded shoe boxes were lined up side by side, each containing high heels matching the dresses.

Kimberly said, “Oh.”

Wade calmly explained, “Ms. Lee, this is a matter of personal lifestyle. I think there’s nothing wrong with a woman taking care to look good. Whether others like it or not is their business.”

That was true enough. Wade smiled slightly. “Ms. Lee, I think we both agree that your trip to Somalia is for negotiations, not a red carpet event.”

“The Saudis have a dedicated negotiation team in Mogadishu; reporters won’t be photographing you. There’s nothing wrong with a woman showing her beauty, but in areas where pirates roam, I think you should be more restrained to avoid unnecessary attention and trouble.”

“Besides, these dresses are made of fine fabric. If you get snagged on something, it would be a shame. When you get back, you’ll have plenty of time to dress up nicely. There’s no need to rush now. So, there’s no need to bring these.”

He placed the evening gowns on the nearby table and stacked the shoe boxes beside them, deliberately slowing his movements—ready in case she objected so they could haggle again, or at least let her keep one set symbolically.

Negotiations required room for compromise.

Unexpectedly, Kimberly said nothing.

Next came a heavy makeup case. When opened, it revealed multiple layers and compartments, containing everything imaginable—just the lipsticks and perfumes alone numbered over a dozen.

Wade considered this and also put it on the table. Kimberly’s expression flickered, waiting for his explanation.

“Ms. Lee, it’s summer in Africa now. In such a hot places, no matter how well you apply makeup, sweat will ruin it quickly. It’s pointless. I don’t think you need to bring this either.”

Kimberly hesitated, glancing between him and the makeup case, but almost touchingly, she still said nothing.

Next was a leather art box. Opening it revealed a stack of drawing paper and over twenty pencils of various hardness.

This was a personal hobby; Wade almost wanted her to keep it. But the art box was heavy, and her backpack couldn’t fit it.

After a moment’s hesitation, he placed the art box on the table as well.

His reasoning: Although Africa is generally underdeveloped, paper and pencils are not hard to find there, so there’s no need to bring them.

Kimberly still showed no sign of opposition. The streamlining went so smoothly Wade began to wonder if she was preparing for a sudden outburst.

He continued, piling more and more items on the table, all arranged haphazardly, like disgruntled wives locked away in the cold, glaring with unwilling eyes.

He accidentally knocked over a silk pouch, its drawstring loose, revealing half a delicate scene inside—a sheer, low-rise lace panty in pearly silver-gray.

Caught off guard, Wade felt a bit embarrassed. He quickly tightened the pouch and stuffed it into her backpack.

Kimberly suddenly stopped him. “No, really. Humans evolved from monkeys, and monkeys never wear these things. So, humans don’t need to either. No need to bring it.”

Wade pretended not to hear, not ruffled by her teasing. In the service industry, a little mockery was inevitable—just a breeze messing up his hair.

When he finished streamlining, the backpack felt a bit loose. Wade wondered if he’d gone too far. After some thought, he opened the art box, rolled up a few sheets of drawing paper with some pencils, and tucked them into the side pocket.

Then he reopened the makeup case, suggesting she pick a lipstick to bring along, reasoning: “If you don’t feel well on the trip and your complexion looks bad, a touch of color on your lips can really brighten you up.”

Kimberly hooked her index finger and picked a gold-capped lipstick from the vertical row, clutching it in her palm. “Mr. Wade, is this like a slap followed by a treat? With time, you could be sitting at the negotiation table yourself.”

Wade took it as a compliment. “You flatter me, Ms. Lee.”

It was almost time to leave. The Eastern European woman lifted the curtain and stepped out gracefully. The timing was exact. At the same moment, music burst out, cheers filled the air, and the revolving lights shone through the curtain, casting moving shadows on the wall behind them.

Kimberly slung the bag over one shoulder, opened a small side door, revealing a narrow staircase leading to the back exit.

She fumbled to turn on the stairwell light and asked, “Mr. Wade, if I cooperate this well, do you think I’ll live longer?”

Her tone was sarcastic as she went down ahead. Saed suddenly tensed, licking his lips, and urged Wade, “Mr. Wade, please protect Ms. Lee well. Our ship and the people on board… we place great hopes on her…”

Wade replied, “From a financial perspective, she’s the employer and I’m the bodyguard; from a gender perspective, she’s a woman and I’m a man. Whichever way you look at it, I’ll do my best to take care of her.”

Saed had no more advice to give. Compared to these two experts, he was just an ordinary employee.

He watched Wade walk to the end of the stairs, open the door just a crack, and patiently observed the outside for a while.

Then he patted Kimberly’s shoulder.

Before the cold air could rush in, they had already disappeared.

On the other side of the curtain, the grand opera *The Masked Ball* filled the air, the soaring tenor voice mixed with the hurried rhythms of the common folk—a noisy world pressed close to the ears.

Suddenly, Saed thought this opera choice was unfortunate.

---

Following the tip from Reindeer, they left through the back door and walked down the driveway. Wade didn’t speak to Kimberly the whole way. Near the car, he tugged her to a stop.

He opened the car doors, checking both front and back seats carefully.

Kimberly asked, “Are you worried that someone might suddenly sit up in the back seat with a gun, or slit your throat with a knife?”

Wade said, “If movies keep showing that, it means it’s happened hundreds of times in real life. Being cautious never hurts.”

He let Kimberly get in first, then opened the trunk. Reindeer had been thorough—there were the luggage bags and a food pack containing compressed biscuits, water, and a brown paper package.

Wade opened the brown paper package. Inside were a fully loaded Beretta M9, a Smith & Wesson Bear Claw, a first aid kit, and two syringes with anesthetic injections.

A note read: “Just in case, for self-defense on the road. Once in Africa, handle it yourself.”

Wade understood the meaning. These items wouldn’t pass airport security and would have to be discarded later.

He holstered the guns behind his waist, slammed the trunk shut with a bang, and carried the gear around to the front...

Wait, Kimberly was sitting in the driver’s seat.

He tapped twice on the window with his finger. Kimberly glanced back at him through the glass but showed no intention to move.

Got it. Wade smiled and went to the passenger side, getting in.

“Don’t you want to explain?”

“To take care of some personal matters.”

That didn’t sound very good.

“The ship and hostages are in pirate hands. Shouldn’t we hurry?”

Kimberly started the engine. “Mr. Wade, this isn’t disaster relief. We’re not racing against the golden 72 hours. Negotiations require steadiness, not haste.”

“As of this month, pirates hold over 200 cargo ships from various countries. Because negotiations stalled, the longest detention has lasted over 25 months. And my personal matter will only take an hour or two.”

Sharpening the axe won’t delay the woodcutting—that was a reasonable excuse. Wade gestured ‘please’.

---

The car headed toward the city.

Wade kept an eye on the road ahead and behind, sure no one was tailing them. He suspected Kimberly’s death threat came from a stalker. Data shows that leaving familiar surroundings—traveling or moving to a distant state or country—is an effective way to avoid certain obsessive stalkers.

“Can I ask you something?”

“Go ahead.”

“That hand… you really don’t recognize it?”

Kimberly’s hand rested on the steering wheel, eyes focused on the road ahead. “Should I?”

Wade felt it wasn’t an ordinary threatening hand. The bite mark on the webbing was a unique sign. Marks like that are usually meant for someone who understands the message.

“Maybe try to recall if anything in your past relates to that bite mark.”

Kimberly furrowed her brow. The car lights and distant headlights scattered across her eyes like a sea of shimmering light.

The car passed Amanda’s bronze statue in the city square. In the dark, a lone pigeon perched on the goddess’s wavy curls.

Kimberly seemed to remember something hesitantly: “It’s like... there was a time...”

“When I was feeling down, I posted a lot of editorials, targeting different people, and was pretty harsh…”

So her editorials depended on her mood.

Wade thought: You admit you were harsh.

“Later, they probably got mad and hired someone to write rebuttals, saying this yellow-skinned woman was like a rabid dog biting anyone she saw…”

“So, sending me a hand with a bite mark—was that to call me a mad dog?”

That didn’t quite make sense either. The card said, “You’re next to die,” indicating a sequence, a cycle.

The hand’s owner must share some trait with Kimberly.

Kimberly slowed down as the car entered a parking lot. “But it doesn’t affect me. If words could drown people, there’d have been no world wars… Whatever, let them say whatever.”

The car stopped. She looked up at the hotel sign, its smooth letters glowing faintly against the dark.

Rita Plaza Hotel.

A meeting? Dinner? A handoff?

None of those. Kimberly led Wade into the lobby, upstairs, and right at a long corridor where temporary banners had been set up. The images showed a shattered Earth floating in deep space.

The title read: *Earth’s Path Forward: Humanity, Environment, and the Future.*

A lecture?

At the entrance, a woman at a registration table whispered, “The lecture’s already started. Just push the door gently and sit in the back row. Try not to make noise…”

She handed over a small booklet. “Sorry, only one free copy.”

Wade, standing close, took it casually. It was a thin pocket notebook, palm-sized, with rough paper. He slipped it into his back pocket.

Environmentalists sure were poor.

Holding their breath, they sat in the last row.

The lecture was interesting, like theater lighting—bright stage, dark audience.

Kimberly whispered, “Sorry, you probably aren’t interested in this kind of talk.”

But there was no apology in her tone.

Wade smiled and lowered his voice, “It’s fine. I used to go with a client to test makeup. I knew the shades better than the salespeople. People like us, relying on youth, better learn some skills. When we get old, we can sell makeup or do environmental work.”

Kimberly glanced at him quickly. His face was half in light, half in shadow, sharp as if carved by a knife but softened by the lighting.

On stage, a student holding a microphone stammered angrily, “I don’t understand why Professor Jiang keeps saying protecting the Earth is wrong. Shouldn’t we protect the Earth? Our home?”

Wade thought silently: Of course we should… What kind of professor is this who doesn’t protect the Earth?

# Chapter 11

A handsome man stepped onto the stage, smiling as he took the microphone from the student.

Wade’s first thought: Another Asian.

Recently, he’d met more Asian nationals than in the past year combined. Then he realized it was a chain reaction—from Kimberly he met Yongfu Lin, and Kimberly was sitting here.

His second thought…

Bodyguards usually have excellent memories, able to recall faces seen within the past three days—and this face was familiar.

A few nights ago, Reindeer had shown him a photo of this man, excitedly saying, “But God is fair—her fiancé at the hospital has a new flame…”

No wonder Kimberly suddenly wanted to attend this lecture. Clearly, her intentions were elsewhere.

The microphone amplified Jiang’s deep voice.

“Here, I’m just correcting a misconception. The Earth never needs protection. Global warming, acid rain, desertification, air pollution—those threats are to humans, not the Earth.”

“The Earth doesn’t care if the atmosphere is mostly nitrogen or oxygen, whether the temperature is 100 degrees or minus 100, if 1000 km/h winds blow across its surface, or if silica dust rains daily. Don’t say the Earth is scarred and needs protection—it simply doesn’t care.”

“It’s us, these fragile two-legged creatures, who need protection. In medicine, a 0.5-degree fever above normal is a fever. Short-term radiation above 100 millisieverts is harmful. Oxygen below 6% kills humans in minutes. We plant trees, fight desertification, protect water, reduce pollution, develop technology to repair the ozone layer—not to protect Earth.”

“When humanity collapses due to environmental issues, will Earth die with us? No. It simply changes captains. Just as it replaced dinosaurs with humans, who knows who the next captain will be…”

…

Moments ago, Wade thought Jiang was a “bad professor.” Now he thought the professor had substance and made good points.

Still, Wade was more concerned about why Kimberly came to this lecture.

— A hopeless heart? Lingering feelings?

No. She was the one caught cheating. Besides, sitting there, her complexion unchanged, she lightly tapped her index finger on her knee once, then again.

— Turning hostility into friendship? Failed lovers becoming friends?

No. If she wanted reconciliation, she could have done it anytime. Why now? There was still a night boat waiting at Turku harbor to take them to Stockholm.

Suddenly, the lights brightened, the crowd buzzed, and a 10-minute intermission was announced. The second half would be a debate.

Everyone stood and filed out. Wade and Kimberly’s seats were last but they were among the first to leave.

Just as they stopped in the hallway, Jiang and his colleagues came by.

Kimberly lowered her head, brushing hair from her face, but kept her gaze on Jiang until they disappeared behind the lounge door.

Wade chuckled, treating it like a show, then glanced at his watch—her “personal matter” was supposed to take only an hour or two.

Kimberly whispered, “Did you see that man in the gray suit, with golden hair?”

He did. Jiang’s colleague, tall and thin, like a gray bamboo stick.

“He has a key card. He just opened the door and put the card back in the right pocket of his suit.”

“So?”

“When the second half starts, help me get that key card.”

Wade smiled lazily, arms crossed, leaning against the wall.

“Alright, if you can convince me, I’ll do it.”

“Aren’t you eager to get going? If I get that card, I can handle my business inside. Ten minutes max, then we can leave.”

“What business? If you set a fire, I’ll be an accomplice, won’t I?”

“You’ll see everything. If it’s inappropriate, you can stop me.”

Wade checked his watch again.

That was a persuasive argument: he really wanted to leave sooner. It was two hours’ drive from Helsinki to Turku.

“Ten minutes, you say? Can I time you?”

“...Sure.”

“Deal.”

When the time came, the crowd streamed back into the hall. Wade pushed against the flow, brushed past the gray bamboo stick, and without looking back, raised his hand.

Between his index and middle fingers, he held the gold key card. Then he loosened his grip, letting the card slip into his sleeve.

Kimberly suddenly thought this guy was quite interesting.

The hallway cleared, and the waiting room was empty. Wade swiped the card and opened the door.

Inside was just an ordinary lounge—bags on hooks, clothes hanging. Some glasses were empty; some half full.

Kimberly went to the coat rack and inspected the outermost white shirt.

Wade looked too. It was a man’s shirt, fine fabric, slightly wrinkled, the back of the vest area damp with faint sweat.

It had to be Jiang’s shirt. Wade hoped Kimberly wasn’t planning to take it as a souvenir—taking sweaty clothes felt no different from stealing underwear.

Kimberly pulled out a cigarette pack, flicked out a long black cigarette with a gold ring near the filter.

She lit it, took a drag, and asked, “What do you think of Jiang’s stage presence?”

Was she talking about his performance? Wade thought for a moment. “Pretty good.”

Kimberly shook her head. “He’s nervous. Always has been. Whenever he speaks publicly, he sweats.”

“I told him later to bring a spare shirt and change halfway through, so he wouldn’t have to wear a wet shirt all the time.”

Wade frowned.

She was nostalgic, ready to vent; ten minutes wouldn’t be enough...

But she didn’t say more. She rotated the cigarette between her fingers, then pinched the tip between index finger and thumb, burning a hole in the back of the shirt.

A faint sizzling sound, a mild burnt smell. The hole’s edges were blackened with char, surrounded by yellowish scorch marks.

Wade stayed calm.

The destruction had begun. By the usual script, she’d also bring scissors to shred the shirt and a bucket of red paint to splash the room in tears.

But no.

She brought the cigarette tip back up, as if checking the symmetry, asking him to confirm: “Is it symmetrical?”

“...Symmetrical.”

The shirt hanging on the rack now had two burned holes, at the same height and evenly spaced.

“Then let’s go.”

That’s it?

Wade was baffled. “You insisted on squeezing this in before we left just to burn holes in a shirt? Can’t you pick another time?”

“No. This is my plan. This day is when it has to be done. And this isn’t burning holes—it’s closure.”

Editors sure loved their wordplay, turning “burning holes” into something noble like “closure.”

As they left, Wade looked back; the shirt swayed gently on the rack, two small holes like dull, clueless eyes.

He felt sorry for it: why burn it? The tailor worked hard. If she had guts, she’d burn Jiang’s skin instead.

Finally back in the driver’s seat, Wade felt something poking his back pocket. He pulled out the gifted notebook. He almost tossed it carelessly but then flipped through the pages.

About a dozen pages. If the trip went smoothly, writing a sentence or two each day about her would make a perfect assignment.

So he slipped it back in—though he hoped he wouldn’t have to write.

The car left Helsinki; now it truly felt like a journey. He’d driven this road before. In daylight, the scenery was beautiful—rolling fields, forests, rivers, and scattered red-roofed white cottages.

But now, only thick darkness, the mournful sound of water, and distant lights.

Wade decided to negotiate.

“Can you not write your thoughts about me every day? Opinions don’t change that fast. I can’t possibly have new thoughts about you every day.”

“Not even a single sentence?”

Wade said nothing. Asking that felt greedy—and a bit embarrassing. How long had it been since he’d felt embarrassed?

“What do you think of me now?”

“Let me think.”

He paused briefly. “I think you’re quite dull. But not in the way everyone means.”

Wade carefully chose his words.

“In Lapland, I met an old Sami man. He invited me into his tent to warm up. He said a person’s life is like a piece of firewood.”

“First it’s a tree, growing. Then it’s cut down, becoming wood.”

“Working, doing things, the wood burns, giving light and heat, full of energy.”

“Finally, old age comes, and the wood becomes charcoal, cooling down.”

“Ms. Lee, you’re like charcoal that’s cooling.”

“Your mood when haggling with the Saudis, talking to me, signing contracts, even burning Jiang’s shirt—it’s always the same.”

Like the flattest melody, no highs or lows. Who knows if this is just the prelude or the whole song.

Kimberly said, “I’m really dull. More than one person has said so.”

She leaned back, pulled her hat down. “If you get bored on the trip, as long as I’m safe, feel free to go have fun. I won’t report you to the Saudis.”

She closed her eyes.

The worst travel companion is one who drives while the other sleeps.

Such a pretty face paired with a dull personality.

Wade tried to comfort himself: dullness only makes companions bored; better that than forcing fun and driving people crazy.

He treated it like a solo nighttime drive, a cruise.

The wind swayed the black treetops.

The river curved like a mirror in the night, dotted with frozen, shrunken stars.

When they finally reached Turku, a giant inflatable duck squatted on the lawn, like it was hatching eggs.

---

Tapio was probably a “local character” at the oil terminal. Wade asked a night shift worker and quickly found his single dorm-cum-guard room.

It was past midnight. The room was lit, the door half open.

Wade pushed it open. Tapio looked up in surprise. He was in his fifties, with a wild golden beard, holding a porn magazine. His fast food box was open beside him, full of fries with ketchup in blobs like old blood plasma.

His greasy hands took Wade’s “boarding pass.” He suddenly understood. “Oh, Saudi’s style.”

Money is Saudi’s face; the world respects it.

Tapio rubbed his hands and flipped through a worn logbook. “You’re a bit unlucky... several cargo ships just left… but there’s one from Lithuania to Germany. It hit a storm at sea, got lost, and has been stuck in Turku for days. It’s about to leave. I might get you on, but…”

He lowered his voice, leaning to Wade’s ear, smelling strongly of fries, beer, and body odor.

Wade held his breath.

“But once on board, you have to stay in your cabin. No matter what you see or hear, don’t interfere or ask questions. When you reach Stockholm, get off.”

Got it: a ghost ship.

Wade frowned. “Any other ships?”

“There are, but you have to wait. The earliest is in four hours.”

Wade looked back at Kimberly, leaning by the door.

She looked tired, sleepy, a bit impatient. “If there’s a ship now, let’s go.”

# Chapter 12

Thinking it over, it wasn’t a big deal. Life often brushes close to evil—like murder next door, or fire upstairs. Riding a ghost ship was just a bit closer, shoulder to shoulder.

Wade drove while Tapio sat beside him, giving directions. The huge oil tanker was docked near the port, connected to the railway. The car seemed like a toy weaving through the shadows of giant ships.

They stopped beside a cargo ship.

It was a refrigerated vessel, small compared to the massive ships nearby. Few lights were on, dim and sparse. Only the bow and stern anchor lights shone bright white.

Tapio got out first, switched on a powerful flashlight, sweeping a big circle over the ship’s bridge. Then he blinked the light three times.

After a while, footsteps sounded on deck. A burly man emerged from the dark, followed by several figures patrolling at a distance.

They left the car behind. Wade helped Kimberly with her bag; she took the food pack, which wasn’t heavy.

The night at the oil terminal was thick and black like oil, with shimmering reflections. The refrigerated ship had a normal draft, painted “EAGLE” on the hull—probably its name.

In the distance, some men joked roughly, their coarse laughter unsettling.

As they approached, Wade saw the man’s face clearly: middle-aged, crew cut, black jacket with sleeves rolled up to the elbows, muscular arms covered in messy tattoos.

Tapio whispered a few words to him. The man’s English was rough and stern, repeatedly saying “No” in several sentences. Tapio nodded each time.

After a while, the man turned and walked up the deck. Tapio hurriedly urged Wade and Kimberly, “Follow closely!”

Their footsteps echoed hollowly on deck, like tapping on wood.

Someone poked their head out of the bridge and shouted something to the man, who laughed and replied quickly in a fast Eastern European dialect Wade couldn’t understand. Kimberly seemed equally puzzled, rustling the paper bag.

At the lower hatch, the man yanked open the door with a crash. A narrow ladder led down into a brightly lit, eerily quiet hold. From the deck, it looked like a white pit.

The man looked at Wade and repeated his harsh commands:

“No wandering.”

“No meddling.”

“No matter what happens, stay in your room.”

…

The demands were unreasonable. What if there was a fire or the ship was sinking? Stay in your room and wait to die? But the man’s face wasn’t joking. Wade swallowed his teasing and nodded.

Suddenly, a piercing scream rang out—hysterical and bone-chilling.

A cold shiver ran from Wade’s wrist up his arm. His half arm went numb. A terrible thought struck him.

It was Kimberly, standing not far away!

Tapio was confused, not sure what was happening. The man seemed to want to rush down but stopped. Wade couldn’t catch Kimberly; she collapsed heavily.

Chaos erupted. Several figures burst from the shadows. The man shouted, “No! No!”

Wade caught sight of several men holding long-barrel assault rifles.

This was armed escort? But Wade couldn’t care. He knelt beside Kimberly, holding down her convulsing body, yelling to Tapio, “Lights!”

The deck lights flickered on, shaking. Kimberly’s eyes rolled back; blood foamed at her lips. Half her face and neck were stained with blood. Her arms jerked like electric shocks.

Wade reached for her pulse, but her throat suddenly emitted a long, gasping sound. Her hands clawed empty air, and her body arched upward, back suspended—like sudden unconsciousness.

No wounds on her head or neck. Not a sniper shot. Poison? When did she get poisoned? He’d been with her the whole time and didn’t notice!

Noisy voices surrounded them as the crew came over. Wade heard them and the man speaking in that unintelligible language. He looked up sharply. The man suddenly understood and shouted, “Not us! Not us!”

Tapio suggested, “Call an ambulance? No, can’t bring people onboard. Take her to the hospital.”

Wade scooped up Kimberly and rushed off the ship. Tapio grabbed the luggage and followed in a quick line.

Some crew members stood around, confused. One tried to wipe the blood on the deck, but the man kicked him down, shouting, “Idiot! Don’t you fear poison or contagion?”

---

Back in the car, Wade put Kimberly in the back seat and sped out.

His palms sweated; his back was tight. His mind raced with questions.

— Where was the hospital? Turku wasn’t big; the signs on high ground should help.

— He was sure no mistakes were made after picking Kimberly up. If she was poisoned, it must have been before he took over.

— Poison? Her blood looked normal, no discoloration. But in this high-tech age, maybe it was a new method.

— It was hard to explain. The trip hadn’t even started and she was already...

Suddenly, a hand grabbed his thigh and whispered, “Don’t stop. Get out of town.”

Damn!

Wade’s heart jumped. The car swerved sharply, tires screeching on the road.

Luckily, his reflexes kicked in and he regained control. His chest heaved. He looked up into the rearview mirror.

Kimberly sat up. Blood smeared her mouth, like a vampire freshly fed. She grabbed a tissue, wiped her face, and said, “Keep driving. I remember there’s a phone booth ahead. I need to make a call.”

Wade said nothing, unwilling to ask questions now. He handed her a water bottle from the passenger seat. Kimberly twisted it open, dampened the tissue, and wiped her face.

After a while, they spotted a red-topped glass phone booth in the woods by the roadside. The lower glass was frosted and converted into an outdoor toilet. Nordic phone booths were practical, designed for the poor, often with extra functions—Wade had even seen some with running water inside.

The car stopped. Kimberly got out steadily.

Wade stayed put, watching her through the window. Good—she walked steadily, no staggering, her sense of direction intact. The earlier convulsions and collapse seemed like a distant memory.

His chest tightened. He suddenly felt sweat on his back and wanted to curse. He rummaged through the glove box but found no cigarettes. Looking down, he saw a blurry blood handprint on his pants—like a scene from a horror movie.

He looked up. Kimberly was already on the phone, leaning against the glass, one hand playing with the coiled cord.

Wade stepped out quietly and stood nearby.

The damp scent of trees mixed with the phone booth’s musty air. The glass door was half open—probably to air it out.

He caught fragments of her conversation:

— “E-A-G-L-E, the name painted on the ship.”

— “I’ve reported this to several regulatory agencies. If the coast guard tries to cover it up, they’ll face consequences.”

— “Even if the ship enters international waters, universal jurisdiction applies. They can board and seize it.”

…

Her lips curled unconsciously, revealing a subtle ruthlessness.

Wade leaned against a tree, watching her with interest.

She’d slipped up.

He’d thought she was like cooling charcoal, but the charred surface had peeled away, revealing a glowing red core.

Finally, she hung up and came out.

Wade said, “Was that an act? Pretty convincing. Can you clue me in?”

Where did the blood come from? She couldn’t always carry blood plasma for drama.

Kimberly said nothing. After a pause, she held out her hand. On her index finger hung a Smith & Wesson Bear Claw, swinging loosely.

Wade stared for a moment, feeling a chill.

— She was carrying the food bag with the Bear Claw and first aid kit inside.

While Wade was distracted by the shipman’s words, Kimberly had used the Bear Claw to cut a blood vessel somewhere, sucked the blood into her mouth, wrapped a tourniquet, and screamed in pain.

She’d caused the incident herself.

Wade’s scalp prickled. His mood could only be described as “damn it.” Thinking back, the bleeding had been heavy. This cut was certainly deep.

“Ms. Lee, the Bear Claw has serrated edges and strong bite force. The wounds don’t heal easily and scab badly. For reporting a smuggling ship... that’s quite a sacrifice.”

Just a smuggling ship. Was it worth it? In international waters, thousands of smuggling routes operate, so large that countries have special agencies, large staffs, and even international cooperation to counter them.

Bleeding the ship dry with such a fuss might not yield results once the blood dries.

Kimberly said, “I think it’s worth it.”

Different values. If you think it’s worth it, then it is. Wade didn’t argue. He turned and got in the car.

Kimberly sat down. “You don’t think it means much, do you?”

Wade shrugged. “I just think it’s a ghost ship. Just a ride.”

“Whether they smuggle guns or drugs, you might not save anyone—people who want guns or drugs will always find a way. But we have a plan. Your interference might force us to change routes…”

“No, it’s not that.”

Wade didn’t understand. “Then what is it? Cigarettes, alcohol, luxury goods?”

“Human trafficking.”

Wade was stunned.

Kimberly rolled down the window slightly, picked a cigarette, and held it in her hand. “Human trafficking ranks third in global underground trade, with a tight network, international cooperation, and armed escorts. Eighty percent of victims are women. You can imagine their fate…”

She inhaled deeply and exhaled slowly, “If I were you, I wouldn’t park by the phone booth. At least find a hidden, quiet spot with a good view.”
