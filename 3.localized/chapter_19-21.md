# Chapter 19

Wade went to the CoCo’s room to take a shower, but on the short walk back, he was already dripping with sticky, thin sweat. It didn’t make sense to him—there was no rain here, the air was dry and hot, so shouldn’t it be drying him out instead? Yet, he was sweating.

Knocking, he entered the room. Kimberly sat on a palm mat, holding a plate as she ate. Her hair was half-dry, and she was wrapped in a black silk shawl.

Wade recognized the shawl. When they pared down their luggage, she’d explained it could serve many purposes: a bath towel, a nightdress, a headscarf, a beach shawl, or even a skirt—half-length or full-length. It was so versatile that Wade thought if he were born a woman, he’d definitely need one.

She had pale skin, and the black garment made her stand out sharply.

The ceiling fan was running at full blast, the hooks creaking like they might snap any second.

Kimberly looked up at him. “You’re staying with me?”

Wade unfolded a reclining chair. “By the rules, yes. But you can ask me to sleep by the door—though if someone breaks in, I’d be a second or two slower getting to you.”

His real intention was to sleep inside and enjoy the fan’s breeze.

Kimberly lowered her eyelids, patiently stabbing at a slippery piece of lamb with her fork. “Then you can sleep here.”

Wade breathed a sigh of relief. As he lay down, he felt something was missing.

Only when the lights went out did he remember, “Are there mosquitoes?”

“North side’s more desert climate. Too hot for mosquitoes. They’ll come out when it cools down.”

Wade chuckled bitterly in the dark: even mosquitoes don’t work under these conditions.

“You seem pretty familiar with the local culture here in Africa?”

“Everyone has their expertise. I study this; you’re familiar with guns.”

Judging by her tone, she wasn’t very interested in chatting, so Wade closed his eyes and focused on sleeping.

But he couldn’t rest. The spot where his body touched the chair quickly became hot and stuffy. He kept shifting and moving around. The enclosed room, stirred by the fan, seemed to generate heat rather than cool air.

He didn’t know how long passed, but in a daze, he suddenly heard a sound—the kind of sudden silence that feels like the world has stopped.

The fan slowed down.

The power must be draining away as fast as water sinking into sand.

The electricity went out.

The air was stifling, sticky with sweat. It was worse than sleeping outdoors. Wade felt he couldn’t bear it.

But someone else couldn’t either.

There was movement on the bed. Kimberly sat up and then grabbed a magazine to fan herself.

When he bought that magazine, he had a feeling it might come in handy for her—but not like this.

Strangely, when she struggled, he felt more comfortable and even a strange sense of superiority.

Kimberly was clearly annoyed. She got out of bed, barefoot, her footsteps silent. She tried to open the window, but the latch was stuck. Failing that, she went to open the door.

The door opened to a pale gray sky. Kimberly leaned against the frame, letting in air, like a slender black shadow growing out of the door wall.

It was quite an effort.

After a while, she came back, stopped beside his recliner, and half-kneeling said, “Hey.”

Just before sleep, she’d ignored him, now awake and unable to sleep, coming to chat?

Wade was too lazy to engage, replying with an annoyed tone, “Hmm?”

“It’s too hot.”

“Too hot... and you wake me up to get cool? Doing something that hurts others but doesn’t help yourself—what’s the point?”

Kimberly sneered, “Keep pretending! Keep pretending!”

“You were already awake, eyes shining bright. Did you think I didn’t see?”

Well, he blamed his sharp eyesight.

Wade sat up.

“What do you want?”

“This house is brick, with a concrete roof. Concrete cools quickly. There’s wind up higher—we can go up there to cool off.”

“...One hundred euros.”

“What?”

“Taking a customer up to the roof at midnight wasn’t in the contract. One hundred euros.”

She asked money from the Saudis, so he could ask from her—did she think only she could shave others’ heads?

An old poem said: “If there’s a head, it can be shaved; no shave, no head.”

Wade wanted to see her lose her temper, but he really hadn’t.

After a moment:

“...Last time, you lent me a cigarette—one hundred twenty euros, no bargaining.”

Damn, twenty euros more than him.

Wade snapped, “You want me to pay you now?”

“No need. All debts will be settled at the end.”

Wade laughed instead of getting angry, paused, then leaned close to her ear.

“Aren’t you afraid the accounts will get mixed up and never settled?”

He brushed her aside, stood up, and pulled the palm mat down from the bed.

---

When this little building was built, the owner probably never intended anyone to climb onto the roof—there was no stairs going up, and no square openings in the corridor ceiling for climbing.

They had to step on the railing.

For him, it was easy to stretch his limbs.

Wade quickly balanced on the railing, grabbed the roof with one hand, took the palm mat Kimberly handed him with the other, swung it a few times to test the weight, then with one strong toss, threw it up.

The mat slid several meters along the concrete and stopped. He used his strength to pull himself up.

There really was a breeze. He bent down, pressing his palm against the ground—the concrete was cool.

Looking out, the view opened wide. The mud-yellow moon was bent like a laughing mouth showing teeth. The great river lay asleep on the black mud beds scattered with houses. If it suddenly woke and stood upright, those houses would probably tumble like ticks.

Kimberly waited a long time before Wade poked his head over the eaves.

“How do I get up?”

“I’ll lie down here. You grab my hand, stand on the railing, and I’ll pull you up.”

“Wait a moment.”

She retreated into the black doorway, loosened the black shawl, held the edges, wrapped it again, tying the back.

Then she came out and reached her hand.

Wade didn’t take it.

“Aren’t you afraid I’ll tear the wound on your right arm?”

Kimberly paused, then switched hands and extended her right hand.

“I didn’t think about it at the moment.”

Wade grabbed her wrist and motioned for her to cross her hand behind his, creating a locked grip.

She had moments of nervousness. First, she sat on the railing, then swung one leg over. As she slowly stood, her slight trembling passed through her damp palm into his arm.

Finally upright, Kimberly’s chest heaved. Looking up, the roof was still just above her head.

“And then?”

Wade lowered his neck. “There’s no good leverage here. Hug my neck tight; I’ll do the rest.”

If it weren’t for this awkward spot—no way forward or back—she probably wouldn’t bother cooling off.

She released one hand, exhaled, and wrapped her arms around his neck. Wade’s other hand shielded her back, giving her a sense of security. Gritting his teeth, he wrapped the other arm around her too.

Sweat dripped from above onto her neck, trailing down, burning hot, mixing with hers as it slid inside her clothes.

Kimberly’s ears flushed hot. Suddenly uncomfortable, she looked down.

“What if we fall?”

As they climbed, Wade was clearly trying to kneel and stand.

“If we fall, the headlines will read: ‘Saudi Arabia hires negotiation expert; two fall from rooftop at midnight while cooling off, both seriously injured...’”

Before he finished, he suddenly groaned, straightened up, sliding his hand from her waist down to her leg. He lifted her firmly, leaned back, and stepped back twice.

Kimberly hadn’t reacted yet; he had already set her down on the hard concrete roof.

Finally steady, the wind blew.

Kimberly sat down on the palm mat, catching her breath. When she looked up again, Wade stood at the edge of the roof, the tip of the moon humorously hooked on his hair like it was about to pluck a tuft.

He suddenly tilted, nearly losing balance.

Kimberly shouted, “Hey!”

Wade steadied himself, looked back at her, then came over and sat beside her.

“Just revisiting the old training exercise—how far can your body lean and still right itself?”

“Weren’t you kicked out?”

“Yeah, but not because of skill—I wasn’t the best that batch, but I was at least top three.”

“So, the beret training kicks out the best performers?”

Wade thought for a moment. “Probably because my discipline was terrible.”

“There was a week of intense jungle hunger training. No food except snails. The instructor set a limit—three snails a day. Some couldn’t bear it and ate four or five.”

“Those people were punished. The punishment was to strip down to underwear, tie hands and feet to a wooden stake, and spend the night tied up. That’s bad enough, but the jungle had termites that crawled into your clothes—and all over your body, even into... your crotch.”

“I managed to loosen the ropes and ran. That’s the worst case: immediate expulsion and possibly execution if caught. Beret training allowed a certain death rate. So I ran thoroughly and never went back.”

“Regret it?”

Wade was indifferent. “No. Those Malaysian soldiers trained to defend their country. But what home or country am I protecting? I have no home, and the country probably doesn’t recognize me.”

The mat wasn’t big enough for him to lie down comfortably. He put his hands behind his head and lay on the ground. Sleepiness crept in. Looking at the moon, he saw multiple overlapping shadows.

In all of Khartoum, only he and Kimberly were on the roof, two Chinese in a foreign land, under the vast black sky, a sudden wave of loneliness washing over them. It was a scene he would never forget.

He slowly closed his eyes. “I’m like a broken ship, drifting in the water... That’s just how it is. I’m not like you—I know even if you go off track, you’ve got backup plans.”

Kimberly said nothing.

“There’s no conflict between us. I hope you stay safe, truly.”

“And there’s something I’ve wanted to tell you for a long time.”

“Next time you write an editorial, tone it down a bit. Those people aren’t good. They’d love to take you down. You have to be smart on your own.”

He was really sleepy now. Sounds around him blurred. His body sank into deep sleep—a vast, pale gray, hazy private space—where huge clusters of dense green leaves spread out. Then, amidst varying shades of rich green, a porcelain-white Buddha’s flame bud slowly opened, slightly curled, like the heavenly crown veil of Guanyin Bodhisattva.

Back in Chinatown, to make a living, he’d mingled in various Chinese shops. Many Chinese believed in spirits and feng shui. Shops often had colorful statues of gods: the God of Wealth, Lord Guan, Maitreya, Zhang Fei, Zhong Kui, and Guanyin Bodhisattva.

Wade liked Guanyin Bodhisattva. He felt her face and eyes were full of compassion.

He should ask Erinn about those two white palms.

In a daze, he heard Kimberly whisper, “I won’t write anymore.”

It must be a dream.

# Chapter 20

Early morning, a fishy smell drifted into his nose.

It wasn’t fish being cooked—there was sea air, fishy brine, and murmuring voices.

Wade opened his eyes. It was still dark, a gray-white cloth tucked at the edges of the horizon. In an hour or two, sunlight would stream in, and the heat and dryness would return.

Turning his head, Kimberly was still asleep.

Wade got up, curious, and followed the voices to the edge of the floorboards. In the courtyard, a pickup truck was parked, its bed covered with thick plastic, piled with countless fish and several large blocks of ice.

The driver sat cross-legged on the hood, holding a metal plate, dipping bread into yellow bean paste. CoCo stood nearby, talking to him, carrying on his shoulder...

A swimming ring?

No, that wasn’t right. Why did it have so many sharp white teeth?

Wade crouched and whistled softly below.

CoCo looked up, eyes brightening, and raised the “swimming ring” over his head. “Wade! Look! Look!”

Look at what? What the hell was it?

Curious, Wade pressed on the floor, leapt up to the railing, then vaulted down. The driver’s mouth dropped open, and after a moment, he said, “Wow...” and gave him a thumbs-up.

Wade smiled, examining the thing CoCo carried. He reached out and touched it, then his expression changed.

Damn—it was a hard shark jawbone, with sharp molar-like teeth, narrow and pointed in front, flat in the back. Running his fingertip over the sharp teeth made his skin bristle.

CoCo’s face lit up. “I’ve been asking people to help me get one for a long time. A shipment of seafood came to Port Sudan and they brought it for me—the real shark jaw!”

Fishermen at Port Sudan sometimes caught sharks and cut off the entire jaw with teeth intact, dried it, and sold it as a pendant.

Wade took it and compared sizes. This shark was relatively small; larger jaws could fit a person. But even this small one could easily bite him in two.

“What are you going to do with it?”

“Put it on my car’s front. Shark jaw! Way cooler than Mitsubishi’s shark-mouth grille.”

“Tie it to your car’s front... your tuk-tuk?”

CoCo was annoyed. “It’s my own car! A 4x4! Didn’t you know?”

Wade did know, but—

You knew you should buy a good car, yet you got a three-wheeler!

The driver finished eating, unloaded some seafood to the guesthouse, then drove away. CoCo clung to the shark jaw, unwilling to let go. Maybe because it was just a mouth, no one would try to grab a live one?

Seeing no one around, Wade crouched and lowered his voice. “Any news about the Reindeer?”

Time to get serious.

CoCo leaned the shark jaw against the wall and squatted opposite him.

This was a safe way to talk—both squatting, low position, easy to conceal. They could watch 360 degrees for any movement and warn each other. Their voices projected downward and inward, minimizing the chance of being overheard.

“Negotiations on the high seas are sure. You’ll have to go east, cross the desert, reach the coast. But pirates won’t go to busy ports. From what I hear, they’ll pick a remote fishing village where a speedboat will meet you. Once at sea, you board the negotiation ship.”

“How do I get there?”

“To avoid attention, you can take a bus or drive a van or pickup. These vehicles often run desert routes.”

Wade relaxed.

Luckily he hadn’t said, “Wade, just drive that tuk-tuk over there.”

“I can help you get a vehicle. Make me a list of what you might need—guns, scopes, medicine, first aid kits... I’ll get everything today. But your trip seems smooth? Thousands of miles, and you got here safe.”

Compared to his previous perilous bodyguard jobs, this trip was unusually calm.

Making money too easily made him uneasy.

Wade said, “Two possibilities.”

“One, those threatening her were just threats. She’s safe once she leaves Helsinki.”

He reasoned: even if some powerful force wanted her, they’d only act in Helsinki—not chase her thousands of miles. After all, writing an editorial isn’t exactly digging up ancestral graves.

“Two, they’re serious. We changed routes and shook them off, so you’re safe for now. But the closer you get to the negotiation spot, the more dangerous it will be. They know she’s meeting pirates and will wait at the endpoint.”

But then a question: getting intel from both Saudis and pirates—who were these people?

Not just any random folks could do that.

CoCo suddenly lifted his chin and gestured upward.

Looking back, Kimberly stood at the roof’s edge, arms crossed, holding the side of her wrapped skirt.

Wade smiled.

He stood and strode over. Looking up from below, the sun was rising, bright and glaring.

“Ms. Lee, want to come down?”

Kimberly nodded.

Wade narrowed his eyes and raised one finger. “One hundred euros, no bargaining.”

Kimberly stared. Wade raised an eyebrow, a hint of challenge in his look: “If you can, don’t come down.”

Just as he was feeling proud, someone shoved him hard, nearly making him stumble.

CoCo shouted, “Ms. Lee, fifty euros!”

Damn, wasn’t this about trust?

Never trust anyone who didn’t wear underwear before age eight! Lacking basic shame.

Wade’s gaze at CoCo was shark-like.

CoCo grinned, then his face fell.

He sulked over to Wade. “She doesn’t want me.”

Really? Wade was surprised but instantly relieved.

Since the trip started, besides reporting that black ship, this was her best move.

Looking up, she still stood there, bored. After a few seconds of eye contact, she winked.

He decided not to charge her.

CoCo was upset. “I don’t like this Ms. Lee.”

Wade replied, “You shouldn’t like her... You should like your wife.”

---

After lunch, the Reindeer called Wade. “I’m at the airport, finally sent the Saudis off.”

Airport?

Stockholm? Turkey? For a moment, Wade thought maybe the Reindeer was following his route.

Then he realized—they had left Helsinki.

Why leave so soon? They’d just arrived in East Africa; negotiations hadn’t started. Why withdraw?

“The Tiger Shark said they’ll contact you directly now. Since the Saudis sent Ms. Lee as their rep, they won’t interfere anymore. Just wait for news.”

“So, I stay in Khartoum waiting for the pirates to contact me?”

“No. You head northeast, cross the Nubian Desert to the coast. The pirates’ speedboat will meet you. They’ll contact you en route—the east is poor, infrastructure bad. I’ve told CoCo to help you get a military satellite phone. Don’t worry about communication.”

Wade was fine with that. “I’ll tell Ms. Lee we leave tomorrow.”

The Reindeer wished him well. “Enjoy Khartoum! It’s the best city in Sudan! And get along with Ms. Lee—Nubian Desert has less than one person per square kilometer. If she ignores you, you won’t find anyone to talk to.”

Wade said, “I’ll try to charge her less along the way.”

...

After the call, Wade made a list and went to Kimberly to see if she wanted to add anything.

She took it, carefully reading and tracing lines with her finger, sometimes muttering.

“Sunglasses—got it. Headscarf—got it. Medicine—got it...”

The power wasn’t back yet. She sprinkled cool water in the room, but it didn’t help much. Her skin was flushed, sweat shining on her forehead. A drop slid down her nose tip—clear, trembling, almost comical.

She didn’t look up, wiping it with the back of her hand.

Wade picked up the magazine to fan them.

Kimberly looked up.

“Bring more drinking water—at least double. Sudan has over twenty states; only two have water that meets international drinking standards. In many places, water is taken from puddles. We can’t drink that.”

“Also bring some electronic protective covers. Starting April, there are many sandstorms. The sand is fine and will damage equipment.”

“That’s all?”

“Yes.”

Good, all things he hadn’t thought of. Wade took the list.

From downstairs, CoCo’s voice could be heard, probably bragging to the owner about his shark jaw. Wade folded the list in half, lifting two corners and folding them toward the center.

He made a paper airplane.

Following the standard folding steps, the wings had an extra fold, making it slimmer than a normal plane.

He held it up, inspecting both sides, and asked her, “Know how to make a paper plane fly far?”

“You’re three years old?”

“You have no sense of humor.”

He blew on the nose, held it level, aimed at the door, squinting one eye.

The plane flew steadily, passed the doorframe, over the railing.

Wade shouted, “CoCo!”

Two minutes later, footsteps approached. CoCo peeked in, excited and sneaky, holding the disassembled paper.

“That’s all?”

“Yes.”

“No problem! Wade, wait for my flight report!”

He left excitedly.

Wade said meaningfully, “See? Men are all three years old.”

# Chapter 21

At dinner, CoCo returned from buying supplies, honking loudly as he drove in. The sound was booming, nothing like a tuk-tuk.

It was a secondhand white Toyota Hiace van. The previous owner had modified it—there was a cutout on the roof with a frame that could be raised, thickened steel plates, reinforced bumpers and shock absorbers, iron cages over the headlights, reinforced mirrors and all four doors, and a tall antenna at the rear topped with...

Wade frowned. The van was practical but ugly, old and dusty. The only new, bright thing was a plastic little bee on the antenna—bright yellow with black rings, two small white wings.

Wade asked, “What’s that?”

He wanted to pull the little bee off.

“Wiring for the radio!” CoCo waved the antenna. “No one’s around in the desert, signals are bad, gotta rely on radio to pass time.”

Wade pointed at the bee. “I mean that.”

“Decoration, looks good. Lots of locals have it.”

Really?

Wade felt unsure, but CoCo’s words made him think it actually looked nice.

The sliding door opened. The back half was packed with gear. Several large barrels of water stood out. Food was all instant dry rations. There was a woven basket with scattered dates, tomatoes, and a watermelon. A satellite phone stuck out of the basket, its antenna stretched like a little braid on top.

CoCo said, “Crossing the desert at full speed takes over ten hours. I expect you’ll take two days, so I packed five days’ worth of food and water. Use the satellite phone in open areas for better signal. Eat the fruit early or it’ll rot.”

But the most touching thing wasn’t that.

Wade looked inside, disbelieving. “This van has air conditioning?”

“Cooler.” CoCo knocked on the metal casing. “Old and noisy, but works well...” He turned on the switch.

A long-missed cool breeze blew over Wade. Khartoum was called the world’s furnace, but here and now, this small spot was paradise.

Wade gave CoCo a strong bear hug.

CoCo said, “You’re welcome. The Reindeer told me to find a fully equipped vehicle. The money’s all deducted from your pay anyway...”

Wade pushed CoCo’s head away.

---

After dinner, the power still hadn’t come back.

The guesthouse owner brought candles. Kimberly organized her luggage by candlelight, removing some winter clothes she no longer needed. Her bag gradually got thinner.

Suddenly, she spotted a golden square lipstick tube. She opened it, twisting out the lipstick, which was soft and oily, a dreamy, glossy red.

She felt a pang of regret, paused, then twisted it back and packed it.

Wade recalled past memories. “The first time I went to Lapland, I was inexperienced. I brought ointment for frostbite. When I needed it, I opened it—it had frozen solid.”

“The container was smashed, but the ointment was hard as a rock.”

“Later, a raven kept cawing over my head, a harsh sound.”

Northern ravens cawed: “Sss—kak—kak...”

Kimberly folded a white shirt, looking down.

“And then?”

The candlelight magnified her shadow, tinting her outline with soft golden hues.

“Then I threw the ointment out to hit the raven, and it flew away, dropping two feathers.”

Kimberly smiled. “You made that up.”

“How do you know?”

When telling Erinn, she believed it, stomping her foot, “What if you hit it and killed it or made it unable to have babies?”

“Who’d throw away medicine in a place that cold? It’s life-saving.”

True enough.

He didn’t throw it away. The raven kept cawing overhead. He cut some ointment, melted it over the fire, packed the rest in a plastic bag, and kept it warm against his body.

“You really like Lapland? I remember at the interview, Janus asked why you hadn’t taken an order for so long, and you said it was because you went to Lapland.”

Wade was stumped.

Why did he like Lapland? He hadn’t thought about it.

—Because it was cold.

Far north, vast and sparsely populated.

No people, no “human energy,” no complicated relationships.

—Because of the legend: when the northern lights appear, you can’t whistle or the aurora will grab your hair.

So he often whistled at the aurora in the middle of the night, then closed his eyes, waiting for someone to grab his hair.

—Because there, with reindeer, ravens, and wolverines, he was just another creature struggling to survive the cold.

They didn’t look at him strangely or ask where he came from or where his home was, didn’t care if he was off track or when a ship docked at the river mouth.

Why didn’t Erinn believe he went there just for vacation?

...

Kimberly didn’t ask more.

Suddenly, a paper airplane swooped in from the darkness outside, diving into the middle of the half-packed suitcase, its tail sticking high.

CoCo’s voice came through. “Wade! Mission accomplished. Give me an A, so I can have face on the way back.”

---

The next morning, they set off again.

Here, Wade and CoCo parted—one went east, the other south.

Wade didn’t have many friends. CoCo was a rare one, but they rarely met: one was cold-intolerant, the other heat-intolerant. Before Khartoum, they hadn’t seen each other for over two years.

This time, their reunion was brief: just “rode in the same car,” “drank together,” “ate meat,” and “flew paper planes.” It was nothing like the warm reunion he’d imagined.

CoCo seemed to feel the same, pulling him aside.

“You probably won’t come back in your life...”

He really understood him.

“In a few days, my work in the southern province will be done. I’ll go back home to Uda. It’s high altitude, rainy, usually around twenty degrees, not hot. After the high seas negotiation, why don’t you come stay with me for a while? Let my wife cook for you.”

Wade smiled. “No way. I’m sending Ms. Lee back.”

CoCo was surprised. “You don’t have to send her back... Didn’t you know?”

“What?”

“You didn’t read the contract carefully, did you?”

He didn’t. With the Reindeer around, he barely read contracts, just signed.

“That’s okay. He’ll tell you later: your protection of Ms. Lee lasts until the negotiation ends, not until returning to Helsinki. After the Red Sea, you’re free.”

Is that so?

Wade felt confused. “Why doesn’t she go back to Helsinki?”

CoCo shrugged. “How would I know? She has her plans. Maybe she has other places to go. Anyway, after the Red Sea, you’re done. Why worry? Bodyguard and client—that’s just a contract relationship.”

He got excited again. “How about coming to my place? My wife makes great pasta, better than Italians! I can take you to see the real African savannah. We’ll ride safari vehicles, drink beer, sleep with lions, ride giant crocodiles...”

Wade said, “You’re trying to get me killed.”

His mood dropped. “Let’s talk later. First, finish the job.”

---

The van left Khartoum.

Without much transition, the landscape quickly became barren—endless, overwhelmingly thirsty earth in shades of dusty yellow.

At first, there was a road, then only fragments scattered like asphalt shards. Tires crushed soft yellow soil, and the van kicked up thick yellow dust clouds behind.

Wade wanted to ask her what she planned after the negotiation.

Then scolded himself for being too nosy. CoCo was right—bodyguard and client were just a contract.

He reminded himself: focus on work, but keep distance from the client.

The cooler hummed—the only sound inside and outside the van.

Kimberly seemed to sense something and wisely stayed silent, staring out the window.

Actually, this wasn’t good—staring at monotonous scenery for a long time is hypnotic. Drivers must be careful; many highway accidents happen this way.

Sure enough, she soon fell asleep.

Wade sighed softly.

She slept, and he felt relaxed.

No other vehicles appeared. On the dunes’ ridges, small camel shadows moved like fingernails.

Occasionally, a lone tree stood in the desert, oddly grown in the middle of dunes. Leafless, its branches and trunk were jagged and white, like claws reaching for the sky.

Monotonous, dead, dull. His eyelids began to droop...

To stay awake, Wade turned on the radio.

The secondhand van’s radio filter was poor. The signal was weak, a constant “crackle” with no end.

Suddenly, a broken sentence came through: “We must be especially vigilant against those who have infiltrated us...”

The voice was angry and forceful.

He heard the south was going to war—government propaganda?

Wade was about to listen for more when Kimberly’s hysterical voice rang out: “Turn it off! Turn off the radio!”

Startled, Wade quickly pulled over and yanked the radio’s tangled power cord.

The crackling stopped. Only the cooler’s hum remained.

Kimberly lowered her head, pale, her hands on her knees trembling slightly.

After a long moment, Wade softly called, “Kimberly?”

She looked up, forcing a smile.

“I’m fine. You keep listening. I just... had a nightmare and didn’t react in time.”

The cooler was on, but her back was sweaty, clothes stuck to her.

Her nightmare involved the radio?

Kimberly avoided his gaze. “It’s stuffy inside. I’m going out for some air.”

Wade wanted to warn her about the heat, but when she went out, it wasn’t bad. The sky had darkened without his noticing, the sun hidden. Without the sun, the desert’s harshness seemed to have lessened.

He turned off the cooler to give it a break, opened the doors and roof for ventilation, then brought out the watermelon.

“Want some?”

He asked without much enthusiasm. Before she answered, he stabbed the melon with his knife.

The fruit was perfectly ripe, the bright red flesh spilling out. Wade held the knife in his mouth and pulled the melon apart with both hands.

A faint scrape came from the rear of the van. Looking up, the antenna wobbled. The little bee on top fluttered its wings, dizzy.

Wade chuckled.

“Wade?”

Kimberly’s voice sounded strange.

She stared at the ground. Tiny grains of sand swirled.

Wade started feeling uneasy.

The wind picked up, carrying the smell of dust and large animal urine.

Far away, a heavy, murky yellow sand wall rose rapidly, growing taller and almost touching the sky. At its edge, a shining line cut across like a blade.

Damn, something big was coming.

Wade hurriedly took a bite of the melon.
