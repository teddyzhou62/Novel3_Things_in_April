Wade woke up freezing, a thought flashing through his mind: “Enough. I’m heading south today!”

He had been in the Arctic Circle for four months. By then, he had retreated from the edges of the Arctic Ocean to the dense forests of Lapland, curled up inside an abandoned kota—a tent once used by the indigenous Sami people.

The kota resembled the Native American tipis, a tall cone with walls covered in layered reindeer hides, bear pelts, and thick felt to keep out the cold. Wade wrapped himself in animal skins and lay on a half-foot-thick bed of ashes. Before going to sleep, he’d lit a fire, so when he lay down, there was still a faint warmth.

Now, when he reached out to touch the ashes, they bit back with icy cold. A sudden chill bit his arm, sending numbness creeping up halfway to his shoulder.

It was time to head south. Four months here, especially the last stretch, and he’d seen fewer than a handful of people. They said that living alone in such extreme conditions could play tricks on your mind.

Just yesterday, Wade was certain he’d seen a reindeer sitting cross-legged on the ground, applying lipstick. The brand was Chanel, shade 99—a bright red. After finishing, the reindeer turned to him, puckering its lips as if asking for a kiss.

Wade even found himself critiquing the makeup: “You should’ve lined your lips.”
