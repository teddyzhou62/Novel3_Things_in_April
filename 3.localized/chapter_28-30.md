# Chapter 28

Inside the tent, there was a cacophony of banging buckets, splashing water, and bleating goats.

Then the tent flap was lifted, and Wade emerged, soaked from head to toe, probably too wet to bother drying off. He threw on a pair of shorts, and in one hand...

Yes, he was gripping the two front legs of a mountain goat, his face stern as he dragged it outside. The goat’s eyes were wide with panic, its hind legs kicking at the sand, stubbornly trying to resist.

“What are you doing? What are you doing? I just wanted to take a look—what’s your problem?”

Kimberly lifted a corner of her silk shawl and slowly fanned herself.

“Wade, you’re a foreigner who just arrived in this village. That goat is the villagers’ property. If you kill or injure it, the villagers will come after you—and that’s a diplomatic incident.”

Wade gritted his teeth. For a moment, he had actually thought about slaughtering it.

But just letting it go? He couldn’t accept that.

He kept dragging the goat outside.

Kimberly’s gaze followed him. Wade stopped in front of a shed and picked up a thick, sturdy wooden stake. He forced the goat upright, crossing its front legs over the stake, then wrapped a rope around it tightly two or three times, securing it firmly.

The goat stood stiff-legged and let out a desperate “bleat.” According to Darwinian evolution, it shouldn’t have been able to stand upright so quickly.

Stand there, then!

Wade wiped the water from his face.

Fortunately, the washing was nearly done. Although the bucket had spilled, only a little water was wasted. He went back inside, took down a towel hanging on the tent’s frame, and sulkily dried off as he sat beside Kimberly.

She kept fanning herself.

Wade couldn’t help asking, “Did you see that goat?”

“No,” Kimberly said sincerely. “I was too busy thinking about how to answer your question, so I didn’t pay attention.”

Alright, she couldn’t deny involvement, but what could he do?

Wade sighed. “Let’s get back to Somalia. What’s the deal with the pirates?”

Kimberly looked at him. “After something like that happens... it’s just over?”

He expected at least two complaints or a few curses, but she carried on talking about pirates like nothing happened. She must be fearless enough to captain a ship.

Wade said, “So what? You just got seen by a goat?”

Kimberly smiled. “Who knows? Only you and the goat know what really happened inside the tent.”

Wade’s teeth itched. “I chased it out seconds after it went in. What could have happened in that short time?”

Kimberly turned her head away, her skirt lifting slowly. She murmured, “Who knows? A glance can last forever, a moment is eternity. The Big Bang lasted just seconds, then everything was born.”

Wade laughed angrily.

Between clenched teeth, he said, “Kimberly.”

She turned toward him.

He pointed a finger at her but didn’t poke—he was holding back.

“You’re lucky you’re my client.”

The employer-employee relationship, the contract—they actually held some binding power for him.

If it were a reindeer, he would have torn its bones apart and stewed it long ago for such provocation.

If it were a CoCo tree, he’d have chopped it up for firewood to cook the reindeer.

You’re lucky you’re still sitting here. If you were my girlfriend, I’d have dragged you over long ago...

Kimberly glanced at him sideways. “So what if I’m your client?”

She tilted her head slightly, chin raised, the elegant tendons on her neck drawing his gaze down to the shallow hollow near her collarbone and the round curve of her shoulder.

His throat went dry. When he spoke again, his voice was low and hoarse, urgently needing a bucket of cold water, inside and out.

So he said, “Now tell me about the pirates.”

---

It was time to talk about pirates.

The glow stick’s light was almost gone. The whole fishing village was dark. The wind carried the sound of sea waves and a faint salty scent.

Kimberly said, “Pirates are just fishermen—very poor fishermen.”

“Since the Somali civil war, society and the education system have collapsed. The illiteracy rate is around 80%. English isn’t the official language. Sometimes, in smaller pirate groups, no one speaks English. To negotiate with shipowners, they have to pay someone who knows English and cover long-distance phone charges.”

Wade wanted to laugh. The pirate who called him spoke English fairly well. So Tiger Shark really is the biggest pirate leader here—the team has a decent range of “talent.”

“Their resentment has been brewing: first, their traditional fishing grounds are off-limits; if they go, foreign fishing boats drive them away. Second, destructive fishing policies have made fish scarce, cutting off their livelihood. Third, warlords fight endlessly, leaving starvation everywhere. Even the UN food aid gets stolen by armed groups...”

Wade fell silent.

He remembered White Robe telling him that Tiger Shark was once just a refugee collecting rations.

“A few years ago, the Indian Ocean tsunami unexpectedly uncovered an ecological disaster: some European countries took advantage of the local government’s incompetence and dumped nuclear waste and toxic chemical waste here.”

“But the tsunami washed the toxic waste ashore. Many people who lived along the coast and scavenged the debris were exposed to radiation. Over 300 died within a year.”

Wade was puzzled. “Europe is pretty far away. They hauled their trash all this way?”

“Europe has strict disposal standards for nuclear waste, costing around $1,000 per ton. But they contracted with the local government to pay $8 per ton to dump it here. Even with transport costs, it’s a bargain.”

Wade sighed.

He recalled the old man in Chinatown shaking his head, reciting ancient texts: “Life is like a tree in bloom.”

The womb bears fruit on the same tree, but where the petals fall is hard to say: into cesspools, onto banquet tables, at the front door, underfoot.

Where it’s precious, toxic waste must be sealed, isolated, and treated with high-tech methods. Why is this place so lowly? Eight dollars, dumped carelessly, followed by infection, mutation, and death.

“So it’s understandable why local fishermen hate everything—foreigners and their government alike. At first, when foreign ships passed by, they boarded to loot, sabotage, and detain crews purely out of anger.”

“Then one day, they found out that shipowners hired middlemen to offer ransom money to get their ships back. Turns out, you don’t have to fish to make money.”

“And that’s how the whole industry was born.”

The glow stick went out completely. The goat’s shadow stretched across the sand, accompanied by a few sorrowful bleats.

“Unless this country becomes truly strong, the pirate problem won’t be solved. The more you suppress them, the more brazen they get. The escort fleets in the Gulf of Aden are increasing, but pirate attacks aren’t decreasing—they’re rising.”

“Also, surveys show that over half of Somalia’s population support the pirates. They see them as heroes who vent their frustrations. Besides, pirates spend their ransom on lavish living. The local economy depends on pirate spending, creating a special supply chain: food, tobacco, alcohol, women. In other words, pirates support many livelihoods.”

She looked at Wade.

It was too dark to see her expression clearly—only her outline and eyes.

She said, “When you meet pirates tomorrow, don’t look at them with curiosity. Except for the leaders, most are poor followers—barefoot, illiterate, full of resentment, sick and unable to afford treatment, spending any money they get on debauchery. Don’t argue logic, reason, or legality with them. They don’t understand.”

Wade was silent for a moment, then smiled.

“You keep saying this ship isn’t important, but you’ve done your homework secretly.”

“I haven’t really—someone gave me a magazine analyzing pirates in Turkey. I flipped through it when I was bored.”

Wade’s interest piqued.

“You read it?”

“Of course. What else would I use it for? To fan myself?”

“What else did it say?”

“It mentioned experts criticizing the first shipowner who paid ransom, saying he set a terrible precedent. If pirates didn’t know ransom was possible, maybe there wouldn’t have been so many hijackings later. So far, the highest ransom paid in the Gulf of Aden is $1.5 million.”

No wonder the world’s eyes were on the Sirius. This time, the pirates demanded $20 million. Shipowners feared the Saudis would set a bad precedent again.

Wade lowered his voice to a whisper. “Can I ask... a question?”

He wanted to ask something probably considered a business secret, so he instinctively lowered his voice, fearing eavesdropping—though outside the wall, there was only the goat.

Kimberly leaned in slightly, also lowering her voice as if sharing a secret. “Go ahead.”

She really could act.

“What’s the Saudis’ psychological price?”

Kimberly touched the back of his hand with her fingertip, then lightly wrote the number “5.”

“Five million?”

“At most five million. My fee is 300,000.”

Twenty million and five million—this wasn’t just splitting the difference. They had to squeeze $15 million out of the pirates’ teeth.

Wade frowned, feeling at a loss.

“Are you confident?”

Kimberly smiled. “I agreed at first, then raised my price to 500,000.”

“What a coincidence—I think I saw that.”

She had made White Robe Janus jump up in fury with her sudden price hike. And she didn’t accept partial deposits—she demanded full payment upfront before departure.

Wade couldn’t understand. “How did he agree?”

“Because I told him if he gave me 500,000, I’d negotiate the ransom down to 3 million.”

Wade gasped.

Three million.

Would the pirates agree? That wasn’t just throwing them scraps—it was giving away the flesh.

“Miss, how will you negotiate?”

She said, “Once we’re on the ship, just don’t miss a word I say to Tiger Shark, and you’ll see how I negotiate.”

She added, “Don’t believe I can pull it off?”

Wade said, “I believe you.”

He lay back, hands clasped behind his head. The wooden bed frame was rough and uneven, with some knots still there, digging into his back.

He deliberately said again in a casual, indifferent tone, “I believe.”

Kimberly sneered, stood up, wrapped her shawl tighter, and said, “We’ll see.”

She walked back into the tent. Wade lay on the bed, smiling at her figure.

He couldn’t explain it himself, but when she said, “I’ll negotiate the ransom down to three million,” he felt an inexplicable excitement and pride.

Her departing figure looked like a warrior charging into battle.

Go ahead—make waves in the pirates’ world and turn it upside down.

I’ll be here to escort you.

He closed his eyes. Half asleep, a smile lingered on his lips as he murmured, “Three million.”

---

Outside the shed, the goat with its front legs tied up had resigned itself. Its head drooped to one side, utterly bored.

I just wanted to take a look... I only licked you once...

So dramatic.

# Chapter 29

Wade woke early, partly because today he would meet the pirates—figures who often made world headlines but were rarely seen.

Many Western journalists came chasing sensational stories but, due to Somalia’s dangerous situation, could only sulk in neighboring Kenya, then pay high prices for pirate stories.

This even spawned a new industry: many Kenyan scammers dressed in ragged clothes as pirates, collecting fees from reporters and spinning wild tales of violent, ruthless pirate life—though some had never even seen the sea.

The other half of the reason...

He had to release the goat before the villagers woke up; otherwise, who would believe he tied it up for anything other than meat?

The goat, half lying, half hanging, somehow managed to sleep. When untied, it woke up, eyes wide and confused.

Goats naturally have an aged, weary face. Wade grew more annoyed as he looked at it, pushing its head askew. “Get lost. Don’t let me see you again. You’d better forget last night, or I’ll slaughter you.”

Probably because it had been tied up all night, its front legs stiff and unable to stand. The goat lay on the ground for a while before getting up, stepping stiffly, its rear muscles twitching, tail flicking once.

How could it forget? Scientists found that mammals have excellent memories, goats included. They can recognize human faces, and some memories last as long as two years.

It would often recall that passionately intoxicating spring night.

Damn, tied up all night.

---

Kimberly hadn’t slept much longer.

Though she had casually said before, “It’s no big deal,” “Just a ship,” when the moment came, she couldn’t take it lightly. After all, it was the world’s largest oil tanker, the highest ransom ever demanded, and labeled by global media as the “most dangerous” pirate.

After washing up and eating some dry food, she entered the tent to change.

Wade boiled water in a titanium bowl with a folding handle, stirring in instant coffee. When it was just right, he tore open the packet, poured it all in, stirred, then set it aside to cool.

In this nearly primitive village, where the salty fishy and goat smells never left, the sudden aroma of coffee rising felt thrilling and romantic.

Kimberly came out wearing light ankle-length jeans and a white short-sleeve T-shirt—more conservative than a few days ago.

She seemed to know how to tone down her femininity in front of pirates. It was strange she had initially brought five evening dresses—where did she plan to wear those?

She pointed to Wade’s open duffel bag next to him. “The ship will have everything we need. We can pack light—just three to five days’ clothes. Put your stuff in my bag. You don’t need to bring your bag; leave it in the car.”

Santos had said nobody in the village stole anything, so there were no doors or locks. Lost items were extremely rare, and when they happened, it was usually the goats’ fault.

Kimberly sat down and took out a golden rectangular tube, twisting it open.

The shiny gold tube could double as a mirror. The lipstick inside was soft and shapeless. She dabbed some color on her lips with her fingertip.

Wade stared.

When he first met her, she seemed like a high-contrast black-and-white photo. Her red lips and the vermilion near her collarbone looked like someone had dipped their finger in cinnabar and painted it on the photo.

Cinnabar?

He noticed she still wore the garnet necklace by her collarbone. Despite all the changes—changing plans, outfits, and their relationship—it was the one thing she never took off.

It must have special meaning. Who gave it to her?

Kimberly noticed his gaze, tilting the gold tube mirror toward his eyes. “What are you looking at?”

Wade met her gaze directly. “The lipstick color looks great.”

It suited her perfectly—a wine red, not too heavy, complementing her porcelain skin.

Wade thought the color itself was sexy, a mix of red’s passion and black’s restraint—free and wild yet conservative.

Kimberly said, “I have prettier lipsticks, but someone threw them out of the suitcase.”

Wade corrected her, “That was polite removal and careful placement, not throwing.”

The coffee was nearly cool. With no extra cups, Wade rolled a white greaseproof paper into a cone, folding the tip to prevent leaks, poured the coffee in, and handed it to Kimberly.

She drank quickly and handed it back.

He was about to toss it—one advantage of greaseproof paper was it was biodegradable, heat and moisture resistant for short periods, and could be folded into cups, bowls, or plates—practical and light.

Suddenly, an impulse struck him.

He gently shifted the paper cup. On the rim was a faint wine-red lipstick print, clear enough to see the fine lip lines.

Kimberly didn’t look at him; she was touching up her makeup.

Wade placed the paper cup on the duffel bag’s sagging handle. It wobbled, almost falling, then a gust of wind made it topple into the bag’s open zipper.

Not my fault—it fell in by itself.

He looked at Kimberly. “Can I ask a question?”

“Do you ever not ask questions?”

“Can’t blame me—you wanted me to write my thoughts on you every day. Ask clear questions, so I can be honest.”

“Did you write it?”

Still brewing.

“...At least I won’t shortchange you when it’s due.”

“What else do you want to ask?”

“That necklace around your neck—does it have a story?”

Kimberly paused.

The sun came up, shining brightly on the golden tube in her hand, dazzling his eyes so he couldn’t read her expression.

“Yes, but I won’t tell you.”

It was fine. Wade felt he had enough patience. Every question had an answer. When the time was right, it would surface. No matter how tempting the bait, you can’t catch fish at the wrong time.

“Then another question—is it from a man?”

“No, I bought it myself.”

“Oh.”

His tone drew out, his heart suddenly light.

He stood and walked to the car, pulled out a pack of cigarettes, and lit one—a cheap Sudanese brand CoCo had given him, wrapped simply, with heavy smoke.

He didn’t mind, inhaling slowly and exhaling a cloud of smoke.

Better that it wasn’t from a man.

Though exactly why was unclear—once the negotiation ended, he’d have to get out of here fast, wouldn’t he?

The smoke drifted, revealing two figures walking along the dirt path at the village’s edge.

Wade squinted.

---

Both were tall, thin black men, wearing loose floral shirts and black shorts, their heads wrapped in white T-shirts. One wore sunglasses; the other...

Carried a gun.

An AK-type assault rifle, shiny black. The muzzle bobbed slightly with his steps. Wade’s spine stiffened, and his Adam’s apple rolled subtly.

The village’s atmosphere shifted.

The morning should have been noisy—like yesterday, with smoke rising, children washing the little goats, fishermen mending torn nets.

But now, only aimless goats wandered the path.

Every shack had someone inside. No one came out. Fearful eyes peeked from cracks, occasionally meeting others across the open space, trembling under the hot sun.

Yesterday, Wade had talked to Santos about pirates.

Santos said, “Everyone along the coast knows about pirates.”

“Somali pirates have a big name, but they’re far from us. Besides, what’s there to rob in a small fishing village?”

“We’ve encountered them once or twice at sea. When they’re fierce, they hijack ships; when less aggressive, they just take the cargo.”

“The worst is when they come into the village with guns, but luckily that hasn’t happened in years...”

The two men stopped a few meters away.

Wade felt invisible. Both stared only at Kimberly with strange, unfriendly looks, sizing her up.

Then one asked, “She’s the negotiator?”

His voice was rough.

Wade answered, “Yes.”

“Then let’s go.”

So blunt. Wade chuckled. “We haven’t packed everything yet.”

“Then hurry up.”

Are pirates always this terse? Or is it because their English is poor and they try to speak as little as possible?

He finished packing, pulling out weapons CoCo had given him: a Desert Eagle pistol, which looked like a toy compared to their AKs.

Just as he was about to holster it, a gun cocked right by his ear. The gunman held the rifle barrel level, muzzle almost touching his ear, shouting, “No guns!”

Wade said, “Hey, hey, calm down.”

He hooked his index finger on the gun and slowly raised both hands in surrender, then straightened and turned.

He looked at Kimberly. “Stay behind me.”

She stepped over. The gunman seemed tense, eyes fierce, muzzle pressed hard against Wade’s ribs.

Wade kept calm, speaking gently, “I’m the bodyguard. Bodyguards always carry guns.”

The man with sunglasses stepped forward, grabbed the gun barrel, and pulled it away.

He said, “No guns allowed on our ship. You’re here to negotiate. Negotiators must be peaceful—no guns.”

Bullshit. You’re here to negotiate too, so why bring guns and point them at me?

Wade suppressed his anger, paused, then smiled. “Fine.”

He flicked his wrist, tossing the Desert Eagle a few meters onto the sand.

The gunman didn’t relax, quickly stepping on the pistol, then picked it up and tucked it into his waistband.

Wade lowered his hands. “Can I finish packing?”

“Pack quickly.”

Wade cursed silently, shook the bag, and muttered, “Tiger Shark should have told his men to show some respect to you. They don’t seem very grateful.”

Kimberly didn’t reply. After a pause, she whispered, “No guns, okay?”

Wade frowned. “I don’t want to scare you, but this is the worst-case scenario—very dangerous...”

Kimberly’s lowered hand clenched without realizing.

Wade noticed but stayed composed.

He zipped up the bag, slammed the car door.

Then laughed. “Just kidding. No guns... their guns are mine. I’ll use them when I want—no need to carry my own heavy load.”

“Once on the ship, if it gets rough, keep your eyes wide open. Don’t miss any of my smooth moves... Then you’ll know what a top bodyguard is.”

# Chapter 30

They set off.

The gunman moved slowly behind them. Only the eyes and brows of his dark skin peeked from the white T-shirt wrapped around his face. He said, “Let’s go.”

Damn, what kind of welcome was this?

Wade’s anger flared. He slammed his backpack down hard. The gunman reflexively reached for the trigger, but the man with sunglasses quickly caught the gun’s bolt.

A faint “click.”

Wade stared at the man with sunglasses, who looked about forty, also with a white T-shirt wrapped around his head. A diagonal scar ran from his eyebrow to cheekbone—no wonder he wore sunglasses; he probably lost that eye.

Wade decided to call him Scar; the other would be AK—always pointing a gun, like it was his life.

He smiled. “If you act like this, I won’t be happy.”

“You guys must have hijacked too many ships and forgotten how to treat people normally. Escorting us with guns—what’s that about?”

“Negotiation means sitting at a table, face to face, equal footing, drinking tea, chatting, smiling, and settling things.”

“Pointing guns at people—do you think we’re prisoners of war or hostages? Is Tiger Shark like this? If so, forget the negotiation. Or call him now and talk about manners and rules before continuing.”

AK’s eyes flashed with fury.

Good. Let the negotiation start here. Whoever loses their temper first loses. Kimberly said pirates are desperate for ransom just like the Saudis want their ship back. For the sake of business, they won’t harm negotiators.

He bet these two wouldn’t dare act up.

Sure enough.

After a while, Scar cleared his throat twice, pushed AK’s gun down, and said, “Please.”

The kid could be taught—finally knowing the rules.

Wade laughed, bent to pick up his bag, dusted it off, and said to Kimberly, “Let’s go.”

Kimberly stood still.

“What if he shoots?”

“Huh?”

“When you slammed the bag, what if he lost control and shot you dead?”

Talking about that, Wade thought for a moment. “If he shot me, would you care?”

Kimberly smiled. “You brought it on yourself. Why should I care?”

She turned and walked. Wade watched her a moment, then followed with long strides. He reached out and grabbed her arm, suddenly remembering her injury. His hand slid up under her arm and gripped her shoulder.

Kimberly stumbled slightly.

No wonder the Eastern European woman at the masquerade said Kimberly’s shoulders were slender—he could cover her shoulder with one hand.

Kimberly glared at him.

Good. Finally angry, not that damp and dull expression anymore.

Wade said, “Can you have some faith in the ‘ace’? That’s not a title I bought.”

“Pirates are so poor, they save bullets. They probably haven’t had much shooting training. Look at the way he holds that gun—elbows loose, muzzle wandering. Do you think he can hit me?”

“I only have one life. Sometimes I flash the gun for show, but I don’t play with it.”

Kimberly’s expression softened.

Wade smiled. He liked smart people who reasoned things out. He noticed that when he helped her pack light.

Looking up, the pirates waited nearby, impatient but not pushing after Wade’s earlier rebuke.

“Actually, it’s a pity he didn’t shoot. I scoped it out; if I ducked low and swept his legs, he’d fall back, and the bullet would fly skyward... a neat move you won’t get to see... Let’s go.”

He reached out, palm pressing lightly against her lower back, sliding to her side—seemingly casual but taking advantage.

They finally moved, and the two pirates relaxed, leading the way.

The village atmosphere eased. Looking back, some shacks peeked out heads again. Further along, small groups stood in open spaces, looking lost and glancing this way.

He asked Kimberly, “Still confident about three million?”

She gestured toward the two ahead. “I don’t believe Tiger Shark didn’t tell them to be polite before coming out.”

“If this was his order, he wanted to intimidate me—a nervous person who acts tough.”

Quite self-absorbed. But Tiger Shark isn’t the only one putting on airs. Didn’t you also pretend at first, refusing to answer calls, saying only Tiger Shark could talk to you?

A faint flash of light flickered through his mind, like a warning, but he couldn’t grasp it.

Wade frowned.

Soon they reached the reef edge. The nearshore water was clear. A small fast boat bobbed by the shore, its bow tied with dirty, grimy rope knotted around a jagged rock.

Looking out, the sea seemed endless. If a reindeer were here, it would probably shout: “Wade! Look, that speedboat’s like a dumpling—barely fits Red Sea’s teeth!”

He wondered where the mother ship for negotiations was anchored—probably at least an hour’s boat ride away.

He asked Kimberly, “The Red Sea... is it calm?”

She said, “The Red Sea is an inland sea between Asia and Africa. Usually, the waves aren’t big, but you never know...”

Wade’s relief faded.

“There’s a story about why it’s called the Red Sea: when the Sahara’s red dust storms blow over, the sky turns red, the sea churns with crimson waves, and red cliffs rise along the coast...”

She shrugged. “I don’t know if there’ll be dust storms these days.”

Obviously not—there was one just a few days ago.

That idiot reindeer said doing business with the Saudis was like paving a shaky golden bridge—are there really that many easy profits in this world? It’s all sweat money.

Scar boarded the ship first. When AK followed, he suddenly yelped and jumped away—he stepped on a sharp stone.

Shoes really are mankind’s great invention...

Another faint flash of light brushed his mind and slipped away again.

A chill rose in Wade’s heart.

This wasn’t the first time. He’d had similar flashes before—a car crash, a gunshot wound.

There’s a saying in the industry: Death wields a scythe, harvesting heads like rice stalks. People on the edge see reflections on the scythe before danger strikes.

That reflection—the faint light in his mind—is a bad omen and a reminder to survive.

What exactly is it?

Will the weather turn bad? Will a dust storm strike soon? Or is Tiger Shark’s setup a trap?

AK impatiently urged them aboard.

Wade helped Kimberly onto the speedboat. It was small, like a flat skiff, open and uncovered. There was a bucket of gasoline for the engine, and a plank across the middle served as a seat—a natural barrier separating pirates and them, like a river dividing two armies.

The engine roared. The boat sped toward the unseen heart of the sea.

The fast ride brought wind and jolts. The shore quickly vanished. All around was emerald green, sparkling in the sunlight.

The Red Sea is the hottest sea in the world, summer temperatures above 30°C. Some joke that bathing there is like a hot bath. Wade was already feeling overheated and unpacked a shirt to drape over Kimberly.

She whispered, “A bit dizzy.”

Wade wrapped an arm around her to steady her from the bumps and jolts. This kind of sun-exposed, rough sea life wasn’t meant for someone like her...

He looked up. Scar steered the boat, waves small but the smaller the boat, the more frequent the jolts from currents. AK seemed uncomfortable too, crouched in the cabin, cursing, gun resting on his stomach, muzzle pointed at them—intentionally or not.

His foot rested on the plank, sole facing Wade’s face.

A red mark from the sharp stone.

No manners at all...

In a flash, Wade suddenly thought of something and instinctively tightened his arm.

Kimberly glanced at him curiously.

Wade didn’t look at her. His eyes flicked between Scar and AK, then he smiled.

Turning to Kimberly, he ran his hand through her hair, palm cupping the back of her neck, forcing her to face him. With a playful tone, he said in English, “You were wild last night. I was crazy about you.”

He used slang.

Out of the corner of his eye, Scar said nothing. The white T-shirt around his face loosened slightly, revealing a lip muscle twitching in disgust.

AK gave Kimberly a strange look—contemptuous and dismissive.

Kimberly stared at Wade.

He still smiled, leaning close to her ear, switching to Chinese: “Come on, push my hand away, and tell me in English to behave. Keep flirting with me all the time. Important things we say, we say quietly in Chinese.”

Kimberly’s eyes tightened briefly, then she smiled, lowered her head, and pushed his arm away. “Annoying.”

Wade laughed loudly, shamelessly leaning in again, kissing her ear, whispering, “Can you swim?”

“Yes.”

She was a little nervous. Wade grabbed her drooping hand and squeezed it firmly.

“Now, listen carefully to everything I say and follow it.”

“If fighting breaks out, try to get into the cabin, like during the dust storm—stay as low as possible.”

“If it gets worse, jump into the sea. Don’t swim far; stray bullets can hit you. Stay close to the ship, away from the engine. I’ll come find you.”

Kimberly nodded in his arms, softly asking, “Why?”

“These two aren’t pirates.”
