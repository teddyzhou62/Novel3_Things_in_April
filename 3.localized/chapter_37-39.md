# Chapter 37

The speedboat pulled up alongside the fishing vessel, lowering the gangplank. Wade waited for two pirates to board, slipping in between them as the third, then helped Kimberly up.

The crew gathered around, as if inspecting some rare animal.

A young pirate tried to push his way through, eager to watch the commotion. Someone nearby found him annoying and kicked him down. Furious, the boy sprang up, drawing his knife with a swipe.

Pointing at the kicker, he shouted, “You! Die! Now!”

Though pirates didn’t speak English fluently, repeated raids forced them to learn some words for intimidation or frequent use: “die,” “eat,” “sit,” “go.” The most common phrase was “you die,” followed by “now,” “today,” or “tomorrow”—meaning you’re going to die right now, today, or surely tomorrow.

Each utterance was a torment for the hostages.

Before the boy could say more, the pirate leader who boarded the speedboat slapped him aside. “Get lost!”

Laughter erupted among the crowd. The boy sniffled, glaring fiercely at his tormentor.

He was just eleven or twelve, small face, huge black eyes set off by the stark white of his sclera. That cold glare made Wade’s heart skip.

So young, so fierce—among these people, in a few years, he’d be a shark of the Red Sea devouring others.

Elsewhere, boys his age might be playing with kittens, hugging dogs, or complaining about homework.

Suddenly, a strange, hoarse laugh broke out from the perimeter. “We meet again, Jin!”

The crowd parted.

Wade finally saw the infamous Tiger Shark, a pirate feared across the seas.

He was black, not tall, somewhat stocky and bulky. His chin jutted forward; lips curled back; his massive head tilted permanently to the left, fused with his left shoulder. A white scarf covered his neck as a disguise.

At his waist hung an unexpectedly ornate, gold-plated revolver—probably stolen from a merchant captain—a gleaming symbol of status.

Unable to pronounce “Cen,” he called Kimberly “Jin.”

Tiger Shark laughed as he approached. “The Saudis didn’t lie to me. Long time no see, Jin! Your hair’s shorter, ha, and you’re thinner than before! Hmm, you don’t seem to like smiling now...”

Wade glanced at Kimberly.

Did she have long hair back then? A ponytail? A bit chubbier—baby fat? What a pity. If he had known her then, he could have pinched her cheeks—must have felt so soft...

Kimberly smiled faintly. “Too tired.”

“I know! The Saudis told me you’re absolutely safe on this ship! If anyone dares come, I’ll blast them away! Look!”

He pointed to the side, where a young pirate cradled a shoulder-fired rocket launcher.

“If they get close, I’ll blow up the ship and the people with it! Come, come, have you eaten? Come in.”

If it weren’t for this ship, this sea, and this strange crowd, Wade might have thought he’d stepped into a hospitable host’s home.

On the way to the cabin, it was like watching a monkey circus. Tiger Shark exploded with rage several times, roaring as he charged at pirates, punching or kicking them. Then he’d turn back to Kimberly and explain:

—I told him to clean up here! This pig won’t move unless I hit him!

—I told them there’s an important guest coming, put on some clothes!

—I said the fresh water here is off-limits! Reserved for guests!

...

Wade couldn’t help but chuckle. Seizing a moment, he whispered to Kimberly, “Pirates aren’t easy to control, huh?”

Kimberly replied, “Pirates aren’t soldiers. Discipline is poor, no one obeys anyone. You see enough, you’ll understand.”

—

The cabin’s small dining room was already set with a “feast.”

Wade had long known better than to expect gourmet meals or culinary skill from pirates.

The main dish was potatoes stewed with sea fish, but the odd seasoning couldn’t mask the fishy smell. The rest were canned and instant foods—clearly looted—packages marked in various languages, including Chinese.

Drinks were canned cola and beer.

With the door closed, four people remained in the dining room: Kimberly, Wade, Tiger Shark, and the English-speaking pirate leader, Shadi.

Two sat, two stood, eating while negotiating. Wade distractedly spooned canned baked beans, washing it down with beer nearby. Shadi glanced at him, a bit envious but too wary to be so bold.

Wade deliberately teased him, raising his beer can in a “Cheers” gesture.

Shadi turned away, probably deciding not to engage further.

But Wade listened closely to every word at the negotiation table.

Tiger Shark said, “Jin, I don’t know if this suits your taste. We eat casually on the ship, no grand feasts. When negotiations succeed, I’ll take you to Bosaso...”

What a scoundrel. If negotiations succeed, you’ll go your separate ways, right? Who agreed you’d bring her to Bosaso?

Kimberly said, “Having food is already good.”

Tiger Shark replied, “It’s been a tough journey, but no choice. Such a big ship—I have to be careful...”

Kimberly nodded. “I understand. I should cooperate. It’s okay.”

Tiger Shark said, “The Saudis told me you’d come as the negotiator. At first, I couldn’t believe it—you saved my life. Jin, I can’t charge you a high ransom. I’m willing to reduce it to ten million to show my sincerity...”

Kimberly smiled. “We’ll talk about the ship later. We haven’t seen each other in a long time... After I left Somalia, where did you go? Straight into maritime business?”

Tiger Shark hesitated, then said, “Well... no. I took some time to recover—you know, I was injured...”

Kimberly’s expression became concerned. “Right, how’s the wound healing? I remember the medic said it was hard to fully recover. Any aftereffects?”

...

Wade almost laughed out loud.

Kimberly’s skill at changing the subject was truly masterful. Every time Tiger Shark mentioned the ship or ransom, she replied with unrelated topics: Red Sea weather, fish species now abundant, Somalia’s new government not recognized by anyone...

Until the meal ended, the topic never returned. Kimberly’s last question at the table was, “Where will I sleep tonight? I’m really tired. The wind on the way over was relentless—I just want a good rest.”

—

It was clear Tiger Shark had put effort into hosting Kimberly: a small cabin of just a few square meters was prepared with a single cot and a small table. A curtain in the corner offered a shower area—a faucet high on the wall connected by a hose to the adjacent water tank, with a drain hole letting wastewater flow outside.

Nothing was prepared for Wade, probably because he wasn’t considered important. After Kimberly closed the door to shower, Shadi took Wade through the nearby passages and restroom. On the way back, he said, “You can sleep on the deck, in the wheelhouse, or the dining room—any place you can lie down.”

Wade said, “No thanks. I’ll just sleep outside Ms. Lee’s door.”

“Oh,” said Shadi.

He pulled some tea leaves from his pocket and started chewing slowly. Wade sat by Kimberly’s door, judging the width of the corridor. “No room for a palm mat—just give me a cushion. I can sleep sitting up.”

“A cushion’s enough?”

“Yes.”

Shadi kept chewing, then suddenly grinned, revealing teeth stark white against his dark skin.

“You don’t have to pretend. You can sleep in her room. I saw it last night.”

He chewed and walked off.

Wade sat a while, cursing silently: Damn.

It felt like a goose that had spent a thousand years chasing ducks, only to have a duck peck out its eye.

He gritted his teeth and knocked.

Kimberly had just finished showering, wrapped in a shawl as she came to open the door. Seeing no one, she looked down and found him sitting there.

“What are you doing sitting here?”

Wade looked up. “I got bullied.”

Kimberly smiled. “So you have your day too.”

She slammed the door and went inside. Wade laughed, propped the door, then got up and followed her in.

She sat back on the bed. A dim fishing lamp on the table gave off a faint yellow light, flickering as if ready to go out. She sat wrapped in a rust-red shawl, its dark gold embroidery shimmering oddly.

Like a painting, alive only by this weak light. If the light went out, she would vanish.

The lamp flickered again. Wade’s left arm twitched strangely. He leaned against the door, trying to suppress the sudden unease.

Kimberly looked at him oddly. “What’s wrong?”

Wade smiled. “Come here. I’ll tell you a secret I’ve never told anyone.”

Kimberly hesitated, then came over. “What secret?”

Wade wrapped his right arm around her waist, pulling her close. He kissed her temple, lingering.

“I first got mixed up in Chinatown when I was starving. I stole food. But I had pride—I didn’t steal in the streets. I went to the white neighborhoods.”

“I didn’t dare steal anything big—just enough to eat: bread, milk, biscuits.”

Kimberly smiled, resting her head on his chest, listening to his heartbeat. “Then?”

“One time, I got caught. I jumped out a window to escape. The owner was a grumpy middle-aged white man, yelling behind me that if I came back, I’d regret it.”

“I didn’t dare go back for a long time. But one day, starving, I wandered back. Only their house had food on the table.”

“The guy was there, working out while watching TV. He turned around—I wanted to run, but he didn’t see me and turned back to the TV. After a while, he left the living room.”

His tone changed. Kimberly tensed. “A trap?”

Wade kissed her lips softly. “Smart girl.”

“I watched at the door a while longer, saw nothing suspicious. Then I sneaked in to open the door. I carried wire—could jimmy any lock.”

Kimberly looked up. “You mean...”

“I got shocked the moment I touched the door. Didn’t pass out, but flew back a meter. Left side of my body went numb. Mouth tasted metallic. I admired myself—I saw the guy, still managed to get up and run like hell.”

“I ran all the way back to Chinatown, only then did I realize my left arm couldn’t move. I panicked, afraid it was ruined. Didn’t dare tell anyone—too embarrassing. No money for a hospital.”

Kimberly felt a pang. She wrapped her arms around him and softly asked, “Would kissing me make you feel better?”

Wade smiled. “It would. But wait—let me finish.”

“I was lucky. After worrying all night, the next day, my arm started moving again.”

“But since then, something strange happened.”

He lowered his voice. “Whenever I feel something strong—fear, joy, anxiety—my left arm senses it first, before any other part.”

He held out his left arm. “Feels like an electric current from wrist to elbow... Weird, huh?”

She was so decisive, Wade felt uneasy.

“Not going to hesitate, put on airs, or make me work for it?”

Kimberly smiled and leaned into his arms.

The sea wind tossed her tangled hair across his face. From the deck, pirates’ noisy shouts rose and were quickly hushed.

Wade felt his ship had finally moored at the gentlest shoal.

He whispered, “Just going with me like that? Not asking where I’m taking you?”

She shook her head in his arms.

No questions.

Willingly embracing this wild, reckless madness—with you as my only direction.

She said, “After we get off the ship, I’ll follow you until...”

Until you don’t want to take me anymore.
