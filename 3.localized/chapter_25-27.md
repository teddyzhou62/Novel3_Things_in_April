# Chapter 25

The desert at night offered surprisingly good visibility. Silver moonlight bathed every undulation of the dunes, highlighting countless tire tracks pressed into the sand.

With a satellite phone’s GPS coordinates, Wade wasn’t worried about losing his way. And without a fixed rendezvous point, he felt even more at ease: as long as the general direction remained the same, maybe by sunrise they’d catch sight of the coast.

The deeper into the night, the quieter it became.

Passing by nomads’ tents, the car’s headlights swept over numerous sheep, some startled, some lying still.

At a gold prospector’s camp, someone peered out of their tent in confusion. Empty cans and water pipes were scattered nearby.

They drove through a supply town—no lights, no voices. The low houses looked like haphazardly stacked blocks. The vehicle sped down the empty streets, kicking up a cloud of dust that trailed behind, leaving yet another fresh set of tracks.

This desert was almost gentle.

Wade thought this could easily rank as one of the best moments and scenes of his life.

Unprepared, unexpected, stumbling into it—so gentle that the only response was to embrace it, unwilling to let go.

Kimberly whispered, “I wish this road never ended.”

Wade glanced at her. “Could you think about the driver’s feelings when you say that? If it never ends, you want to wear me out?”

Kimberly smiled.

“Want me to drive for a while?”

Wade shook his head. “Don’t steal my job. Just talk to me now and then, keeps me awake.”

She was doing well tonight—not falling asleep immediately.

Kimberly said, “I’m really craving something to eat right now.”

“Yongfu Lin cooks well. The first dish I had from him was sweet and sour pork—chunks of meat coated with a thin, crispy layer of sweet and sour glaze. It was crunchy, sour with a touch of sweetness, and a little spicy…”

Kimberly chimed in, “The Japanese chef I hired makes Arctic surf clams every meal. Chilled, with rose-red edges on the shell, cool to the touch, fresh and tender, silky smooth. A dab of mustard in the dipping sauce…”

Wade interrupted, “Stop, stop, you should just get some sleep.”

He had only eaten compressed biscuits, a few dates, and a bite of melon today—too little to handle such temptation.

Kimberly sighed wistfully. Wade glanced quickly at her—her pale teeth lightly biting her lip. In that moment, she was both adorable and tempting.

Compared to when they first met, she gave him a very different feeling now. It wasn’t that any side was fake—there was a contradictory harmony, a complex coexistence.

“Can I ask you a question?”

“Do you always ask your clients so many questions?”

“No.”

“I’m usually cold, don’t talk much—like a wall.”

“And now that wall has come alive?”

Wade laughed.

It was hard to explain.

At first, he probably just wanted to make the journey easier, to find some fun along the way—otherwise, how dull. He was a wall, she was a painting, and this trip was just the painting hanging on the wall, battered by wind and sand, with no visitors.

Then he actually wanted to talk to her, sometimes deliberately provoking, sometimes just teasing.

So what? Even the proudest peacock, when faced with a potential mate, spreads its feathers, twists its body, ruffles its plumage, and dances to catch attention.

He said, “Not really. I’m not interested in them, so I don’t have much to say.”

Silence filled the car for a while.

From afar came a wolf’s howl, carried by the wind.

Damn it.

He knew wolves lived in the desert, but nature providing that background noise right now was hardly friendly.

Kimberly turned to look at him. “Saying that… means you’re interested in me?”

Wade didn’t look away. “Smart people speak plainly. I never hid my interest in you. I wasn’t subtle about it either. If you still haven’t noticed—then ignore what I said. I overestimated you.”

They say three things in a person can never be hidden: poverty, coughing, and liking someone.

So just lay it out in the sun. Even without a response, at least the light shines bright.

“What if I’m not interested in you?”

Wade shrugged. “Lots of people like literature. Does literature like them back? Doesn’t stop them from reading and buying books.”

“What were you about to ask me?”

Oh, right. The question. He almost forgot.

“Why do you like wearing evening gowns so much?”

“Because they’re pretty.”

“Just that?”

“Yeah.”

Wade thought she was telling the truth, but not the whole truth.

No matter—loving beauty was fine, and he liked seeing women look beautiful.

---

In the latter half of the night, he told Kimberly to stop forcing herself to stay awake—if she wanted to sleep, she should.

He occasionally stopped to nap for a few minutes or smoke a cigarette, then continued once refreshed.

During one stop, he started feeling cold. The desert’s temperature swings were huge, sometimes dropping below freezing at night. Though it wasn’t that extreme here, the chill was still intense.

Looking at Kimberly, she seemed cold too, curled up tightly in her seat.

Wade got up, went to the front, then climbed into the back of the vehicle to grab a blanket. He draped it over her and tucked the edges into the seatbelt. As he did, he caught a glimpse of her face.

His heart skipped a beat, and he leaned closer.

This probably woke her.

Most people couldn’t tell, but Wade could distinguish between pretending to sleep and truly sleeping by breathing patterns, eye calmness, and eyelash movement.

He studied her lash roots, then gently blew on her eyelashes.

Her eyes twitched; her lashes fluttered—a reflex only the awake could fake.

Wade smiled and ran his hand along her brow, down her cheek, to her lips.

He lowered his head and kissed her eyelids.

His lips felt the slight shiver of her eyes, the soft flutter of her lashes brushing against his mouth, tickling him.

He thought, *I know you’re awake.*

---

Kimberly woke up to the sound of waves.

She sat up, a little dazed.

It wasn’t fully dawn yet; the sea breeze was cool. The car was parked on a rocky shore with all doors open. The satellite phone hung from the side mirror, its antenna stretched long.

Looking back the way they came, a small fishing village lay low—just a few dozen households. The shacks leaned crookedly, as if ready to collapse. A solitary goat slowly wandered the open ground.

Where was Wade?

She got out, shading her eyes, scanning around until she found him.

He was swimming in the sea, riding the waves. White crests engulfed him. Kimberly thought he might disappear—

But the next second, he surfaced again.

She sat cross-legged on the ground, watching him until he came ashore and shook the water from his face.

Kimberly closed her eyes.

On her brow, she still felt the soft kiss—warm and uncooled by the wind.

Then suddenly, droplets splashed her face.

Opening her eyes, Wade was smiling right at her.

He sat beside her, drenched—his shorts soaked and clinging to his body. On his back were small wounds, the wet patches tinged with blood.

Kimberly frowned, then looked away.

This wasn’t her concern. She wouldn’t get involved.

Wade pointed to the satellite phone hanging from the mirror. “I sent the GPS coordinates and called them. We arranged a meeting for tomorrow.”

“Tomorrow?”

“After a night of driving, I think you need to rest—build up your momentum. Isn’t that what you said about negotiations needing momentum?”

Kimberly nodded.

After a pause, she stood, grabbed her bag, found a cigarette, lit it, took a long drag, then exhaled slowly.

The smoke blurred her face.

Wade suddenly felt like some things had gone back to the way they were before.

She came over and sat beside him.

“Resting a day’s good. Recharge, then tackle this all at once. Get it over with sooner.”

“Not that easy, right? Some ships have been detained over 25 months, and negotiations have been tough.”

He didn’t want the talks to fail, but he also didn’t want them to end too quickly.

Kimberly smirked. “That’s because neither side is sincere, and the negotiators lack ability. If I were negotiating, it wouldn’t take that long.”

“So confident? But you said you don’t know Tiger Shark?”

“I don’t need to know Tiger Shark. I just need to understand people.”

Wade laughed. “Sounds like you have everything under control. Yet you don’t even understand the person closest to you.”

Kimberly looked at him sharply. “Who? Ming Jiang?”

“With all his cunning and planning, how did he get caught red-handed? Is he harder to deal with, or are you careless?”

Kimberly smiled. “You want to hear about that?”

“I know Ming Jiang better than anyone.”

“He gets nervous speaking in public, sweating profusely. So he brings two shirts to change mid-event.”

“He studied abroad following his family’s wishes to immigrate. Many of his ideas are traditional. He’s a good man, tolerant, but there are things he absolutely can’t accept—like a woman cheating on him.”

Wade paused, a strange feeling creeping over him.

Kimberly kept smiling, tapping the cigarette butt on a nearby rock.

“He’s gentle, timid, faints at the sight of blood, deeply disturbed by violent scenes—can’t even think about them. Someone like that, if he wanted to die, would choose a gentle way, not jumping off buildings or slashing wrists.”

“He never suspected who halved his medicine and mixed in vitamins. Nor why his friend ‘just happened’ to visit for basketball, why the door was ‘just barely’ left open, letting that friend discover the suicide scene.”

Wade stared at her. “You arranged that?”

Kimberly didn’t look at him. She pressed the cigarette butt firmly into the ground.

“So, who exactly has the right to call me his ‘disaster’?”

“If he thinks the woman he met later is his true love, who should he be most thankful to?”

# Chapter 26

The fishing village woke early.

First, another goat wandered by. Smoke rose from chimneys; voices grew louder. People mended nets, others dried seafood on the rocks. Though the sky was still dim, the day’s rhythm had begun.

The van stood out and was a curiosity. Several kids, dragging a goat to bathe, gathered around, fascinated. Wade talked to them; they laughed but didn’t understand. Then they jabbered excitedly among themselves.

Wade didn’t understand either.

He glanced at Kimberly, who shook her head. “Some African countries have hundreds of local dialects; languages aren’t unified. But fishing villages that trade outside always have someone who speaks English. Ask them.”

Wade shooed away the scrambling kids and shouted, “English! English!”

The children laughed, dragged the goat back to the village, then returned moments later, crowding around a middle-aged man with a red face and curly hair, who shouted back, “English!”

Wade was puzzled. Couldn’t they leave the goat and just call someone? The kids ran too fast; the little goat couldn’t keep up, dragged on the ground, looking utterly miserable.

The man was Santos, owner of a speedboat. He often ferried goods to Yemen’s fishing boats. The nearby countries were unstable, with little regulation. Smuggling and border crossings were common. Fishermen knew little about laws, thinking fishing and selling fish was their right.

This place felt like a barren backwater.

Santos said the village was called Bhuku.

“No phones here. If you want to call, you drive over twenty miles north to a bigger village with a community office and a phone. There’s a cop who comes once a week to settle disputes. Bhuku doesn’t have police; if something happens, people settle it themselves.”

A cop once a week—what a meager presence.

“Everyone fishes by the sea. I’m the only one with a boat here. A few families can afford nets—our nets go out to sea overnight, and we haul fish in the morning…”

“Where do you stay? Just check the village. Any empty house, you can stay.”

“Are you from National Geographic?”

He actually knew National Geographic.

“A couple years ago, an American came, said he was a photographer for National Geographic, took a bunch of photos and left. Last year, a Frenchman said the same. Where are your cameras?”

Santos peeked inside the van.

Wade pointed at the cracked window. “We hit a sandstorm; the camera got blown away.”

Santos nodded in understanding.

---

The shacks in the fishing village were... indescribable.

No wonder they leaned crookedly—it was obvious how they were built: branches roughly cut and driven into the ground, tied together with straw. Gaps of varying sizes between sticks. A large plastic sheet covered the roof; some added thatch for extra protection.

When the wind blew stronger, the shack would lean more. Even stronger, it’d lean further. Goats would nibble on the straw bindings, making the shack lean even more.

When it was too crooked to live in, they’d rebuild.

Did such shacks cost much? They were built in moments, only to fall apart with wind or goat bites.

Empty houses were the most crooked.

Wade parked at the entrance and set up a tent inside. Once the sun rose, the heat would be unbearable. Though crooked, the shack and tent together provided double shade. Kimberly would be more comfortable.

He glanced back at Kimberly. She sat in the car, expressionless, eyes lowered, indifferent to the curious villagers staring at her.

After swimming in the sea, something felt off. Wade vaguely sensed he’d made a mistake last night.

He couldn’t figure out what.

With the tent set up, he went to the van for luggage. Kimberly tried to get out, but suddenly the sky darkened.

Wade blocked her.

She looked up at him once, then sat back down.

“Is it because I kissed you last night that you think I’m too wild?”

“No.”

“Then why?”

“Because you’re not wild enough.”

Wade didn’t understand.

They were alone, miles from civilization. If desire ran unchecked, he could have done anything to her.

He hadn’t. He only let himself think about it occasionally.

Last night, he could have been more reckless, but he wasn’t—he was almost hesitant. Sometimes when you like someone, you speak softly, handle them gently—like caring for a flower. He never plucked flowers; he preferred to nurture them, tend the soil, build shade, shield from wind and rain.

What’s the point of breaking a flower and keeping its scent on the bedside for one night? He wanted far more than possession.

Kimberly smiled. “That day, on the plane, I was the one who made the first move. You made me think—was it just a moment of impulse, looking for comfort?”

“It was. I was seeking comfort.”

“I thought you felt the same. We got along well, saw eye to eye. On this boring trip, if it was mutual, kissing, sleeping together wouldn’t be impossible. After all, you didn’t marry me, and I didn’t marry you. A little impulse wouldn’t hurt anyone.”

“But you took it seriously. You kissed my eyes—I knew you cared.”

She looked up at Wade.

Which passionate man would be so tender, so gentle as to kiss a woman’s eyes?

“Then it’s too embarrassing. I was playing, but you were serious. That’s not fair.”

“But it’s okay. The negotiations start soon. Within three or five days, I can settle the ship. Then everyone goes their own way—you know our contract is valid until the negotiations end, when Tiger Shark agrees. Then you’re free.”

She got out again.

This time, Wade stepped aside.

Kimberly walked past him into the shack, lifted the tent flap, and crawled inside.

The groundsheet was smooth. She sat down. The tent’s fluttering flap let in noise and bright light through the gaps.

So hot.

---

The foreigners and the van were more novel to the village than a shark stranded on the rocks. Wade endured being poked and stared at by nearly everyone, yet hadn’t charged admission.

Children were the most curious and enthusiastic, and with nothing else to do, they just wouldn’t leave.

Santos felt it was his duty to accompany the foreign guests since they didn’t speak the local language. Even unpaid, it was an honor.

With his translation, Wade quickly bonded with the children.

At the entrance, the noise gave Kimberly a headache. She lifted the tent flap slightly—

Wade sat at the shack’s entrance, surrounded by lively kids. A gray donkey carrying water bags stood nearby. Even the donkey came to watch?

One small black child clung to Wade’s shoulder, using his back as a climbing wall.

Don’t you realize your back is injured?

For a moment, Kimberly wanted to stride over and toss the kid aside.

She clenched her teeth.

It wasn’t her business. Let him be, even if his back got trampled to pieces, he deserved it.

Wade suddenly looked back.

She quickly closed the tent flap.

After a while, someone knocked twice on the tent frame.

“Kimberly?”

“Yeah.”

He lifted the flap, squatted at the entrance. “Want to talk to you about something.”

“There’s no well in this village. The nearest fresh water pond is two kilometers away. When fishermen fetch water, they borrow donkeys from families that have them to carry water bags.”

“A kid just returned with water. I checked—it was all muddy.”

“I gave them some of our water to drink. They were amazed—said they’d never seen water so clear.”

“I thought about it: if we leave tomorrow, we still have plenty of water in the car. I’ll reserve enough for you to drink and use the rest to trade for their water.”

“I can filter their water simply. You can shower with it, no problem. Sound good?”

Kimberly didn’t look at him. “Whatever. The CoCo tree gave you the water, not me.”

Wade felt a pang.

“At first, when I gave water to the kids, they didn’t dare drink it. Said they hadn’t seen such clear water and were afraid it might kill them.”

Kimberly said, “It’s wild how different the world is, right? Some people hold gold in their hands and still feel it’s not enough, while others risk their lives just for a sip of water.”

Wade was silent for a moment, then stood.

Kimberly thought he was leaving, but he didn’t.

She looked up at him.

Wade smiled.

From the first time they met, she noticed he loved to smile—casually, dismissively, slyly, with hidden sharpness.

He said, “Kimberly, if you don’t want to get involved with me, just say so. No need for so many words.”

“I like you. I say it plainly, no hidden meanings. I just don’t like guessing games or secrets.”

Just like that time when he realized Erinn was serious, he told her straight: “Erinn, there’s really no spark between us.”

Erinn said, “You need friction to create sparks. You keep your distance; no friction, no sparks.”

He groaned, “I think even you don’t know what you want. Figure it out before coming back.”

Life was full of questions—like how Erinn suddenly realized she liked women.

---

Wade continued.

“Now I get what you mean. I’ll hold back, won’t make you uncomfortable. The negotiations end in a few days. I don’t want to see you always frowning. Can we get along? I like seeing you smile.”

“And...”

He crouched down.

“Don’t say you’re just playing. That’s not you. Real players don’t care if the other is serious or not, where they kiss you, or if they have to save Ming Jiang. Players have no heart. You have one.”

He knew she did—when she covered the tea bowl in the white robe, he saw.

Kimberly’s lips twitched faintly.

It was hot in the shack.

She slowly closed her eyes and said, “You’re so talkative. I didn’t sleep well last night. I’m tired. I’ll nap.”

She lay down, turned to her side, cheek against the warm sand.

Wade watched her.

Why close her eyes?

He had done the same—not wanting others to see the truth in his eyes, the redness.

He smiled.

Like a little girl.

# Chapter 27

By evening, the villagers’ and children’s curiosity about the outsiders had finally worn off. They drifted away in small groups, carefully carrying basins or water bags, not once looking back.

Life was cold and indifferent. The same little black child who clung to Wade earlier now went home silently, not even speaking a word.

Wade stood up, shaking off the sand, and began filtering water.

He opened a water bag, poured some into his palm, watching the murky yellow swirl. He sniffed—it had no strange smell.

If the villagers relied on such water long-term, the harm probably wouldn’t be severe. The filtration process was simple, and purification tablets could handle it.

He weighed the water bag’s weight, dropped some purification tablets into a borrowed metal bucket, covered the opening with clean cotton cloth, and poured the water through.

Kimberly watched. The cloth caught fine sand and impurities, as clear water dripped into the bucket.

Wade smiled. “Purification tablets make things easier now. Before, in the wild, I’d layer sand filters or shave wood to use fibers to filter water—it was a hassle. I’ll boil this later; it’ll be safe to drink. But you should still drink bottled water—just to be safe.”

Kimberly asked, “How did you know I was going to shower again today?”

In the desert, some people went ten days or more without washing.

“Such hot days, sweat sticks to your skin. Without washing, it’s uncomfortable. The car windows are broken—we’ve been eating sand all night, haven’t we? Besides, tomorrow’s the negotiation. You have to look your best. In ancient times, people bathed and burned incense before big events.”

Kimberly looked at him. “Your Chinese is very good.”

“Yours too.”

She sat on the sand. “I’m different. My foster parents are university professors studying humanities. In a way, I’m their research subject—a preschooler growing up in a culture vastly different from her own. How to preserve her native culture while adapting to a foreign one?”

Wade looked surprised.

Kimberly guessed his thoughts. “Don’t overthink. They didn’t treat me like an experiment. They were good to me. You said sometimes the purpose behind actions isn’t pure.”

“I had a Chinese teacher and regular Chinese lessons. My foster parents often invited Chinese students over to talk with me. My ex-boyfriend, Ming Jiang, was also Chinese.”

“You’re different. You were smuggled to Europe as a child, living a turbulent life. Yet when you speak of home, you’re not unfamiliar.”

One water bag emptied. Wade stacked stones to build a stove, broke a wooden pole from the shack into pieces, started a fire, and placed the metal bucket on top.

The shack leaned further, no doubt unprepared for this assault beyond wind and goats.

Wade said, “Miss, there’s a street called Chinatown. I even know how to play mahjong, believe it or not?”

All kinds of people, hidden talents, diverse faces, countless hopes. You can’t say you see 5,000 years of history just by looking down a street, but you can see a lifetime of human stories.

“After being rescued from a factory by humanitarian groups, I was fostered. Not as lucky as you. I went from sewing clothes by machine to sweeping floors, cleaning windows, scrubbing toilets... I ran away in frustration.”

“I lived in Chinatown, working for food. Though it was work, it was freedom. If someone treated me badly, I’d switch places. I could even secretly smash their window—no one would know.”

“There was an old man, a teacher back home, wearing round black glasses, like an accountant. After many difficulties, he came abroad but his family couldn’t join him. He couldn’t work in his field, so he cleaned floors and dishes. I think he was lonely. Once we became close, he said, ‘Wade, I’ll teach you to read.’”

“I said, ‘Forget it, I’m busy.’”

Kimberly laughed.

Wade watched her. He wasn’t lying—he loved to see her smile, especially when her eyes held him.

“Later he said, ‘How about this: I cook dinner at home, you come eat, but you have to listen to my lessons during dinner. Deal?’”

He looked at Kimberly. “He wanted to control a meal. You understand? Who could refuse? I’d even call him Dad.”

Milk is mother. Food is father. Both more reliable than my real parents.

So at night, I went to eat. Sometimes hungry all day, just to eat with him at night, wearing down this silly old man.

He’d chatter in my ear, even brought a little blackboard and chalk, writing carefully.

At first, I didn’t listen. Then I did, as a pastime, arguing with him: “This small triangle’s interior angles add to 180 degrees, I agree. But that triangle next to it, as big as my head, must have at least 200 degrees!”

Kimberly nearly laughed to tears. “You’re an idiot.”

Wade lowered his head, smiling.

You think I don’t know triangle angles total 180 degrees? I was just teasing you, little girl.

The water in the bucket bubbled, bubbles gathering then bursting.

The water was about to boil.

Wade’s mind drifted.

He remembered once when the old man was lecturing, banging on the blackboard: “I’ve taught this problem many times, class. Who’ll answer? The fewer hands raised, the more I’ll call on that person…”

Wade had rice in his mouth, nearly spat it out laughing. “Only me! And you say class! Are you sleepwalking?”

The old man stared, looking at the cramped room like it was a big dream, then sat down, removed his glasses—Wade couldn’t recall if he was wiping his glasses or tears.

Kimberly whispered, “The water’s boiling.”

Wade came back to reality, sighed, and grabbed the bucket. “One big bucket. Enough for a wash?”

Kimberly thought, shook her head.

“Boil more.”

“One bucket’s enough—more than you used yesterday. Boiling more is wasteful.”

“Boil more.”

Fine, you win. Wade didn’t want to argue. He borrowed another bucket from the nearest household, using gestures to communicate.

---

Night fell.

Kimberly entered the tent to shower. Wade took on guard duty. The shack had no door, just a frame. Villagers weren’t used to doors—most just hung a cloth at the entrance. With only a few dozen households, everyone was related or knew each other. They were poor and didn’t guard against anyone.

Wade’s main job was chasing away goats.

The goats roamed free, coming out in the cool evening to graze, breaking into houses, then being chased out. It was routine.

In moments, neighbors shouted repeatedly. Each time Wade peeked in, he saw a goat strolling out leisurely.

He shooed two or three goats, then as darkness deepened, placed two glow sticks high for light.

Another goat came, trying to sneak inside.

Wade pressed its forehead and pushed it out.

“Have some shame! Someone’s showering!”

Before he finished, the tent flap whipped open. Kimberly emerged, wrapped in a silk shawl, towel drying her hair.

“Didn’t finish. Half a bucket left.”

Wade wore an “I knew it” expression.

There was a bed made of sticks on stone slabs, uneven.

Kimberly sat down casually. “You go wash. Don’t waste the water.”

Wade said, “I’m fine with just wiping down—”

He stopped. Kimberly’s face darkened, stern.

Really, it wasn’t about water scarcity. If there was enough, who wouldn’t want a proper wash? After a night of sand, sea salt, setting up tents, making fires—he wanted a good wash too.

He ducked inside the tent.

The glow sticks gave dim light. Two metal buckets sat inside; one barely touched.

She said one bucket was enough but insisted he boil more.

Wade took off his clothes halfway, then something stirred inside him.

He slowly sat down, looking at the bucket.

He knew he was smiling.

Really...

---

Kimberly sat on the bed, drying her hair slowly, listening to the tent’s sounds.

Are you washing or just lying down? Not drinking the water, right?

“Kimberly?”

The sound of water finally came—splashes and rustling.

“Yeah?”

“Tomorrow the pirates will arrive... What kind of people are they?”

“How can I explain?”

“Just give me a general idea. Before meeting, you should know who you’re dealing with. Are they like Caribbean pirates? Vikings? Will they raise pirate flags? Like a skull with crossed thigh bones?”

Kimberly laughed. “Nonsense... Most pirates are fishermen—very poor fishermen.”

She thought about how to explain.

Initially, Somali fishermen lived well—the country’s coastline stretched over 3,000 kilometers with abundant fish.

But in the 1990s, the government was overthrown, and the country plunged into ten years of civil war. Warlords controlled regions; lawlessness prevailed.

First, currency devalued. The Somali shilling became one of the world’s least valuable currencies. At its worst, 2,000 shillings equaled a few yuan.

Second, European and American fishing vessels arrived. With warlords fighting and no government control, foreign boats exploited the waters, overfishing and even driving out local fishermen.

Their own waters were off-limits—not because of law but because there was no government. Without fishing, livelihoods vanished.

Then—

A goat entered.

Kimberly stared at it.

It looked gentle.

She slowly pulled her legs onto the bed.

Silently, *Don’t come in. I just showered.*

The goat seemed indifferent, eventually turning its head to stare curiously at the tent flap.

Water splashed.

Women are made of water. At that moment, Kimberly felt she was made of bad water.

She cleared her throat, using her voice to paint a peaceful illusion.

*Go on, be good, come inside.*

The goat did, moving slowly, carefree—probably thinking this was just another warm, drunken evening.

Wade’s shout came from outside.

“Shameless! You scoundrel!”
