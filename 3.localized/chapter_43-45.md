# Chapter 43

Everyone on the boat was either drunk or asleep, except for him—awake and struggling to endure the night.

Kimberly was sound asleep, and Wade didn’t want to disturb her. With nothing else to do, he started unpacking his bag—after all, the negotiations were over, and they’d be disembarking soon. Sooner or later, he’d have to sort through it.

In the past, his clothes had always been rolled up messily, but today, he found himself unusually interested. Without any instruction, he folded the edges neatly, tucking corners just right, making everything square and orderly.

He silently praised his untapped potential: maybe one day he could take up housekeeping—money-making opportunities were everywhere in this world.

He rifled through his belongings: two passports, a few clothes, small travel-size toiletries, a tiny roll of drawing paper with folded edges, a small notebook, a simple cup with lipstick stains, and a tangled mix of foreign currencies...

His weapons were limited to a dagger and a Desert Eagle pistol. If danger arose again, this was hardly adequate.

Wade hesitated for a moment, then stepped outside and locked the door behind him.

He marveled at the scene: what kind of ruckus had the pirates made last night? Among the sprawled-out bodies lying in all directions, one was even dressed as a woman, wrapped in a curtain like a miniskirt, with a chest that protruded oddly. Wade bent down to take a closer look. There were two small upside-down iron bowls strapped to the chest, one on each side.

He flicked one with his finger. It rang sharply.

He was definitely luckier.

At the end of the corridor, he opened the door leading to the deck.

There was a breeze, not strong, and visibility was about two to three meters—everything was shrouded in a yellow haze.

Yesterday, Shadi had said that when a large sandstorm swept across the Red Sea, the ports would close. So now, in this vast sea, perhaps only this one boat remained.

No wonder it felt abandoned at the edge of the world.

A thin layer of sand covered the floor. After taking a couple of steps, Wade looked back and saw his footprints, clear as shoe prints.

He needed to find Tiger Shark. Tiger Shark always slept in the control room and had the satellite phone.

Sure enough, Wade found him there. Four people were lying inside—despite the spacious room, they were piled up like sacks. Tiger Shark was at the bottom, drooling down half his face, snoring loudly. On top lay a pirate boy of about ten, sprawled carelessly with a satisfied smile on his sleeping face.

Crushing the boss underneath—he was probably laughing in his dreams. But once Tiger Shark woke up, things would be different; those guys would likely get a severe beating.

Wade picked up the little pirate and set him aside. The boy was soft and light, frowning impatiently—only at moments like this did he seem like a child.

The others could get beaten—Kimberly had said she wasn’t a saint and couldn’t save everyone.

Wade pulled the satellite phone from Tiger Shark’s grasp.

Since satellite calls to normal numbers were expensive, he planned to make the call and then slip the phone back, not mentioning it to Tiger Shark. Best if he didn’t find out, but if he did, it wouldn’t matter much; Tiger Shark would probably just glare at him.

Wade could forgive Tiger Shark’s stinginess—he was in a good mood and could forgive the whole world.

He sat on the railing near the control room, pulled out the antenna from the satellite phone, and dialed.

He only remembered three numbers.

The first was Reindeer.

Reindeer answered quickly and congratulated him as soon as he recognized Wade’s voice. “Wade, the Saudis called me last night. I know the negotiations went well. Great job! Another deal, and you haven’t missed yet. Congratulations.”

It was indeed something to celebrate, but for Wade, the real reason to celebrate wasn’t this—apparently, the ancients agreed, since among the three great joys of life, they listed “wedding night,” but never “successful negotiation.”

He casually told Tiger Shark, “For the second half of the trip, Ms. Lee also hired me. I’ll be taking her back.”

Reindeer responded with a long, doubtful “Oh…”

“Why would she hire you?”

“I did a good job.”

“And her offer… was it reasonable?”

Worried Wade might overthink, Reindeer quickly explained, “I’m not taking a cut of your share. You negotiated that yourself; all yours… I’m just asking.”

Wade said, “The offer was expensive.”

She’s offering a person, so of course it’s all mine. If you want a cut… just try.

After finishing with Reindeer, Wade dialed the second number—CoCo’s.

CoCo was her usual slow self, answering after a long delay, sounding like she’d just woken up. “Hello?”

“It’s me.”

CoCo realized who it was. “Wade, you… finished the negotiations?”

“Almost. How about you?”

CoCo was close to finishing her South Sudan deal and would return to Uda in a day or two.

Wade said, “I need a favor.”

“Arrange it for me. After disembarking, I want to get new gear immediately. You know what happened to Kimberly at sea; I need to prepare. The route we took isn’t safe, so I’m not going back that way. Just leave the car in the village. If you don’t want it, toss it; if you do, have someone deal with it.”

CoCo said, “Let me check the map. Hold on.”

Wade heard pages rustling on the other end.

“Wade, I heard the pirates’ ship is anchored in the Red Sea. If they return to Somalia, they’ll head south. You should have them drop you off at the Suwe border, a small town called Komk. I have a friend there who can prepare weapons for you.”

Suwe border, small town Komk.

Wade memorized these names. He wasn’t familiar with African geography, and the place names were a tongue-twister. When encountering crucial details, he had to memorize and relay them to those who understood.

“If you don’t want to backtrack, you might consider Ethiopia, bordering Sudan. We call it Ego—it’s a plateau with a short rainy season now, soon to enter the heavy rains. Not hot; you’ll like it.”

Truly like a brother, knowing he disliked heat.

At the end of their call, CoCo brought up old topics. “You’re really not coming to Uda? Wade, think about it—you’ve never been to my home. If you come back to Africa, it might be in your next life.”

Wade laughed, paused, then said, “We’ll see. Kimberly might be in danger as soon as she lands, and Uda is so far…”

Long nights breed many worries. He was concerned something might happen.

CoCo asked in puzzlement, “Does she really not know who wants to kill her?”

“I asked. She said she doesn’t know.”

“You just believe her?”

“What do you mean?”

CoCo shrugged. “I just think anyone would have some awareness. The enemy chased her from Northern Europe to Africa, even to the sea. Such hatred can’t just be settled by shouting insults.”

“If someone’s tangled up with a dangerous figure, even if uncertain, they’d at least have a rough idea. She could share her suspicions with you, so you wouldn’t be completely in the dark…”

The third call was to Erinn, just to ask how the white peace lily was doing.

It was all because of that chef, Yongfu Lin, who kept mumbling about “plants having mysteries, protecting your journey,” and “if you’re safe, they grow well.”

At first, Wade laughed it off, not caring much. But gradually, he became anxious—he hoped for a safe journey and wanted all signs about him and Kimberly to be good.

Erinn answered, “Very well, growing beautifully. Wade, this flower really brings good luck, I tell you…”

Then the signal cut out.

Wade looked up; the wind had picked up. A new sandstorm was passing through. When heavy sand or rain falls, satellite signals get disrupted.

The screen showed it was reconnecting, but Wade thought it unnecessary.

He slipped the satellite phone back into Tiger Shark’s arms.

You’re safe, so it grows well.

Since it was “growing well” and “beautiful,” that was a good sign, wasn’t it?

---

Back at the cabin door, he remembered it was locked. After trying to turn the knob with no success, he found a wire and fiddled until he pried it open.

Pushing the door, he froze.

Kimberly was already awake, still lying on the bed, nervously looking toward him. Seeing it was him, she visibly relaxed, sighed softly, and lay back down.

Wade closed the door. “So tense?”

Kimberly said, “You spent the night with a man, then woke up to find he’d run off, leaving you on a pirate ship where someone’s trying to break in. If it were you, wouldn’t you be tense?”

Wade sat beside the bed.

“When someone’s breaking in, why are you lying there so calmly instead of grabbing a weapon to defend yourself?”

Kimberly closed her eyes lazily. “If that happened, I’d be abandoned before the bed even cooled. What’s the point of defending myself? Let fate decide.”

Wade smiled, both amused and heartbroken. “You don’t trust me that much?”

He leaned in to kiss her, but she pulled the veil over her face and said, “Get lost.”

Wade kissed her lips through the veil. “Ms. Lee, if you keep this up, I’ll complain to the Saudis—they threatened me with a gun last night, said if I didn’t comply, they’d blow my head off. I obeyed with tears, and now you tell me to get lost? Fair’s fair. Can women be irresponsible like this?”

Kimberly laughed angrily.

Wade smiled, lowered his head, gently pulled down the veil, and pressed his forehead to hers. “Does it hurt?”

She nodded, a faint crease between her brows. He wanted to kiss it away.

“Where?”

She whispered, “My back is sore; I don’t want to move. My legs burn; it hurts even when I touch them.”

Wade pulled the veil aside. The bruises and purple patches beneath her skin were more pronounced than before—the worst on her legs, back, and chest. His preference was obvious at a glance.

Wade winced. “I thought you’d like it… thought it’d feel good…”

Kimberly glanced at him, half-smiling, half-scolding. “Even if paper likes a pen writing on it, if you press too hard, the paper tears. After last night, why would you think I wouldn’t hurt? How long has it been since you touched a woman?”

Wade laughed. “Half my life before I met you—I was too excited and lost control. I’ll be careful next time.”

Kimberly was wary. “Next time? How soon? In a few seconds?”

Wade laughed awkwardly. “Whenever you say.”

She raised her chin. “I get to decide how long?”

“Whatever you want.”

“What if I say a year?”

Wade laughed. “That too.”

He said it confidently, knowing she wouldn’t really mean it.

Sure enough.

Kimberly gritted her teeth. After a pause, she warned, “Not allowed… to touch me like that today.”

Wade said, “Okay.”

He wrapped his arm around her back, holding her close but trying not to touch her bruised skin. She smiled, her cheeks flushing.

Her voice dropped to a whisper, just for him: “Actually… aside from some pain… I liked everything else.”

Wade smiled, unsure how to like her even more. After a moment, he softly asked, “Want to get off the boat today?”

She shook her head. “No, I’m too tired, sleepy. Tell Tiger Shark we’ll rest on the boat tonight and disembark tomorrow.”

Fine. The pirates were still drunk and unconscious; it was unlikely they’d set sail today.

She looked truly exhausted, so lazy she quickly closed her eyes again, murmuring, “I’m too tired to talk. You talk; I’ll listen.”

Wade hummed softly, moved gently, kissing her neck, eyelashes, earlobes, and collarbone, stroking her hair. Kimberly clearly liked it and didn’t resist, unconsciously curling into his arms.

This was nice too.

Skin on skin was intense; whispering close was long-lasting.

They’d live together now, he thought.

Her clothes would be folded or hung near his, swaying gently, touching each other. Just the thought made his heart flutter.

His bed…

A typical single bed with a hard mattress. If she stayed, maybe he’d get a bigger, softer one, add more pillows...

Maybe they should move somewhere else. He wasn’t comfortable leaving her in that apartment—it was the place where someone had been killed, and Mark, the security guard, had been stabbed because of it.

Erinn’s advice made sense: save money, marry a girl you love, buy a big house...

He could rough it alone, but with her, no. Even if she was willing, he wasn’t.

“Can I ask you a question?”

“Go ahead.”

“Why did you choose me during the interview?”

Kimberly’s body stiffened suddenly in his arms.

She slowly opened her eyes, looking a little helpless but amused. “You won’t stop until you get a clear answer, huh?”

“I just thought maybe now’s the time I can ask.”

Kimberly looked at him quietly for a moment, then said softly, “I’ll tell you later, but not now. Is that okay?”

Still not the right time?

Wade smiled.

After a pause, he said, “Then can you promise me something?”

“What?”

“Kimberly, promise me you’re not part of any plan.”

She looked into his eyes.

After a long moment, her eyes watered slightly. She whispered, “You’re silly.”

She reached up, hooked her hand around his neck. Wade bowed his head and buried it in her neck.

He heard her whisper in his ear, “All the plans I’ve made in my life don’t compare to you, this unexpected surprise. Wade, you’re so good. I never planned for this.”

# Chapter 44

By late afternoon, the pirates slowly got up, and the boat gradually came alive.

Wade went to find Tiger Shark and encountered the expected scene: the two pirates who had been sleeping on Tiger Shark were now holding their heads and dodging wildly. Tiger Shark cursed and kicked hard. His slippers weren’t secure, and one flew off with a kick. One pirate sheepishly retrieved it, but Tiger Shark grabbed the slipper and immediately smacked him.

*Slap, slap,* the sound of flesh being struck made Wade’s scalp tingle. That was worse than just being kicked.

Unexpectedly, the little pirate laughed wildly nearby. Whenever Tiger Shark smacked someone, the boy would follow up with a spit or a kick—a perfect lackey.

Wade realized his sympathy had been misplaced. Now, he just wanted to see that little brat get a beating.

Tiger Shark, the pirate leader, kept his expression under control. When he saw Wade, he immediately switched to a friendly smile and greeted him, “Hey…”

Then he faltered, as he hadn’t asked Wade’s name.

Wade patiently helped him along: “Wade.”

He explained the next steps, mentioning “Suwe border” and “Komk.” Tiger Shark nodded repeatedly.

With a regretful look, he said, “Leaving like this? I wanted to invite her to Bosaso for dinner. No, I have to tell her—she saved my life; she’s a good friend…”

Wade stepped in front of him. “Ms. Lee is resting… She has an important negotiation at Suwe border tomorrow and needs to prepare materials. I suggest you don’t disturb her.”

Tiger Shark believed him immediately.

Regret turned to envy. “Kimberly’s amazing. She said she quit the international organization to become a professional negotiator… I don’t know what I’d do if I went abroad…”

There was genuine melancholy in his tone.

Wade almost laughed. Government negotiations often dragged on for years, sometimes requiring one to two years of vetting—that meant you had to follow through on agreements for a time before the government would proceed.

Tiger Shark was already thinking about what to do abroad. Wasn’t that a bit premature?

---

While it was still light, the fishing boat weighed anchor and set sail. On his way back to the cabin, Wade met Shadi, who gave him some Arabic tea leaves—probably a favorite habit—handing him another bundle.

Not wanting to refuse, Wade popped some into his mouth.

They chatted about the terrible weather. Surprisingly, Shadi was optimistic: “Keep heading south; maybe we’ll get out of the sandstorm soon.”

Wade asked curiously, “Get out of the sandstorm?”

“Yeah, a sandstorm is like a ribbon,” Shadi gestured. “The Red Sea is narrow, with deserts on both sides. When the wind’s strong, sand lifts and drags across the sea like a snake… but the Red Sea is long; no sandstorm can cover the whole sea. If we keep going, we’ll break out of the sandstorm.”

Suddenly, Shadi complained, “Last night, drinking, I wanted to invite you. I knocked on your door, but you didn’t answer.”

Wade was startled. “You knocked?”

Shadi nodded. “Yeah.”

“Did you hear anything?”

Shadi frowned. “You were sound asleep, Wade. A bodyguard needs to stay alert… I don’t know what you were doing; I just heard *shh shh*… *shh shh shh*…”

Of course, he only heard the sandstorm.

He was in the dining hall, drunk with a group, suddenly thinking of Wade and shouting, “Drinks are better with friends. I’m going to get Wade!”

People banged pots and bowls, clearing a path. Shadi stumbled out, went the wrong way, staggered, and finally crashed into the door leading to the deck.

He pounded on the door desperately. “Wade! Come out! Drinks!”

No response. Frustrated, Shadi kicked the door, but it was bolted. Curious, he pressed his ear against it.

Outside, the sandstorm hammered the door—*shh shh, shh shh, shh shh shh.*

---

Shadi’s face grew serious. “Wade, as a bodyguard, you have to stay alert. Otherwise, it’s dangerous…”

---

That night, Wade didn’t sleep well. He knew the ship had sailed for a time, knew when it stopped, and near dawn, it sailed again, then stopped once more.

Shortly afterward, Shadi knocked on the door. “Ms. Lee, we’ve arrived. The ship can’t dock too close. You’ll take a speedboat next. When you’re ready, you can leave.”

Wade grabbed a beer bottle cap from under the bed and tapped it on the door as a sign he was alert. “Got it.”

After Shadi left, Wade looked down at Kimberly, still sleeping in his arms. “Time to get up.”

She was too sleepy to open her eyes, reluctantly burying her head in his chest. Wade smiled, kissed her ear, and his hands wandered to the places where she was ticklish and sensitive.

She giggled and dodged him, finally opening her eyes. “Get lost. You’re so naughty.”

Wade laughed. “If you break down the word ‘good’ (好), isn’t it a man and a woman together? I’ve learned it so well. How else should I learn?”

Kimberly couldn’t argue and went to shower. When she came out, she wore the same clothes as when they boarded—a white T-shirt and jeans. The bruises mostly hidden, but on her neck, collarbone, and behind her ears…

She looked at Wade with a half-smile, as if to say: what now?

Wade smiled wryly, then had a silly idea. “It doesn’t really matter if people see. Think about it—black skin hides hickeys well. Even if they see, they won’t guess what they are…”

Kimberly laughed in disbelief. “Are you stupid?”

She took out the black veil from her bag and tied it like an Arab woman’s headscarf, knotting it front and back to show only her face.

Her pale skin contrasted sharply with the black veil, making her eyes shine.

Wade pulled her close, studying her carefully. “A bit of lipstick would make your lips even prettier.”

Kimberly said, “Do you think I don’t know? I lost all my lipsticks. I had dozens of shades prepared, but then someone…”

Here we go again.

Wade smiled. “Lip color doesn’t have to come from lipstick.”

He kissed her lips with more force than before. Kimberly winced, and Wade grabbed her waist, deepening the kiss.

When he let go, he was very satisfied. The skin on her lips was thin and delicate; after a moment, it flushed pink and glistened.

Wade said, “This color suits you best. I’ll study how to control the pressure and timing. If you want it darker or lighter, just ask… Honestly, you won’t have to buy lipstick again. I can do touch-ups anytime.”

Kimberly gritted her teeth. “You…”

Wade finished for her, “Get lost? No way.”

---

On deck, no one was curious about Kimberly’s attire; most local women dressed like that, and it was normal for foreigners to imitate.

The fishing boat had already lowered the speedboat, rocking gently with the murky current. The sea was still shrouded in yellow haze but clearly outside the storm’s core, with visibility expanding.

Shadi was still at the helm, taking them to the Suwe border coast.

Tiger Shark’s reluctance was genuine. The money settled, he could now focus purely on friendship and gratitude.

“Kimberly, you saved my life. I never properly thanked you.”

“I wanted to invite you to Bosaso, but your bodyguard, Wang, said you were busy.”

What “Wang”? It’s “Wade,” okay? If you can’t pronounce “Kimberly” properly, fine, but being stupid enough to confuse the name? Time to retire.

“If I go abroad, I’ll find you. I’ll treat you well. You helped me a lot…”

Wade got into the speedboat first and reached out to help Kimberly, who took his hand but then let go and turned to say something to Tiger Shark.

Tiger Shark seemed confused; his mouth hung open. Even after the speedboat left, he stood at the railing, motionless.

The sandstorm slowed the speedboat. The wind was strong; sand sometimes hit their faces. Kimberly sat inside, pulling the veil up to cover her face.

Wade whispered, “What did you say to Tiger Shark?”

“That people should quit while they’re ahead. No matter how proud, always leave a backup.”

“Did he understand?”

“Seems not.”

“Why tell him that?”

“Remember when I mentioned the Nazi scientist Wernher Braun during negotiations?”

Wade nodded.

Kimberly said, “He’s just one example. There were actually hundreds of Nazi scientists protected and brought to the U.S. after the war.”

“When Germany lost, more than the U.S. wanted them—Stalin and Churchill sent special forces too.”

“They knew the war was ending, and postwar reconstruction would change the world. Whoever controlled the best brains would win first.”

“The U.S. got lucky to nab them first. But do you know what happened to those scientists?”

“Weren’t they granted U.S. citizenship, awarded medals, paid?”

“That was before.”

“Starting in the late ’70s, the U.S. systematically expelled hundreds of Nazi scientists—even those who contributed to research—stripping their citizenship and kicking them out in old age.”

Wade found it both tragic and ironic—Americans were experts at burning bridges behind them.

Kimberly looked back at the barely visible fishing boat in the yellow haze.

She said, “Tiger Shark did kill hostages. Whether he’ll live a good life is uncertain. Turning himself in to the government doesn’t erase everything.”

“Maybe someone will seek revenge. Maybe the government will turn on him: you’re valuable but guilty. Once your value’s gone, you’ll suffer worse than anyone.”

Wade was silent for a long time.

He suddenly felt some sympathy for Tiger Shark: when someone is arrogant and domineering, believing everything is under control, that’s often when tragedy begins.

He asked Kimberly, “What will happen to Tiger Shark?”

She smiled, paused, then nodded toward the horizon. “Instead of worrying about him, think about us.”

Following her gaze, Wade saw a reddish-yellow coastline stretching endlessly north and south beyond the dark sea waves.

Shadi slowed the speedboat’s pace. When they reached shore, the engine sputtered weakly.

Wade helped Kimberly ashore.

The coast was full of reefs, with endless reddish mud inland. Surprisingly, sparse bushes and trees grew there.

Shadi stepped onto the shore barefoot, turned the speedboat around, and reminded them, “You know this is the border, right?”

“Yeah.”

“You know Suwe’s relations aren’t good, right?”

“…”

They didn’t know. CoCo hadn’t said.

“You know Sudan and Ego don’t get along?”

“…”

“You know the three countries—Sudan, Suwe, and Ego—have all fought each other?”

Shadi’s last words: “Good luck. Goodbye.”

Wade watched the speedboat’s wake fade and smiled bitterly.

It was awkward—he got Kimberly off the boat and led her into an African version of the Three Kingdoms.

Kimberly didn’t mind. “Let’s go.”

Wade said, “Seems… a bit dangerous.”

Kimberly laughed. “Sudan’s not dangerous? They had a 20-year civil war. Somali pirates aren’t dangerous? They just hijacked the world’s largest oil tanker. You get off a pirate ship and frown about danger? Isn’t that funny?”

Wade laughed, then asked, “If I bring you into danger, will you blame me?”

Kimberly said, “Following you isn’t a joke; it’s my choice. If it gets really dangerous, I’m half responsible. Blaming only you isn’t fair.”

Wade smiled.

She was a great travel companion. How had he disliked her for sleeping so much when she first got on the boat?

He took her hand. “Let’s go.”

Kimberly let him lead, making many requests.

“When we reach the market, buy me new clothes—I have nothing left.”

“Okay.”

“A pair of shoes, please. Slippers aren’t good for walking.”

“Okay.”

“Buy me lipstick…”

Wade glanced at her.

She quickly added, “Some colors, you can’t kiss on, like wine red…”

“Maybe if you’re drunk, you can. No saying ‘get lost’.”

---

Suddenly Wade stopped.

He bent down, frowning at the many messy tire tracks in the muddy ground. He scooped up the soft, wet mud at the edge—it was fresh.

Logically, this place should be remote. How did so many vehicles come here all at once?

Kimberly wanted to ask something, but Wade gestured for silence. He put his hands on the ground and listened closely.

The next moment, he stood up quickly. “There are vehicles. Whoever they are, we need to hide first.”

He looked around and cursed silently.

Bushes, tall trees, mud—there was nowhere to hide.

Already, he could hear engines nearby. From a dirt slope, a black convertible Jeep sped down. Someone in a red vest stood in the back, rifle raised, aiming.

At the same time, distant sounds came from behind. Turning, Wade saw another off-road convertible speeding wildly, the driver in camouflage. The rear tires splashed mud high.

Kimberly smiled. “Let’s not run. We can’t outrun cars, and running would just be embarrassing.”

Wade pulled her close, quickly opened the bag, shoved the Desert Eagle into the mud, and handed Kimberly the dagger to tuck under her veil. He whispered, “Watch my eyes. When I signal, act.”

The two vehicles approached, curved around them, swung in a wide arc, then slowed to a stop.

Wade smiled and slowly raised both hands, showing he was unarmed.

Kimberly whispered, “Wade, if something bad happens, kill me first. I’ve never been humiliated.”

Wade remained calm, eyes shifting from one car to the other.

Three men, three guns.

He whispered back, “Don’t you believe I can handle three at once?”

The man in camouflage poked his head out and scrutinized Wade from head to toe. “Hey, you’re ‘Sirius,’ right?”

# Chapter 45

Fifteen minutes later, along the vast coast, only one convertible remained in sight.

Wade lay in the back seat, dialing CoCo’s number.

The call connected, and CoCo’s angry voice blurted, “Sending gear with such an entourage scared the hell out of me.”

Kimberly was leaning against the car frame, enjoying the sea breeze. She glanced at Wade, who quickly covered the mic and explained, “Exaggeration. How could I be scared?”

CoCo was self-righteous. “Do you know who I protect in South Sudan? Military and political officials! I had to be shameless to make this call for you. Otherwise, with my skills, I’d only get you a van. Who can reach the border? Think about it!”

“My client gave the order to send soldiers with the car and gear. You should be grateful instead of complaining.”

Wade laughed.

The soldiers had said the same: the higher-ups took it seriously and arrived before dawn. The coastline was long, and since they didn’t know exactly where “Sirius” would land, they drove along the coast, even racing a few times for fun.

It was touching: CoCo protected an important person without personal gain and helped Wade earn a big favor.

Wade said, “Thanks officially.”

CoCo was proud. “Of course!”

“Wade, don’t just abandon the car. They want it back. Tell me where you finally parked it; I’ll have someone drive it back. Also, consider yourself lucky to know me. Did you see the pass?”

Pass?

Wade sat up.

He rifled through the canvas bag’s gear and found several papers tucked inside. At first, he thought they were random scraps and hadn’t paid attention.

He pulled out the papers: thick paper with a national emblem, covered with stamps, text in Arabic—he couldn’t read it.

CoCo boasted, “Ordinary people can’t get these. It’s a special pass! It lets you cross the border and enter Ego. I expedited it for you last night thanks to my client’s influence. Do you know how hard it is? The approval takes weeks. Remember to show it with your passport…”

Wade’s heart sank.

After hanging up, he felt a headache and rubbed his temples as he lay back.

CoCo might have meant well but made things worse.

They avoided the old route to protect Kimberly, but this special pass increased the risk of exposure.

Once someone knew their location, tracking them became easy—two Asian foreigners stood out here.

Kimberly noticed his unease. “What’s wrong?”

Wade sat up and pulled her into his arms. “Can I ask you something… Do you really have no idea who wants to kill you?”

Kimberly said, “You’re asking me for the second time. Do you think I should know?”

Second time asking, second time answering, both the same.

Wade was silent.

The first time, in Helsinki, when she said that, it made sense—she had many enemies from editorials and had received multiple threats.

But now, CoCo’s words made sense.

—Chased from Northern Europe to Africa, such hatred can’t be settled by insults.

—Everyone should have some awareness, a rough idea, some suspicion.

Wade tried to guide her. “Think carefully. Did you offend anyone powerful who won’t let you go?”

“Yes.”

Wade was surprised.

“I offended a man who chased me relentlessly. I got together with him, and now I’m following him.”

Wade laughed helplessly, then burst out laughing and hugged her tightly, kissing her passionately.

Fine. Whatever. No matter who’s coming, he’d protect her.

Kimberly asked, “Where are we going?”

The car had been stuck in the mud for a long time, fully loaded with gear and supplies. Everything was ready except for one thing: direction.

Where to go?

Wade answered honestly, “Logically, we should choose the safest route back to Helsinki, but we both know that as long as your threat isn’t resolved, going back or staying here is equally dangerous.”

Kimberly nodded. “Then pretend there’s no danger. If you could choose, where would you want to go?”

Wade laughed.

If there were no danger, having just finished a big job, earned a lot, and gotten the woman he liked, he’d be on top of the world.

“Of course, I’d take her to see new things, travel and explore, visit CoCo’s home, and eat her out of house and home.”

Kimberly said, “Then let’s do that.”

“What?”

Before Wade could react, Kimberly comfortably leaned into his arms, pulled out the map, and studied it.

“Ego… here, northwest, is Mien National Park, Simien Mountains. Lots of animals: baboons, Ethiopian wolves, even jackals…”

“When I was helping Africa, local colleagues told me about the fun places—Kenya’s animal migration, Botswana’s wild lions… I never saw them. After Mwenda, I left in a hurry and never came back.”

She looked up at Wade. “Ego is so close. Let’s go see it. You don’t like heat and probably won’t come again. Take this chance, okay?”

Wade was silent for a moment.

She spoke seriously and naturally—not joking, not begging.

Wade felt he couldn’t refuse any of her wishes, but—

“Do you know someone wants to kill you? With that hanging over us, do you really want to think about sightseeing?”

Kimberly smiled, squinting her eyes. She draped the map over the car frame, making a small shade for the two of them.

She said, “Wade, we need to make some promises.”

“Go ahead.”

He couldn’t see her expression clearly; the map blocked the light, and her face was in shadow.

“When we arrived in Africa, one day, a senior gathered us newbies—men and women—in a room and showed us some photos too gruesome to release publicly. Men and women. You know women have it worse.”

“They said the agency would protect our safety, but nothing is absolute. When chaos breaks out, the worst, most humiliating, and degrading things could happen to us.”

“We looked at the photos one by one. Some got sick, some cried. I clenched the corners of the photos until they wrinkled.”

“The senior said: now, tell your closest colleague—if this happens and you’re helpless, what do you want them to do? Decide now, so you won’t hesitate at the moment—it’ll be too late.”

“We were silent for a long time, then made promises. I told everyone: rather than suffer endless humiliation and die without dignity, please kill me early. Compared to some photos, dying sooner is a blessing.”

Wade guessed the gist and felt sad, tightening his arms around her.

Kimberly smiled. “People hate to talk about unpleasant things, but that doesn’t mean they don’t happen. Wade, I know you heard my conversation with the doctor in the greenhouse. Some thoughts haven’t changed.”

“I don’t know who wants to kill me, but I know that no matter how strong my bodyguard is, stray bullets can kill me. Maybe one day, while laughing with you, a bullet will explode in my head.”

“Or maybe another explosion at sea will happen, with more attackers, and it’ll be even more dangerous…”

She lowered her voice. “We must agree: if it happens again, if you’re in danger too, Wade, please don’t risk your life protecting me.”

Wade was silent, then smiled.

“Impossible. I’m your bodyguard.”

“I’m with you, not as your bodyguard, but as your lover.”

“Lovers matter more than clients. If I’m your lover, shouldn’t I fight for you even more?”

Kimberly whispered, “You don’t understand. Like the photos—if you die because of me, it hurts me more than dying myself.”

Wade threw the map aside with a *whoosh.*

Kimberly closed her eyes slightly.

The cold light shone in, still dazzling.

Wade said, “Ms. Lee, if you’re this pessimistic, I’m not happy. I’m still thinking about the future. You keep saying stuff about dying and living—doesn’t that kill the mood?”

Kimberly laughed. “I know you hate hearing it… Just making a promise. It might never happen.”

“You love promises? Fine, let’s make one.”

He extended his hand, curling all fingers except the pinky for a pinky promise.

Kimberly smiled and hooked her pinky gently.

Wade said, “First, Ms. Lee, if you want to marry, while I’m alive, you can only marry me. No doctors, lawyers, or professors. When I’m dead, you’re free—pretty girls have plenty of suitors; you don’t have to mourn for me. That’s inhumane.”

Kimberly’s eyes reddened, but she smiled bravely.

“Second, if either of us dies, the other must live. Live well—eat well, dress well, sleep well. Remember each other, send flowers on anniversaries, visit graves. You can cry to release emotions, but no more than ten minutes at a time—too much is bad for health.”

Kimberly buried her head in his chest, nodding through sniffles.

“Third, from now on, no gloomy talk. Don’t let irrelevant people affect your mood. Buy clothes, shoes, lipstick. Visit mountains and parks, watch Ethiopian wolves. Touch up makeup during the day, be affectionate at night. This is especially important, okay?”

Kimberly laughed out loud.

Wade smiled, then softly said, “If you agree, seal it.”

He tightened their pinkies and pressed his thumb against her finger pad, then bowed his head and kissed the back of her hand.

It was strange. Before, he thought being intimate was the end of a relationship.

After Reindeer and Eve confirmed their relationship, he and CoCo teased, “Got her. Now that’s done, put her aside to cool off. Time to play cards, drink, and hit the nightclubs.”

Now he realized it wasn’t the end, just the beginning—how could it be the end of a matter? She would entwine with him, becoming a lifelong bond.

The car wobbled along the muddy dirt road, leaving the coast.

They even saw a road sign.

Passing a tree, he noticed a painting hanging from a branch, fluttering in the wind. The image was clear—a bar of soap.

What kind of custom was this?

Kimberly said, “Advertising. There’s nowhere to post it, so they hang it on trees.”

A lonely advertisement.

They entered Komk town just in time for the weekly market. It was small—less than fifty meters from end to end—lined with stalls selling chickens, palm oil, soap, woven shoes and hats, and clothes.

The clothing stall was a small shack. A string displayed a dozen or so colorful cheap dresses. Not much, but better than nothing. Kimberly went down to browse while Wade waited by the car, smiling.

A local woman approached, selling trinkets. She had dozens of golden-colored bracelets on her arm, with shell-shaped pendants. From afar, they looked good, but up close, the craftsmanship was crude and poor. Wade shook his head. The woman got anxious, unable to communicate, and broke open a shell to show him.

Inside was red ointment.

Wade still didn’t understand. The woman smeared some on her finger and pressed it to her lips.

It was local homemade lipstick, made from natural dyes and mixed ointments. Wade got interested and broke a few shells open. Probably due to poor technique, there was no color variation—every piece was the same.

He bought one, wrapping the chain twice around his hand.

A chicken clucked and ran wild, with a butcher chasing wielding a knife.

Inside the shack, Kimberly was holding a sea-blue dress. The vendor circled with a square mirror, showing her the front and back.

Wade picked up the satellite phone and dialed Reindeer.

“Help me check something… the murder case Kimberly was involved in.”

Reindeer was confused. “Huh?”

“If her death threat isn’t related to those editorials, who’s chasing her? I’ve thought about it, and only a murder could explain it.”

“Didn’t you mention she was once a suspect in a pufferfish toxin murder? Help me dig up that case. There might be clues.”

Reindeer hesitated, then asked, “Are you in love with Ms. Lee?”

Otherwise, why would you care so much about her?

Wade said, “Yeah.”

Reindeer sulked but admitted the truth so frankly Wade had nothing to say.

He warned, “She was a suspect once but cleared due to insufficient evidence. What if you find out she really was the killer?”

If she was the killer, it might make sense—maybe the victim’s family was haunting her for revenge.

Kimberly turned to him, showing off the dress. Wade winked to say: very pretty.

Then he replied to Reindeer, “Even if she was the killer, it doesn’t matter. It depends on whether the victim deserved to die.”
