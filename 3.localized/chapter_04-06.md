# Chapter 4

The reindeer said it was the hand of an adult white man, dried by the wind, with old bite scars on the webbing between the thumb and index finger. The hand held a folded card.

The card was plain white and exquisite, with delicate lace-like cutouts along the edges. The cover bore a gold-embossed blessing and carried a faint fragrance, just like a high-end greeting card sold in any boutique store.

When the package was opened, the strange hand was twisted into a fixed pose, as if offering the card—like an invitation.

Opening the card, there was a line of writing inside:

—The next one to die will be you.

The reindeer murmured, “If it were me, to hide the handwriting, I’d cut out matching letters from a newspaper and paste them together to form a sentence.”

But the sender didn’t seem to want to hide anything: the line was handwritten, the strokes smooth and flowing.

Wade asked, “Did you report it to the police?”

“Yes. Optimistically, maybe it will take ten years to solve this case.”

A single hand, dried and easy to carry, convenient to pass around—it could have come from anywhere a white man lived. Countless nameless corpses have no identity to match, let alone just a hand.

“How did Miss Lee react?”

“Not at all.”

Wade thought he misheard.

The reindeer added, “Really no reaction. The police report was filed by an hourly temp worker for her. She said she’d received rotten cat corpses, wax heads soaked in blood plasma, clumps of hair soaked in unknown solutions. Compared to those, a dried hand is restrained—at least it doesn’t smell disgusting.”

Wade was speechless for a long time.

There must be a reason for someone to hate her so intensely, right?

The reindeer guessed, “It probably has to do with her profession.”

“Her profession?”

“Helping Africa—that kind of work offends people?”

The reindeer shook his head. “That was a long time ago. You know, many people who withdrew from war zones suffer severe psychological trauma. After leaving Carlong, Miss Lee completely quit aid organizations. Now she’s more of a…”

He frowned, trying to find the right words: “A writer… a columnist, yes, a freelance social commentator.”

“Sharp-tongued?”

Wade had an idea.

Sharp was too mild a word here. The reindeer chuckled dryly, “Her articles are like ice picks, stabbing you with a dozen bloody holes, blood spurting everywhere.”

“Who has she insulted?”

“Italian Mafia, Colombian drug lords, luxury companies doing cruel animal testing, government officials, corrupt police officers, religious extremists… basically anyone you can think of, she’s offended them.”

Got it. Whatever threats she receives are normal.

“She’s somewhat famous, right?”

“How do you know?” The reindeer was surprised. “She has a column; she’s quite well known in the industry.”

Wade smiled. “With fame, people think twice before messing with her. Without it… she’d be dead already.”

His feelings about Kimberly shifted.

Courage is commendable, but Wade didn’t admire reckless defiance—he believed in strength and strategy. Unless she had an entire platoon of mercenaries protecting her, shooting arrows blindly at the world’s underbelly only put herself in danger. Social commentators should value their lives; after all, survival comes first.

The reindeer checked his watch, a children’s plastic one with Mickey Mouse hands.

“Shall we go now? It’s almost time for the meeting.”

The details were vague. The industry middleman who arranged the meeting said it was a top-tier job, confidential, and money was no object.

Wade thought he could take it.

Just a job.

—

On the main road, people and cars finally appeared occasionally. Sometimes oncoming headlights would flash, making the whole world suddenly bright.

The reindeer chatted idly.

“Out of money again?”

“Yeah.”

“You wouldn’t take jobs if you weren’t broke!”

His tone was resentful and reproachful. “Look at CoCo—buying houses and cars, investing in forex, dressing flashier than clients.”

Wade had heard about that. CoCo took several jobs, wore designer brands head to toe, making the quiet big shots look like followers. A client once complained, and CoCo replied slowly, “Personal taste. What’s it to you?”

But why should Wade follow CoCo? Everyone has their own path; different mountains have different shapes. Besides, they’re different trees.

Wade changed the subject. “Who do you think would threaten Miss Lee?”

Professional curiosity made him want to narrow the suspects.

The reindeer had read Kimberly’s recent columns and guessed, “She wrote four articles in two months opposing female genital mutilation in parts of Africa.”

A nearby car honked, the sound clashing with the reindeer’s voice, fragments reaching Wade’s ears.

—She wrote… four articles… opposing… Africa… mutilation…

Wade knew little about mutilation. “Isn’t that men’s circumcision? Does she oppose that too?”

The reindeer emphasized, “Female mutilation.”

“What do women have to cut?” Wade was lost.

The reindeer paused a few seconds, then explained, “Usually done between ages 4 and 10. They use a blade to cut off external genitalia, then sew the wound to ensure virginity before marriage. Women who undergo it feel no pleasure during sex; the wound tears painfully. But it’s said to guarantee fidelity to their husbands.”

His gaze drifted, landing on Wade’s exposed arm at his cuff, seeing hairs standing on end.

He felt a bit relieved—exactly the same reaction he’d had reading about it two days ago.

Wade felt a heavy weight on his chest and wanted to crush something. “What kind of sick people come up with this?”

The reindeer warned, “Watch your language! Quiet. Those conservative forces who defend mutilation see it as their precious tradition. They view outside interference as colonialism or cultural invasion. If they hear you, they’ll knock your teeth out!”

Wade sneered, pointing at Kimberly’s photo. “She’s a woman brave enough to put her thoughts in the newspaper for the world. How gutless do I have to be, sitting in your car with the windows up, having to ‘keep quiet’?”

The reindeer shrugged. “Just a friendly reminder… Did you know over 100 million women worldwide have undergone mutilation, and the number grows by over a million each year?”

Wade was baffled. “Doesn’t anyone do anything about it?”

“There are. Miss Lee wrote articles opposing it. WHO, women’s groups, and the UN cooperate with African countries to abolish the practice. Most countries have passed laws banning it. But some conservative areas are hard to change quickly. So there are rescue organizations helping girls escape.”

Wade felt somewhat hopeful. “Keep an eye out, will you? I want to donate part of my pay this time—to cover girls’ travel, tuition, and resettlement.”

The reindeer’s eyes widened. “Why?”

No reason. It just hurt. He’d been kicked in the groin and suffered terribly—imagine the pain of having that done forcibly. Besides, most girls are so cute, like Erinn…

Suddenly he remembered how Erinn reluctantly let him run a tab. No praise for her.

“Don’t you keep some for yourself?”

“I already saved most for food and fun. I’ll earn more when I run out.”

The reindeer gasped. Newspapers say Chinese save money as disaster protection. How does Wade do the opposite?

“What if you get seriously ill one day?”

“Better if I get well. If not, then I’m done.”

“Then you won’t even afford a coffin!”

“Why bother with a coffin? It’d just hinder my return to nature.”

The reindeer didn’t want to argue anymore.

Luckily, Wade returned to the topic. “You think the threats come from fanatical defenders of mutilation?”

“That’s my guess. Her recent articles all cover that topic. Probably offended some people.”

Wade understood the guess but disagreed.

“Why?”

“That hand was sent with care, meaning the sender is meticulous. They wouldn’t let you find a connection so easily.”

—

Kimberly lived in a private villa district on the outskirts of Helsinki. The houses showed strong Aalto influences—thick brick walls, solid shapes, understated but distinctive.

Driving up the driveway, the reindeer pointed out that most homes were quiet; the villas appeared as dark blocks hidden among trees. Only one house was brightly lit, its glow casting a soft, hazy halo in the night.

Several cars were parked outside. Through the frosty lawn, shadows of people clustered behind floor-to-ceiling windows—some sitting, some standing—like a party not yet over.

Wade was surprised. So many people?

The door was half open, as if waiting for them. When pushed, four or five men inside all turned to look.

Wade looked back.

They were in their 20s or 30s—some huge, muscles stretching their suits; others small but tough, eyes fierce.

Fellow professionals recognized fellow pros: they were bodyguards.

Wade stood at the door, not intending to enter, and asked the reindeer, “What’s going on?”

In this line of work, top-level jobs are solo or with old partners—not with strangers.

The reindeer was puzzled too. “Hold on.”

He ran inside, whispered to a short man nearby, then hurried back, sweat beading on his forehead, his dark skin shining.

He stammered, “They said… it’s… an interview.”

Wade laughed. “An interview?”

That was a bit… humiliating.

He was a top gun, not a rookie. He wasn’t short of clients; taking jobs was a privilege—people always begged him to take their money. Who gets picked like produce at the market?

The reindeer cursed the middleman in his heart: he had eagerly researched Kimberly’s info and pushed Wade to take the job, only to find out there’d be an interview. No need to come!

It’s like luxury brands—brand matters more than price. Better to hold out than discount and lose face.

He quickly clarified, “Wade, I didn’t know about the interview. If I had, I’d have brought others. We have principles; I’ll make it clear to them…”

The door to a side lounge opened.

A young man with a high nose and deep-set eyes stepped out, wearing a loose white robe that reached his feet, and a red-and-white checked headscarf secured with a black wool band.

A white robe?

That outfit sparks endless imagination.

The reindeer instinctively grabbed Wade’s hand, excited and stammering, “Wade! See? White robe! Saudi! Could be from Dubai or Abu Dhabi! All rich people!”

Wade’s gaze deepened.

Strange to see white robes here.

# Chapter 5

It turned out that rigid principles can soften in the face of profit.

Wade sat on a sofa by the window in the hall, watching the reindeer whispering eagerly with the white-robed man at the lounge door. Their cooperation didn’t look like “making things clear,” more like partners in crime.

After a while, the reindeer came over excitedly.

“Wade, I respect your choice—you can refuse the job. But can you hear me out first?”

“Go ahead.”

“They are really Saudis. We’ve never done business with Middle Eastern billionaires before. This is a golden opportunity! Imagine if we cooperate this time!”

Wade casually imagined a golden road to a mountain of wealth.

But why would Saudi people hire bodyguards for Kimberly?

“Also, they explained the interview is because this isn’t a ‘defense’ job but an expedition.”

In the industry, “defense” means local protection within Helsinki. “Expedition” means a long journey, and naturally, pay is multiplied.

That made the interview reasonable: traveling together requires getting along, almost as important as the bodyguard’s skills.

But as he listened further, Wade’s expression darkened. The process had three steps: briefing, competition, and client interview.

Damn, competition too? Wade thought that was just monkey business.

The reindeer wanted Wade to take the job badly. This was a trembling golden bridge—if it connected, who knows? Maybe the next job would come from the Saudi king!

But Wade’s personality didn’t allow rushing him.

He casually added, “Anyone who participates, even if they quit halfway, will get 500 euros after signing a confidentiality agreement.”

Meaning: you’re here anyway, might as well take something. The money isn’t hot.

—

Wade entered the small lounge.

The confidentiality agreement seemed meant for those rejected, promising not to disclose details.

After signing, the white-robed man collected the document and handed over a rolled-up newspaper.

Facing Wade was a bold black headline with an exclamation mark:

**Ransom!**

Familiar. Wade’s heart stirred as he took the slowly unfurling paper.

The headline read “Vast Ransom.”

The news photo showed a vast blue sea, with EU escort ships patrolling.

A quick scan caught key words: Sirius, pirates, Gulf of Aden.

Wade’s heart skipped.

He pushed the paper aside. “You’re the Saudi shipowner.”

The white-robed man was surprised by Wade’s quick insight, then nodded. “The Sirius is a supertanker, over 300,000 tons displacement—about the size of three aircraft carriers. It launched just six months ago, with 25 crew onboard. The ship and its crude oil cargo are worth over 200 million dollars.”

Wade smiled. “Pirates demand 20 million. Twenty million for two hundred million. Not a bad deal.”

The white-robed man smiled too. “We can’t pay such a high ransom—it would embolden pirates, causing endless future troubles. We’re trying various channels to negotiate and lower the ransom.”

He showed Wade a photo.

Blurry, but a middle-aged black man holding a rocket launcher was visible. His head tilted oddly to the left, as if fused with his shoulder.

“This is one of Somalia’s fiercest pirates and the mastermind behind the Sirius hijacking—Tilted Tiger Shark. He has a history of killing hostages. Two years ago, he hijacked a Danish cargo ship. When negotiations stalled, he pulled out the chief officer and shot him six times in front of the negotiators.”

Wade was calm. “Then you must be very cautious in negotiations.”

The white-robed man put the photo away. “Over six years ago, Somalia was torn by warlords. Refugees were countless. The UN deployed food aid convoys. At the distribution site, two warlord factions fought over supplies, shooting refugees. At that time, Tiger Shark was a civilian, his neck riddled with bullet holes.”

Lucky—there’s a major artery in the neck.

“At the time, Miss Lee was stationed in Somalia, assisting the UN with food distribution. She was one of the officials on site. Out of humanitarian spirit, she helped medical staff save Tiger Shark from death’s door…”

Understood.

The Saudi shipowner was looking for someone to negotiate with Tiger Shark. Who better than Kimberly?

“So this trip is to…”

“Somalia.”

Wade was silent for a while.

How did CoCo describe Somalia?

—The only real stateless country in the world.

—Almost every household owns an AK. You can live without a phone or TV here, but never without a gun.

—Wade, guns are openly sold at the market! Next to the fruit stalls are gun sellers. You can test-fire a watermelon. Great!

—

The villa’s gym had been temporarily converted into an arena for competition: three events—10-meter multi-target rapid pistol shooting, hand-to-hand combat, and knife fighting.

Before the competition, there was a half-hour coffee break.

The reindeer urged Wade, “Somalia isn’t so bad.”

Wade sipped coffee. “It’s hot.”

His nickname was Fir—not without reason. Wade loved all things cold. In most places on Earth, firs grow only in winter.

“But CoCo has been in Sudan lately. Wade, you two could meet nearby! How long has it been?”

Unlike Wade, CoCo hated the cold and spent most time in the tropics.

His nickname came from the real cocoa tree, which demands warm temperatures; below 15°C, it risks death.

Wade set down his coffee. “Maybe later. I need to pee. Where’s the bathroom?”

The reindeer didn’t know either, but a big guy nearby kindly pointed, “Go out that door, then left or right, walk to the end, then left, or maybe right.”

A simple and clear answer. Wade stared at him a moment. “Thanks.”

He quickly took a wrong turn but didn’t go back.

In the villa’s backyard stood a large glass greenhouse, shaped like a geometric cell division, double-glazed with steel supports, at least five meters high.

Under Helsinki’s cold, dim sky, the glass shielded a lush subtropical forest.

Approaching, the automatic door opened silently.

The greenhouse had temperature and wind control systems. The designer was a master, using trees, potted plants, and ground streams to carve the space into a self-contained ecosystem—seasons, paradise.

Undoubtedly a marvel of modern technology and wealth. Was the social commentator’s pay really this generous? The villa, gym, and costly greenhouse suggested Miss Lee was quite wealthy.

A nearly angry voice rang out: “Miss Lee!”

The greenhouse was quiet; the voice was abrupt, like rain mist disturbing a patch of leaves.

Wade turned toward a dense green screen.

Someone was there—two opposing auras. The speaker wasn’t dominating.

“I believe we’ve agreed on your pay, and you accepted.”

Curious, Wade stepped forward, pushing aside thick entangled vines.

A long branch framed a picture.

Another white-robed man, about forty, angry, pacing like a trapped beast.

Beside him was probably Kimberly.

She faced away, sitting on a high stool, wearing a black sleeveless, low-back evening gown. Her hair was loosely but carefully pinned, with two or three strands lazily curling against her pale neck—a fragile, worrying balance that seemed ready to break with a heavier breath.

The gown’s hem brushed the floor.

In front of her stood an easel with white paper; she held a pencil, sketching lines.

Her voice was calm, casual: “A verbal agreement isn’t a written contract. I’ve changed my mind—it’s not illegal.”

The white-robed man tried to stay calm: “Miss Lee, last-minute price hikes are against the rules.”

“As long as it’s legal.”

Deliberate calm; a butterfly’s wingbeat, and the white-robed man tensed instantly.

The difference was clear.

But sudden price hikes? Wade disapproved—rules and law mattered in their line of work.

“Why? The deal was going smoothly. There must be a reason for the sudden hike.”

“I received death threats. Going out under these circumstances, isn’t the hike reasonable?”

“Miss Lee, to my knowledge, your threats have nothing to do with us. In fact, we spared no expense to hire the best bodyguards for your safety…”

“Bodyguards?”

She tossed down her pencil, picked up another.

“Bodyguards are useless.”

“Even with ten bodyguards, a stray bullet could kill me. You can pay me more, so why waste on useless guards?”

A slap in the face, unexpectedly.

Every profession has dignity—even begging has rules. Kimberly’s words spat in his face.

Wade cursed silently.

Forget the 500 euros, Somalia, pirates, Saudis, the job—fuck it all.

Suddenly noticing her pencil box.

Earlier, she set up an easel and paper. Wade thought she was a typical painter, about to do oils or watercolors. Her box should have colorful palettes, brushes, palette knives, water jars, sandpaper, oil cans.

But no. Her box was custom, divided into wooden compartments, each labeled by pencil hardness and shade, with HB as the boundary—from hardest 9H to softest 9B.

The compartments were filled with pencils, long and short, scattered without order—like a bulk sale or a pencil graveyard.

She only used pencils of varying hardness and shade?

On the paper, a human figure’s portrait was emerging.

The white-robed man’s irritation turned to threat: “If this is the case, cooperation will be difficult.”

Kimberly held the pencil at an angle; the tip scratched the paper: “Whatever.”

“But a friendly reminder: I heard Tiger Shark is excited that I’m negotiating. He promised to keep hostages safe until I arrive. If he knew you replaced me, he’d feel mocked. He has a volatile temper.”

Tiny graphite dust fell on the paper. She flicked it off, and the paper shivered, dust scattering.

Wade felt sorry for the white-robed man. There was no one else like Kimberly—he had to endure her threats.

The white-robed man seemed aware but unwilling to concede immediately. Kimberly was calm, focused on her drawing.

Wade looked at the drawing.

The portrait gradually became clear: a black woman wearing a headscarf, smiling blankly. Deep eye sockets, the entire eye sunken in shadow. The smile muscles were pronounced; eyebrows and lip wrinkles were messy. The sternocleidomastoid muscles looked like twisted old tree roots.

Kimberly carefully sketched, occasionally switching pencils.

The varying shades of black created light and shadow, gray areas, highlights, and darkness. The eye corners had knife-like lines; lip wrinkles connected folds. The darker the shading in the eyes, the more the pupils’ chilling brightness stood out.

Wade stared into the woman’s eyes.

This wasn’t a drawing—it was a living woman looking back, her gaze locked with fear, despair, and a flicker of hope.

The white-robed man bit his lip repeatedly, finally deciding: “Fine, as you say. I hope there are no more changes.”

Kimberly said, “Also…”

She signed the paper: “I don’t accept half payments. All money must be transferred at once. I won’t leave until I see the money.”

…

I thought those war volunteers were selfless and altruistic. Turns out they can be greedy too.

Wade turned and left the greenhouse.

Poor white-robed man—he must be going crazy.

# Chapter 6

Back at the arena, the first round of rapid shooting was nearly over. The reindeer hurriedly handed Wade a Glock L, dragging him to the firing line. “Hurry, it’s your turn.”

Wade habitually checked the weight, ejected a round, inspected the gun, and let the reindeer help with goggles and earplugs.

Suddenly, he blurted, “I saw Miss Lee.”

The reindeer was caught off guard. “How… is she?”

Wade smiled but didn’t answer. He took his stance—stand firm, suspend the arm, straighten the wrist. Ten meters ahead were five circular targets in a row.

Rapid fire—almost a continuous burst. The gunshots echoed in the air. The round ended.

When scoring, the reindeer couldn’t help but let out a short scream.

Wade scored a 2.

What the hell! Even rookies wouldn’t score a 2!

“How is she?” The reindeer didn’t need an answer anymore.

Since seeing the white-robed man, he had been dreaming: take the job with Kimberly, connect with the Saudi royal family, get a generous Saudi sheikh to give him an oil well, become an oil tycoon, buy a private jet…

All vanished with Wade’s gunshots—doomed, at the end of the road, ashes to ashes.

The reindeer lost interest in the hand-to-hand and knife fights, sitting cross-legged in a corner, holding his head, trying to psych himself up:

—No, don’t blame Wade. It’s his right to refuse jobs.

—Maybe this isn’t the best time to cooperate with Middle Eastern billionaires.

—The Middle East is just a passing storm; cooperation with Wade is long-term…

When the competition ended, the reindeer was finally calm. Wade came out with two “cuts” on his face—of course, the knives were special props, blunt, leaving only red paint.

Worried, the reindeer said, “Is she really that bad?”

Wade said, “She called bodyguards useless. What do you think?”

Well…

Insulting professional dignity is unacceptable and a reason to walk away. But if she had money, shouldn’t she face the challenge and change her mind?

He only dared think, not say—sometimes, he was genuinely afraid of Wade.

The reindeer pretended not to care. “It’s like this now; no point continuing. Shall we leave? I’ll get the car.”

He rummaged for the keys, calculating how to claim the 500 euros.

Wade said, “Wait.”

The reindeer looked up.

“The last round is the client interview, right? So Miss Lee will be there?”

The reindeer nodded. Kimberly had a decisive vote.

“Then let’s do the interview.”

“Why?”

Wade thought a moment. “She draws… quite well.”

—

Wade meant nothing else—just wanted to see the real person after photos and voices.

The meeting was upstairs, in the living room. The white-robed man from the greenhouse was the interviewer—smiling, dignified, courteous.

Kimberly was there, unchanged from photos, but with a watery, impenetrable gloom. She held a thin ladies’ cigarette between her fingers, hardly smoking, seemingly just using the scent to refresh.

She and the white-robed man exchanged polite glances. The greenhouse scene seemed never to have happened—no threats, no anger.

Wade found it amusing. He suddenly missed the reindeer with lipstick in the Lapland hallucination—at least it was natural, unpretentious, and had a heart for beauty.

As he sat down, he noticed a faint gleam at Kimberly’s neck.

A thin platinum chain with a small garnet pendant nestled just below her collarbone, like a tiny vermilion mole.

Wade thought Kimberly’s fashion sense needed improvement.

A black evening gown like that would pair better with a round, full pearl necklace or a metal pendant with design. Clothes and accessories should complement and hold their own ground.

The white-robed man’s questions were sharp.

“Mr. Wade, your pistol rapid fire scored 10, 8, and 2 rings. You ranked first in hand-to-hand but last in knife fighting, taking two cuts… Can you explain?”

Wade frowned. “That’s hard to explain. Sometimes, I really… don’t perform steadily.”

“Don’t you think unstable performance is terrifying for a bodyguard? One mistake could cost the client’s life.”

Wade agreed. “I will try to improve.”

“Try?” Who gives you a ‘try’? Without manners, the white-robed man wanted to slam the table and leave.

Nearby, Kimberly, bored, flicked the cigarette ash upward.

The white-robed man kept a calm tone but asked everything.

“If we cooperate, Mr. Wade, do you have any requirements? Any special rules… needing our cooperation? I heard top bodyguards have personal demands.”

“I don’t like protecting scum.”

The white-robed man didn’t understand. “What?”

“If Miss Lee’s behavior is seriously flawed or if she’s done something unspeakable, I suggest not hiring me—I’d quit halfway.”

The white-robed man’s eyes widened, mouth half open. The room was quiet. Nearby, a specially designed clock stood—no hands, only a metal ring, like a Stargate.

Kimberly’s smoking hand drooped. The side of her palm had graphite smudges from drawing. She paused; the cigarette ember neared her fingers. Just as Wade thought she’d burn herself, she flicked the cigarette, sliding her hand back.

Ash fell like dust.

The white-robed man realized, “Mr. Wade, a bodyguard is a business. The employer’s character isn’t your concern. Once paid, you must fulfill your duty. Walking away is irresponsible.”

Wade smiled.

“I agree. That’s why I always notify ahead.”

…

The interview ended quickly as expected. The white-robed man was polite. “We’ll consider carefully and look forward to cooperating.”

But his eyes said: Go to hell.

—

The reindeer waited downstairs, tactfully silent about the interview, looking happy.

“I’ll get the car. If there’s time, we can go to Erinn’s bar for a drink… Oh, the pay is in the lounge. See you later.”

He opened the door, keys twirling lightly on his finger.

Wade felt a touch of guilt but it faded quickly: he and the reindeer, the reindeer and the Saudis—business at its core.

He went to the lounge, took 500 euros in cash from the young white-robed man, and kindly advised, “We usually don’t use such large bills. Restaurants and supermarkets reject them.”

The white-robed man was puzzled; 500 euros was only about 2,000 dirhams. Not a large denomination to him.

Wade didn’t explain further, folded the bill, and pocketed it, closing the lounge door behind him.

Through the glass, he saw the reindeer’s battered Volkswagen already waiting on the driveway, ready to go. The night had been productive—at least he could pay back Erinn’s tab…

Someone called him, “Mr. Wade.”

He stopped.

Not because it was Kimberly, but because he hadn’t heard pure Chinese in a long time.

Her voice had the softness of Jiangnan water and the toughness of Jiangbei iron—something the reindeer’s parroting couldn’t match. Wade wanted to hear more.

He turned.

Kimberly stood a short distance away, like a high-contrast black-and-white photo—black hair, eyebrows, eyes, and gown; white shoulders and arms.

Everything else didn’t disturb the picture, except the red lips and garnet pendant—like someone dipped a finger in vermilion and painted color on the photo.

Wade asked, “Yes?”

“Mr. Wade, you speak very directly, leaving a strong impression.”

So?

“I hope it’s not too abrupt, but I want to ask—what impression do you have of me?”

Impression?

Hard to say. Everything tonight was about her—good and bad, front stage and backstage, what should and shouldn’t be heard.

Wade didn’t want to complicate matters. He gave a polite answer, “Miss Lee is excellent… her aid work in Africa is admirable, very brave… I look forward to working with you…”

Kimberly interrupted, “Mr. Wade, I want the truth—you can say what you really think. No one will blame you.”

Wade didn’t understand her intent.

But whatever. Since she didn’t mind, he told the truth: “My impression… isn’t very good.”

Kimberly smiled. “I thought so.”

She nodded politely, then turned and left.

He thought she was arrogant and prickly, always sharp-tongued. Now, so courteous—strangely confusing.

As he got in the car, the reindeer complained, “So slow!”

Wade unfolded the big bill, flicked it twice in front of the reindeer’s eyes. If money could shine, it would be dazzling now.

The reindeer stopped complaining. The roadside trees loomed as he began to talk to himself.

“Actually, many clients book you. If you want to take jobs, there are plenty. But I think you should wait and pick. Wade, is it really over with the Saudis?”

Hope was not dead yet; he still clung to the trembling golden bridge.

“Anyone serious about work and responsibility wouldn’t choose me.”

The reindeer sighed sadly.

“But maybe not.”

What?

A sudden twist. The car swerved, pulling over.

Irreversible things—how could it suddenly be “maybe not”?

The reindeer’s fading hope stretched like melting sugar, eyes flickering with renewed light.

Wade told him about Kimberly’s request.

The reindeer was relieved and excited: “Why? I was downstairs all the time. I promise, the others took the money and left. Miss Lee never came down to see you off. Wade, maybe she likes you? I knew it! When I saw her photo, I felt you two would get along!”

Wade smiled. “If she were seventeen, I’d almost believe that.”

Kimberly had the background, eyes hardened by blood and death, calmly bargaining with the white-robed man, speaking politely, writing articles sharp as knives, recently receiving a dried human hand.

She wasn’t the type to play a fairy tale of love at first sight.
