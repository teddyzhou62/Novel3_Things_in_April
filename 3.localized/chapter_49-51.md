Kimberly kept her eyes down, lashes fluttering, and after a moment, she forced out, “Does it hurt?”

Wade laughed heartily. “You think I’m you? With your teeth and bite strength, it’s more like you’re just tickling me.”

She got up and looked at him. The teeth marks on his shoulder had nearly faded, but there were red marks on his back, some broken skin oozing tiny beads of blood. She didn’t know she could lose herself so completely—maybe it didn’t matter if it was man or woman, when passion reached its peak, there was always a bit of a destructive impulse.

She rested her chin on his bare shoulder, wrapping her arms around him from behind, quietly feeling his warmth. His back was broad and strong, with a deep spine groove in the middle and firm muscles on both sides. Just a gentle embrace already made her feel safe.

Kimberly whispered, “Why do you like me?”

Wade smiled. “There’s no clear way to explain that.”

It was like accepting that the sum of the angles inside a triangle is always 180 degrees without questioning why.

He wasn’t the first man to protect the most beautiful woman he knew. Among his clients were supermodels and glamorous stars. He’d look at them with a man’s appreciative eyes, joke lightly with colleagues, then go back to being a cold, expressionless wall.

The person who catches your eye and the one who touches your heart are often different. You can easily say who dazzles your eyes, but you can’t always say who unlocks your heart—you have to open that door yourself to see who’s outside.

Kimberly said, “I can’t explain either. If I’d known it would be like this…”

If she had known, would she have chosen him that day at the interview?

A voice deep inside said: Absolutely not.

But if she hadn’t chosen him, she’d have missed this forever.

She was momentarily lost in thought until Wade prompted, “Don’t leave the sentence hanging—if you’d known, then what?”

Kimberly smiled and changed the subject. “Look at that.”

Following her gaze, Wade saw the burnt-down candle, melted flat and delicate, with a few thick drops hanging from the edges.

The chaos of the world was like a consuming fire. People were like wax blocks, slowly melting from birth to death. Even without love or with the wrong company, they melted away the same—life as the beginning, death the end, no difference.

But if you were lucky enough to meet the right person, he was like a candlewick. When the fire came, he helped you burn bright with light, warmth, and heat, staying with you until the very last moment.

Wade asked, “What do you want me to see?”

Kimberly scolded, “I want you to see that when the candle burns out, it’s time to ask the boss for a new one.”

Wade left but didn’t head straight to the shack. He lingered in the nearby street like an ordinary tourist, fiddling with black wood carvings and inspecting sheepskin paintings.

He was going to make them lose sight of him.

The mountains were the best cover—rocks, water, trees. With his training, no one could tail him here.

After running a fair distance, he stopped, leaned against a tree, and waited a moment. Then he climbed up and, hidden by dense leaves, took out a monocular telescope to scan the area.

In view was only a lone Walia ibex, its long curved horns like the pheasant feathers on an opera headdress.

Wade recalled his route and changed direction, aiming to reach the back of the shack.

Before reaching it, he spotted the white van Jeannie mentioned. The occupants seemed to have tried to hide it with branches. Wade circled the van, smashed a window, and peered inside. Good—there were ropes and other gear he could use.

He drew his knife and slashed three tires, leaving one intact—not wanting to be too ruthless.

He moved forward and stopped a few dozen meters behind the shack. Hiding behind a tree, he used the telescope to watch the red-roofed house.

The windows were open, and people occasionally moved inside. Wade fixed his gaze on one window: he couldn’t see faces clearly but based on build, height, and clothing, he confirmed three men inside.

He pondered.

Shooting wasn’t ideal—at most, he could take out one at a time. It would spook the rest and make things worse.

Taking all three at once was possible but dangerous. He didn’t want to risk it—he still had to pick up Kimberly later.

The best plan was to lure them out one by one, isolate them, defeat each quietly, and tie them up for negotiation.

How to lure them?

Opportunity came easily. One man stepped out to pee, circling behind the house. He glanced at the window but, feeling exposed, moved further to a large rock.

Wade silently thanked him.

Out of humanity, Wade waited until the man finished before pouncing like a leopard. With his knuckle duster, he struck the man’s lower back hard. The man’s face twisted in pain, and before he could shout, Wade pressed his head into the mud, knee pinning him down, making it hard to breathe.

The operation went surprisingly smoothly. Wade frowned.

Damn it, could they respect the ace? The first guy sent wasn’t professional, and this was the second time. Couldn’t they send someone stronger?

He’d been hit once and dared not leave cover.
